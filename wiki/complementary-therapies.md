# Complementary and Alternative Therapies

*Looking for a simpler explanation? See [Plain English version](plain-living-well.md).*

Complementary and alternative medicine (CAM) approaches cannot replace the established medical treatments for MG — pyridostigmine, immunosuppression, biological therapies, and thymectomy. What some approaches may offer is support alongside conventional treatment: reduced inflammation, improved stress regulation, and better quality of life.

**Always discuss any CAM therapy with your neurologist before starting.** Some herbs carry serious risks in MG, and interactions with MG medications can be clinically significant.

---

## Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM)

### How TCM Understands MG

In Traditional Chinese Medicine, MG is classified within **Wei Syndrome** (痿证, *Wèi Zhèng*) — "wilting" or "flaccidity syndrome" — a concept documented as far back as 300 BCE. TCM holds that the Spleen governs the muscles and limbs; when Spleen Qi (vital energy) is deficient, nourishment cannot reach the neuromuscular system. The characteristic fatigue-with-activity pattern of MG maps closely onto this framework.

TCM practitioners do not treat all MG patients the same way. They differentiate treatment based on individual "patterns":

- **Spleen and Stomach Qi Deficiency** — generalised weakness worsening with activity, fatigue, poor appetite (most common in classic generalised MG)
- **Spleen and Kidney Yang Deficiency** — severe or "brittle" MG with cold intolerance, frequent relapse
- **Liver and Kidney Yin Deficiency** — primarily ocular symptoms, dryness, insomnia
- **Qi and Blood Stasis** — complex chronic presentations

This individualised approach means treatment formulas differ between patients.

### Herbal Formulas With Evidence in MG

#### Compound Huangqi (Astragalus) Preparations

The single most clinically studied herbal approach in MG. Proprietary preparations based on **Astragalus membranaceus (Huang Qi)** — including Qiangji Jianli Capsules — have been the subject of formal clinical investigation in China.

A **2022 meta-analysis** (*Frontiers in Pharmacology*) analysed 19 randomised controlled trials involving 1,283 patients. Results showed:
- Significantly better clinical response rates when combined with conventional treatment versus conventional treatment alone
- Significant reduction in QMG (Quantitative Myasthenia Gravis) and MG-ADL symptom scores
- A trend toward reduced corticosteroid requirements

**How Astragalus works in MG — modern mechanisms:**
- **Gut microbiome → Treg pathway:** Astragaloside IV (the active compound) acts as a prebiotic, feeding bacteria that produce short-chain fatty acids. These stimulate the production of regulatory T-cells (Tregs) — the very cells whose failure drives MG
- **BATF inhibition:** Calycosin (a flavonoid from Astragalus) inhibits BATF, a protein that T follicular helper cells need to give B-cells their signal to produce anti-AChR antibodies
- **Th17/Treg balance:** Suppresses the JAK2/STAT3 signalling that drives pro-inflammatory Th17 cells, while supporting Treg stability
- **NMJ protection:** May protect mitochondrial health at the neuromuscular junction via mitophagy pathways

**Safety:** Well-tolerated; no significant interactions with pyridostigmine or corticosteroids documented. Use under practitioner guidance.

#### Buzhong Yiqi Tang (补中益气汤)

A classic 13th-century formula — the standard herbal prescription in Chinese integrative neurology for MG. The chief herb is Astragalus (in large doses), alongside ginseng, Atractylodes, and "lifting" herbs. Clinical studies from integrative medicine centres show improvements in QMG scores and reduced steroid dependence.

#### Shengxian Decoction — Mechanism Discovery (2025)

A 2025 study identified that this traditional Qi-lifting formula promotes degradation of TAZ protein, which normally blocks FOXP3 expression in developing Tregs. By removing this block, the formula directly enhances Treg function — an elegant molecular bridge between a traditional herbal approach and current MG immunology.

#### Tripterygium wilfordii (Thunder God Vine / Lei Gong Teng)

Potent immune-resetting herb. Its active compound triptolide stabilises FOXP3 via microRNA-125a-5p, directly prevents Treg conversion to pro-inflammatory cells, and suppresses Th17 differentiation. These mechanisms are directly relevant to MG.

**However:** Tripterygium carries a significant risk of hepatotoxicity and reproductive toxicity. It has a narrow therapeutic window and must not be self-prescribed. Only consider under the supervision of a specialist integrative medicine practitioner with regular liver function monitoring.

### Acupuncture

**Evidence level: Moderate (adjunctive use)**

A **2024 systematic review** of 14 randomised controlled trials assessed acupuncture as an adjunctive treatment for MG. Results showed statistically significant improvement in QMG scores, effective rate, and MG-ADL scores compared to medication alone.

#### How Acupuncture Works

Modern research has moved beyond meridian explanations toward neuroimmunological mechanisms:

- **Vagal nerve activation:** Electroacupuncture at ST36 (Zusanli) activates the vagal cholinergic anti-inflammatory reflex — releasing acetylcholine via vagal efferents that activate nicotinic receptors on immune cells. This pathway has particular resonance in MG, where the nicotinic AChR is the central pathological target.
- **HPA axis normalisation:** Acupuncture reduces excess cortisol production, protecting FOXP3 methylation and Treg stability — directly counteracting the stress-flare mechanism documented in MG
- **Th17/Treg shift:** Multiple studies document shifts toward Treg-promoting cytokine profiles (increased IL-10, TGF-beta; decreased IL-17, IFN-gamma) after a course of acupuncture

#### Key Points Used in MG

Practitioners typically individualise treatment based on pattern differentiation, but commonly used points include:
- **ST36 (Zusanli)** — most studied point for immune modulation; activates vagal anti-inflammatory pathway
- **GV20 (Baihui)** — used to "lift" Qi; addresses ptosis in TCM terms; has central nervous system regulatory effects
- **SP6 (Sanyinjiao)** — Spleen, Kidney, and Liver meridian intersection
- **Local eye points (BL1, ST2, GB1)** — for ocular MG or prominent ptosis/diplopia
- **CV12 and CV6** — Spleen and Kidney Qi support

#### Moxibustion

Burning mugwort applied to acupuncture points is routinely combined with needling for patients with "Yang deficiency" patterns. Warming moxibustion at CV4, CV6, ST36, and GV4 is commonly prescribed and has demonstrated effects on NK cell activity and IL-2 production.

## Ayurvedic Medicine

### How Ayurveda Understands MG

Ayurveda — the traditional medicine of the Indian subcontinent — views MG as a **Vata disorder**. Specifically, it involves dysregulation of **Vyana Vayu** (the sub-dosha governing motor impulse transmission and circulation) combined with **Kapha/Ama** (obstructive metabolic residue) blocking the channels through which nerve impulses travel. The primary affected tissue is **Mamsa Dhatu** (muscle tissue).

Ayurveda's framework for autoimmune disease traces the root cause to **Mandagni** (diminished metabolic fire), which generates Ama (unprocessed metabolic residue). When Ama accumulates in the body's deeper channels, it disrupts cellular self-recognition — conceptually similar to the breakdown of central tolerance in MG.

The therapeutic aim is to restore **Ojas** (vital immune essence) through herbs, diet, and purification treatments.

### Ayurvedic Herbs

#### Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera)

**Evidence level: Moderate-Strong (adaptogenic and immune-modulatory properties)**

The most clinically researched Ayurvedic herb. Multiple double-blind RCTs confirm 15–32% reductions in serum cortisol compared to placebo.

**Relevance to MG:**
- Cortisol suppresses FOXP3 transcription, destabilising Tregs. By reliably lowering cortisol, Ashwagandha may improve Treg stability — addressing the molecular mechanism behind stress-triggered MG flares
- Its withanolide compounds inhibit NF-κB and reduce pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-6)
- Contains compounds with mild acetylcholine-mimetic effects — potentially supportive of NMJ function, though monitoring is sensible when combining with pyridostigmine

**Safety:** Generally considered safe. High doses (>1000mg/day extract equivalent) warrant more caution. The mild cholinergic potential means monitoring is sensible alongside pyridostigmine.

#### Bacopa monnieri (Brahmi)

Primarily used in MG to address the cognitive and psychological dimension — the mental fog, stress-reactivity, and sleep disturbance common during high-dose steroid courses. Has adaptogenic, anti-inflammatory (COX-2 and 5-LOX inhibition), and neuroprotective properties.

**Caution:** Bacopa weakly inhibits acetylcholinesterase — the same mechanism as pyridostigmine. Combining both without supervision could potentially produce additive cholinergic effects (excess salivation, GI cramping, muscle twitching) at higher doses.

#### Shatavari (Asparagus racemosus)

Primarily a female Rasayana (rejuvenative), but with immunoadjuvant properties relevant to MG: shatavarin glycosides appear to enhance cellular immunity while moderating humoral (antibody-producing) immune activity. Anti-inflammatory via TNF-alpha and IL-6 inhibition. No significant drug interactions at normal doses.

### Panchakarma

Panchakarma — Ayurveda's five purification procedures — is used by experienced Ayurvedic physicians for deep constitutional treatment of autoimmune conditions. Procedures most discussed for MG-like conditions:

- **Nasya (nasal medicated oil administration):** Medicated oils administered through the nostrils, targeting cranial nerve and head-and-face motor function. Relevant to ocular and bulbar MG symptoms.
- **Basti (medicated enema):** Considered the primary treatment for Vata disorders. Oil-based and decoction-based enemas target the colon — the same site where SCFA production and Treg induction now occur in conventional immunology research.
- **Abhyanga (oil massage):** Documented effects on cortisol reduction and parasympathetic tone.

**Important:** Full Panchakarma requires a trained Ayurvedic physician. It is not appropriate during MG crises, when muscle weakness is severely limiting, or without neurological clearance.

## Safety and Contraindications

### Herbs Definitively Contraindicated in MG

- **Curare-containing plants** (*Strychnos toxifera*, *Chondrodendron tomentosum*): Directly block neuromuscular transmission. Found in some exotic traditional preparations.
- **Gelsemium sempervirens (Yellow Jasmine):** Used in some homeopathic preparations; contains gelsemine which inhibits nerve transmission. Avoid entirely.
- **Quinine-containing herbs** (Cinchona bark, high-dose Artemisia): Quinine has neuromuscular blocking properties documented to worsen MG.
- **Huperzine A** (from *Huperzia serrata*): A potent acetylcholinesterase inhibitor found in some "brain supplement" formulas. **Never combine with pyridostigmine** — additive cholinergic toxicity.

### Herbs Requiring High Caution

- **High-dose magnesium supplements:** Inhibits acetylcholine release at the NMJ. Dietary magnesium from food is fine; therapeutic supplements are not.
- **Immune-stimulating mushrooms at pharmacological doses** (Maitake, Shiitake extract, Reishi): Culinary amounts are generally fine; therapeutic-dose extracts may upregulate the pathogenic immune response.
- **Echinacea (long-term):** Non-specific immune stimulant; may upregulate autoantibody production. Short-term for acute infection may be acceptable; long-term prophylactic use is not recommended.
- **Tripterygium wilfordii** at uncontrolled doses — specialist supervision only.

### Drug Interactions Table

| Herb / Supplement | MG Medication at Risk | Interaction |
|---|---|---|
| St John's Wort | Cyclosporin, tacrolimus, MMF, biologics | CYP3A4 induction — reduces drug levels, risks loss of immunosuppression |
| Grapefruit / Bergamot | Cyclosporin, tacrolimus | CYP3A4 inhibition — increases drug toxicity |
| Liquorice root (high dose) | Corticosteroids | Potentiates corticosteroid effects, Cushing-like side effects |
| High-dose Turmeric / Curcumin | Azathioprine | Inhibits thiopurine S-methyltransferase at high doses |
| Kava | Azathioprine, MMF | Hepatotoxicity risk compounded |
| Bacopa monnieri (high dose) | Pyridostigmine | Additive acetylcholinesterase inhibition |
| Huperzine A | Pyridostigmine | Additive AChE inhibition — contraindicated |

See also [Drugs to Avoid](drugs-to-avoid.md) for pharmaceutical drug interactions.

## Evidence Classification

| Approach | Evidence Level |
|---|---|
| Compound Huangqi/Astragalus + conventional treatment | **Evidence-based** (19 RCTs, n=1,283, 2022 meta-analysis) |
| Acupuncture as adjunct | **Evidence-based** (14 RCTs, 2024 systematic review) |
| Ashwagandha — cortisol reduction | **Evidence-based** (multiple RCTs, systematic review level) |
| Vitamin D correction | **Evidence-based** (deficiency documented in MG cohorts; direct FOXP3 mechanism) |
| Mediterranean diet — inflammation reduction | **Evidence-based** (systematic review level in autoimmune disease) |
| Meditation/MBSR — immune regulation | **Evidence-based** (multiple RCTs in autoimmune populations) |
| Buzhong Yiqi Tang | **Traditional use with characterised mechanisms** |
| Shengxian Decoction | **Mechanisms characterised in 2025; clinical evidence emerging** |
| Tai chi | **Growing RCT evidence in adjacent conditions** |
| Panchakarma for MG | **Traditional use; no controlled evidence** |
| Mucuna pruriens for MG | **Plausible mechanism; no clinical evidence** |
