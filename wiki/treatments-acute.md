# Acute Treatments — IVIG, Plasmapheresis, and Crisis Management

Some situations in MG require rapid treatment — not the slow, steady work of long-term immunosuppressants, but an immediate reduction in the circulating antibodies causing harm. Two treatments can achieve this quickly: **intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG)** and **plasmapheresis (plasma exchange)**. Both are used for myasthenic crisis, severe acute worsening, and as preparation before surgery.

---

## When Acute Treatment is Needed

Rapid treatment is used in the following situations:

- **Myasthenic crisis** — life-threatening respiratory failure requiring ventilation (see [Myasthenic Crisis](myasthenic-crisis.md))
- **Severe acute worsening** — significant deterioration in swallowing or breathing that is not quite at crisis level but requires urgent intervention
- **Pre-surgical stabilisation** — before thymectomy or other major surgery, to ensure MG is well controlled
- **Bridge therapy** — while waiting for long-term immunosuppressants to take effect

---

## Intravenous Immunoglobulin (IVIG)

IVIG involves infusing large quantities of pooled human IgG antibodies — collected from thousands of blood donors — directly into the bloodstream.

### How It Works

Several mechanisms are proposed:
- **FcRn competition**: IVIG saturates the FcRn recycling receptors, causing pathogenic autoantibodies to be degraded faster than they normally would be
- **Immune modulation**: The infused antibodies appear to modulate the activity of immune cells involved in the attack on the NMJ
- **Anti-idiotypic antibodies**: The pooled antibodies may contain antibodies that directly neutralise the disease-causing autoantibodies

### Administration

- Typically **2 g/kg** given over 2–5 days (as a series of daily infusions)
- Given through a drip in the arm
- Usually administered in hospital, though some people receive it as an outpatient or at home

### Onset and Duration of Effect

- Improvement usually begins within **days**
- Effect typically lasts **3–6 weeks**
- For crisis management, it is used as a bridge while longer-term treatments take effect

### Side Effects

Generally well tolerated, but can cause:
- **Headache** — sometimes severe (aseptic meningitis); can be helped by ensuring adequate hydration
- Flushing, fever, and chills during the infusion
- Fatigue for a day or two after
- **Thromboembolic events** (blood clots): deep vein thrombosis, pulmonary embolism, or stroke are the most serious risks, particularly with rapid infusion rates, pre-existing cardiovascular disease, or in older patients. IVIG must be infused slowly.
- Kidney problems (acute renal failure) — particularly with sucrose-containing IVIG products in patients with pre-existing kidney disease
- **IgA deficiency: CONTRAINDICATED** if you have IgA deficiency *and* anti-IgA antibodies — this combination can cause life-threatening anaphylaxis. A screening test for IgA deficiency is done before first administration; IgA-depleted IVIG preparations exist for those who need IVIG but have IgA deficiency.

## Plasmapheresis (Plasma Exchange / PLEX)

Plasmapheresis is a mechanical process in which blood is removed from the body, separated into its components, and the plasma (the liquid part, which contains the antibodies) is removed and replaced with an alternative fluid. The blood cells are returned.

### How It Works

Plasmapheresis directly removes pathogenic autoantibodies from the bloodstream. Unlike IVIG, it is not modulating the immune system — it is physically extracting the problem.

### Administration

- Typically **5–7 sessions** over 10–14 days, each session taking 2–4 hours
- Requires venous access — often a **central line** (a line inserted into a large vein in the neck or chest)
- Performed in hospital

### Onset and Duration of Effect

- Often **faster onset** than IVIG — improvement can begin within days of the first exchange
- Effect is temporary — typically lasts **3–6 weeks** before antibody levels rise again
- Used as a bridge to longer-term treatments

### Side Effects

- Drops in **blood pressure** during the procedure (volume shifts)
- **Bleeding risk**: clotting factors are removed along with antibodies — avoid invasive procedures immediately after PLEX until clotting normalises
- **Hypocalcaemia** (low calcium): the citrate anticoagulant used in the PLEX circuit chelates (binds) calcium. This can cause perioral tingling, muscle cramps, and in severe cases tetany. Calcium gluconate is given to replace it. Patients should report any tingling around the mouth during the procedure.
- **Infection risk** at the central venous catheter site — catheter-related infections (including sepsis) are a recognised complication
- **Hypothermia** from cold replacement fluids
- Anaemia (small amounts of red cells lost during machine processing)
- Not suitable for everyone (e.g., those with severe clotting disorders, haemodynamic instability, or very difficult venous access)

**Note on drug levels after PLEX:** Pyridostigmine levels may fall after a PLEX course; dose adjustments may be needed. Oral immunosuppressants should also be checked after a PLEX course as plasma drug levels may be affected.

## IVIG vs Plasmapheresis — Which Is Better?

Both are effective for myasthenic crisis and short-term stabilisation. The choice increasingly depends on MG subtype, urgency, and patient factors:

| Factor | IVIG | Plasmapheresis |
|---|---|---|
| Speed of onset | 3–5 days | **1–3 days (faster)** |
| Mechanism | Immunomodulation / FcRn saturation | Direct antibody removal |
| AChR+ MG efficacy | High | High |
| **MuSK+ MG efficacy** | **Lower** | **Higher — preferred** |
| Preferred for crisis + respiratory failure | Second choice | **First choice** |
| Invasiveness | Peripheral IV drip | Central line required |
| Availability | Widely available | Requires specialist equipment |
| Clotting effects | No direct effect | Removes clotting factors |
| IgA deficiency | Contraindication (risk of anaphylaxis) | No restriction |
| Outpatient possible? | Yes | Generally requires hospital |
| Duration of effect | 4–8 weeks | 4–8 weeks |

**Key clinical guidance (2025):**
- **Plasmapheresis is now the first-line treatment for myasthenic crisis** — particularly where respiratory failure is present or in MuSK-positive patients
- **IVIG is preferred** for moderate exacerbations, pre-surgical bridge therapy, and where PLEX access or tolerance is limited
- For **MuSK-positive MG**, plasmapheresis is considerably more effective than IVIG

### Critical Sequencing Rule

> **Never give IVIG immediately before plasmapheresis.** The plasmapheresis procedure removes antibodies — and exogenous IgG — from the plasma. IVIG infused before PLEX will simply be removed in the next session, wasting the treatment.
>
> The correct sequence when both are needed: **PLEX first, then IVIG** if required.

Similarly, if **rituximab** is needed urgently alongside PLEX, rituximab should be re-dosed *after* the PLEX course, as PLEX will remove rituximab from the circulation.

## Subcutaneous Immunoglobulin (SCIG)

In some cases, immunoglobulin can be administered subcutaneously (under the skin) at home, rather than intravenously in hospital. This is used for maintenance therapy in some patients — not typically for acute crisis management but for ongoing immune support in some settings.

---

## Managing the Aftermath of a Crisis

After a myasthenic crisis has been treated and the person is recovering:

- **Long-term immunosuppression** is typically reviewed and adjusted — a crisis may indicate that current treatments are insufficient
- **Trigger identification** — what caused the crisis should be investigated and addressed
- **Psychological support** may be needed — a crisis can be frightening and traumatic for both the patient and their family
- **Rehabilitation** — occupational therapy and physiotherapy may be helpful after a period of weakness and hospitalisation
