# Pregnancy and Myasthenia Gravis

Becoming pregnant with MG requires careful planning and close collaboration between your neurologist, obstetrician, and — when the time comes — your anaesthetist and neonatal team. The good news is that most women with MG have successful pregnancies. The key is preparation: stable disease before conception, medication review in advance, and vigilant monitoring throughout.

---

## Planning for Pregnancy

The ideal time to become pregnant is when MG is as well controlled as possible — ideally at **Minimal Manifestation Status (MMS)** or better for at least **6–12 months** before conception.

**Before you conceive:**
- Discuss your pregnancy plans with your neurologist at least 6–12 months in advance
- Review all medications: some MG drugs are safe, others must be stopped well before conception
- If thymectomy is planned and appropriate, complete it at least **12 months before conception** to maximise its disease-modifying benefit
- Ensure you are up to date with recommended vaccinations (live vaccines cannot be given once on immunosuppressants)

### Medication Review Before Conception

| Drug | Action Required Before Conception |
|---|---|
| Mycophenolate mofetil (MMF/CellCept) | **STOP at least 6 weeks before conception** — highly teratogenic |
| Methotrexate | **STOP at least 3–6 months before conception** (for both women AND men) — teratogenic |
| Pyridostigmine | Continue — safe in pregnancy |
| Prednisolone | Continue — generally safe; preferred immunosuppressant in pregnancy |
| Azathioprine | Continue if MG requires it — acceptable risk when benefit outweighs risk |
| Ciclosporin / Tacrolimus | Discuss with specialist — can be continued with monitoring if essential |

## How MG Behaves During Pregnancy

MG can be unpredictable in pregnancy. Response varies significantly between individuals and can also differ between different pregnancies in the same woman.

**Trimester by trimester:**

- **First trimester:** MG may worsen. This is also the most critical period for fetal development — drug safety is paramount.
- **Second trimester:** Many women experience improvement or even remission. Progesterone-driven immunomodulation may explain this.
- **Third trimester:** Variable. Some women improve further; others experience worsening. Avoid IV pyridostigmine in this trimester (risk of stimulating uterine contractions).
- **Postpartum:** **The highest-risk period for MG flare** — in the first 4–8 weeks after delivery. Immunological rebound after the relative immune tolerance of pregnancy means flares are common. This period requires close monitoring.

**Monitoring schedule:**
- Neurology review every 4–6 weeks throughout pregnancy (more frequently if symptoms are changing)
- MG severity scores (MG-ADL, QMG) at each review
- Regular medication level monitoring (ciclosporin/tacrolimus if used — pharmacokinetics change significantly in pregnancy)
- Fetal growth monitoring (especially with ciclosporin or tacrolimus)

## Drug Safety in Pregnancy

| Drug | Safety in Pregnancy | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Pyridostigmine** (oral) | **SAFE** | Avoid IV form in third trimester (may stimulate uterine contractions) |
| **Prednisolone** | **Generally safe** | Preferred immunosuppressant; slightly increased risk of cleft palate (first trimester); neonatal adrenal suppression at high doses near term |
| **Azathioprine** | **Relatively safe if essential** | Category D; crosses placenta; some neonatal bone marrow suppression risk; acceptable when MG cannot otherwise be controlled |
| **Ciclosporin** | **Caution** | Category C; associated with intrauterine growth restriction; not teratogenic; specialist monitoring required |
| **Tacrolimus** | **Caution** | Category C; neonatal hyperkalaemia and renal issues possible; specialist monitoring required |
| **Mycophenolate mofetil** | **ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED** | Causes miscarriage and birth defects (MMF embryopathy: cleft palate, ear defects, cardiac defects) — stop 6 weeks before conception |
| **Methotrexate** | **ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED** | Highly teratogenic; stop 3–6 months before conception in both women and men |
| **Rituximab** | **Not recommended** | Limited data; neonatal B-cell depletion reported; use only if benefit clearly outweighs risk |
| **FcRn inhibitors** (efgartigimod, rozanolixizumab, nipocalimab) | **Not recommended** | FcRn is critical for maternal-fetal IgG transfer and neonatal passive immunity; theoretical risk of neonatal hypogammaglobulinaemia; very limited data |
| **Complement inhibitors** (eculizumab, ravulizumab, zilucoplan) | **Caution — very limited data** | Some data from use in PNH (another complement-driven condition); discuss individual risk-benefit |
| **IVIG** | **SAFE — preferred rescue therapy** | Used for acute MG exacerbations during pregnancy |
| **Plasmapheresis** | **Can be used if essential** | Technical challenges in pregnancy but feasible; preferred for MuSK+ crisis during pregnancy |

## Labour and Delivery

### Mode of Delivery

- **Vaginal delivery is preferred** unless there are standard obstetric reasons for Caesarean section — MG does not itself indicate Caesarean
- The **first stage of labour** (uterine contractions) is not affected by MG — the uterus is smooth muscle and is not impacted
- The **second stage of labour** (pushing) relies on voluntary skeletal muscle and can be affected by MG weakness. This may be prolonged.
- **Forceps or vacuum-assisted delivery** should be available and planned for, to assist if skeletal muscle fatigue limits pushing

### Anaesthesia for Labour and Delivery

- **Regional anaesthesia (epidural or spinal) is strongly preferred** over general anaesthesia
- General anaesthesia carries significantly increased risks in MG patients (variable and unpredictable response to neuromuscular blocking agents; risk of difficulty with extubation due to muscle weakness) — see [Anaesthesia and MG](anaesthesia.md)

### CRITICAL SAFETY WARNING — Magnesium Sulfate

> **Magnesium sulfate is STRICTLY CONTRAINDICATED in MG.**

Magnesium sulfate is the standard obstetric treatment for **pre-eclampsia seizure prophylaxis** — a common drug in labour wards. However, in MG, magnesium blocks calcium channels at the neuromuscular junction, dramatically impairing neuromuscular transmission and potentially triggering a **life-threatening myasthenic crisis**.

**All obstetric staff caring for a patient with MG must be explicitly informed of this contraindication.** If pre-eclampsia occurs, alternative anticonvulsants must be used — discuss with your neurologist and obstetrician in advance.

Ensure your MG diagnosis and the magnesium contraindication are clearly documented in your obstetric notes and highlighted on any hospital admission.

### Steroid Cover During Labour

Patients on chronic corticosteroids (prednisolone >5 mg/day for more than 3 months) have a suppressed natural stress response. The body normally produces extra cortisol during the physical stress of labour — this automatic response is blunted by long-term steroid use.

**IV hydrocortisone "stress dosing"** should be given during active labour. This is standard practice and should be planned with your medical team in advance.

### Pyridostigmine During Labour

- Continue oral pyridostigmine throughout labour
- If you are unable to swallow during labour, a nasogastric tube or IV neostigmine (a short-acting cousin of pyridostigmine) can be used under close medical supervision
- Avoid IV pyridostigmine (risk of stimulating uterine contractions)

## Neonatal Myasthenia Gravis (Transient Neonatal MG)

Approximately **10–15% of babies** born to mothers with MG (some studies report up to 20%) experience **transient neonatal myasthenia gravis (TNMG)**.

### Why Does It Happen?

Maternal IgG antibodies cross the placenta as part of normal pregnancy (this is how babies receive passive immunity against infections). In some infants, the maternal anti-AChR or anti-MuSK antibodies that cause MG in the mother temporarily impair the baby's own neuromuscular junction.

**Important:** Maternal antibody levels do not reliably predict whether TNMG will occur or how severe it will be. Even mothers with very low antibody levels, or in clinical remission, can have affected babies.

### Symptoms in the Baby

Symptoms appear within **hours to days** of birth:
- Weak, floppy cry
- Poor feeding — difficulty with sucking and swallowing
- Facial weakness
- Generalised floppiness (hypotonia — "floppy baby")
- Drooping eyelids (ptosis)
- Respiratory difficulty (most serious — may require ventilation)

### Duration and Outcome

TNMG is **self-limiting and temporary**. Once maternal antibodies are cleared from the baby's circulation (typically within **2–4 weeks**, occasionally up to 3 months), the baby's own NMJ function is completely normal. There is no permanent damage.

### Management

- All babies born to MG mothers should be **observed in a neonatal unit** (or facility with NICU capability) for at least **3–4 days** after birth
- Treatment if symptomatic: oral or intramuscular **neostigmine** to manage weakness; respiratory support if needed
- Severe cases may require **IVIG or exchange transfusion** to accelerate removal of maternal antibodies

## Breastfeeding

| Drug | Breastfeeding Safety |
|---|---|
| Pyridostigmine | **Safe** — minimal transfer into breast milk |
| Prednisolone (up to 40 mg/day) | **Safe** — low transfer; prednisolone preferred over prednisone |
| Azathioprine | **Caution** — low levels in milk; some guidance considers compatible; consider monitoring infant FBC |
| Mycophenolate mofetil | **CONTRAINDICATED** — excreted in milk; potential infant toxicity |
| Methotrexate | **CONTRAINDICATED** |
| FcRn inhibitors | **Data lacking** — caution advised |

---

## Postpartum Period

The first 8 weeks after delivery is the **highest-risk period for MG flare**. Plan for this:

- More frequent neurology contact in the early postpartum weeks
- Resume any medications paused during pregnancy once breastfeeding decisions are made
- If you were taking a less effective drug during pregnancy (e.g., azathioprine instead of MMF), discuss switching back with your specialist after delivery
- Contraception: if restarting MMF or methotrexate, reliable contraception is essential — both drugs are teratogenic
- Practical support: exhaustion is a powerful MG trigger. Accepting help with night feeds and early childcare is not weakness — it is good MG management.
