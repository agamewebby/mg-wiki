# Diagnosis of MG

Diagnosing MG can sometimes take time. Because muscle weakness is a symptom of many conditions, and because MG fluctuates in ways that can make it hard to capture in a single appointment, it is not unusual for there to be a delay from first symptoms to confirmed diagnosis. Understanding what tests are involved can help you prepare for the process.

---

## Why Diagnosis Can Be Challenging

Several features of MG make it tricky to diagnose:

- Weakness is a very common symptom with many possible causes
- MG symptoms fluctuate — a person may feel nearly normal at a clinic appointment but significantly affected at other times
- In ocular MG, symptoms may be very subtle (mild ptosis or intermittent double vision)
- Up to **50% of people with ocular MG** and **5–15% of those with generalised MG** do not have detectable antibodies in standard blood tests
- Symptoms overlap with other neurological and neuromuscular conditions

---

## Initial Assessment — What Your Doctor Will Check

When MG is suspected, a neurologist will take a detailed history and conduct a thorough neurological examination. Key things they will assess include:

- **Pattern of weakness** — does it follow the characteristic fatigable pattern? Which muscles are involved?
- **Time of day** — are symptoms worse later in the day or after activity?
- **Eye signs** — is there ptosis or double vision? Does holding one eyelid up cause the other to droop (a test called the "cover test" or Cogan's lid twitch)?
- **Speech and swallowing** — is there nasal speech, slurring, or difficulty swallowing?
- **Neck strength** — can the person hold their head up?
- **Limb strength** — is weakness proximal (near the body) and does it fatigue with repetition?
- **Respiratory function** — can they breathe comfortably? (Vitally important to assess)

---

## The Ice Pack Test (Bedside Assessment for Ptosis)

A simple, safe, and surprisingly effective test for ptosis in MG: applying an **ice pack to the closed eyelid for 2 minutes**. In MG, ptosis often improves significantly with cooling (because the acetylcholinesterase enzyme works less efficiently at lower temperatures, allowing more acetylcholine to accumulate). This test can be a useful pointer in clinic.

---

## Antibody Blood Tests

A blood test for **autoantibodies** is usually the first confirmatory test:

### AChR Antibodies
- Positive in approximately **80–85%** of people with generalised MG and **50%** of those with ocular MG
- A positive result is strongly supportive of the diagnosis
- The level of antibodies does not reliably predict severity

### MuSK Antibodies
- Tested when AChR antibodies are absent
- Positive in approximately **5–8%** of generalised MG overall; a higher proportion of AChR-negative cases
- A positive MuSK result changes the treatment approach (see [Biological Therapies](treatments-biologics.md))

### LRP4 Antibodies
- Tested when both AChR and MuSK antibodies are absent
- Found in approximately 1–3% of MG cases
- Still being established as a routine test in all centres

### Cell-Based Assays
- More sensitive than standard tests — can detect antibodies in some people who appear seronegative on conventional testing
- Not yet universally available but increasingly used in specialist centres
- Important for diagnosing ocular MG, where standard antibody tests are often negative

### Seronegative MG
- Around 5–15% of people with generalised MG and up to 50% with ocular MG will test negative for all known antibodies
- Seronegative MG is not "less real" — the diagnosis is made on clinical and electrophysiological grounds
- Specialised testing may detect antibodies that standard assays miss

---

## Electrodiagnostic Tests

These tests measure the electrical activity of nerves and muscles and are crucial when antibody tests are negative or the diagnosis is uncertain.

### Repetitive Nerve Stimulation (RNS)
- The nerve is stimulated repeatedly with small electrical pulses
- In MG, the muscle response typically **decreases progressively** with stimulation (a "decremental response")
- A drop of 10% or more in the electrical response is considered significant
- Sensitivity: approximately 50–70% for generalised MG; lower for ocular MG
- Abnormal in most cases of MG when the relevant muscle groups are tested

### Single-Fibre Electromyography (SFEMG)
- Considered the **most sensitive test** for MG
- Measures the timing of individual muscle fibre contractions within the same motor unit
- In MG, there is increased variability in timing (called "jitter") due to impaired neuromuscular transmission
- Can detect abnormalities even when other tests are normal
- Particularly useful for diagnosing mild MG or ocular MG
- Technically demanding; typically performed only in specialist centres
- Can confirm the diagnosis in approximately **90% of patients** with suspected MG when other tests are inconclusive

---

## Imaging

### CT Scan of the Chest
- Performed in virtually all people diagnosed with MG
- Looks for a **thymoma** (a tumour of the thymus gland) — present in approximately 10–15% of people with MG
- Even if no thymoma is found, the thymus may be abnormally enlarged (hyperplasia)

### MRI
- Occasionally used to look more carefully at thymus anatomy or to exclude other neurological causes of weakness
- **MRI of extraocular muscles** can be helpful in diagnosing ocular MG

---

## The Tensilon Test (Edrophonium Test) — Less Common Now

Historically, an injection of **edrophonium** (a very short-acting acetylcholinesterase inhibitor) was used as a diagnostic test. A positive response — rapid but brief improvement in symptoms — was considered strongly suggestive of MG.

This test is now used less frequently because:
- It carries cardiac risks (bradycardia) and requires monitoring
- False positives and false negatives occur
- Antibody and electrophysiological tests are more reliable
- It requires specialist administration

However, it may still be used in selected cases, particularly where a rapid diagnosis is needed.

---

## Excluding Other Conditions

Because muscle weakness has many causes, the diagnostic process also involves ruling out other conditions, including:
- **Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome (LEMS)** — a similar condition but caused by antibodies on the nerve side of the junction; typically associated with cancer
- **Motor neurone disease** — causes progressive weakness but usually with different features
- **Multiple sclerosis** — central nervous system disease with different features
- **Mitochondrial disease** — can cause eye muscle weakness
- **Graves' ophthalmopathy** — thyroid disease causing eye muscle changes
- **Botulism** — toxin poisoning affecting the NMJ
- **Congenital myasthenic syndromes** — genetic conditions that look similar to MG but are not autoimmune

---

## After Diagnosis

Once MG is diagnosed, it is important to:
- Determine your **antibody subtype** (AChR, MuSK, LRP4, seronegative) — as this guides treatment
- Assess **disease severity** and which muscles are most affected
- Perform a **CT chest scan** to check for thymoma
- Establish **baseline lung function** (especially important before starting treatments that might affect breathing)
- Begin treatment and establish a monitoring plan with your neurologist
