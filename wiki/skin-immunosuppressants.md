# Skin Care on Immunosuppressants

If you are taking immunosuppressant medication for MG, looking after your skin is an important part of your overall healthcare. People who are immunosuppressed have a greater risk of developing certain types of skin cancer, and this risk increases with time. The good news is that most skin cancers are highly treatable when caught early — and there is a great deal you can do to reduce your risk.

---

## Why Are Immunosuppressed People at Higher Risk?

Immunosuppressant drugs work by reducing the activity of your immune system. This is exactly what is needed to control MG — but it has a side effect: the immune system is less able to detect and destroy abnormal cells, including early skin cancer cells. This is why long-term immunosuppression is associated with an increased risk of skin cancer and some benign (non-cancerous) skin lesions.

---

## Who is at Highest Risk?

Everyone who takes immunosuppressants has some increased risk, and this increases with both the duration of treatment and the number of immunosuppressant drugs taken. Certain characteristics increase the risk further:

- **Fair skin** that burns easily in the sun
- **Light-coloured eyes** (blue, grey, or hazel)
- **Blonde or red hair**
- **Numerous freckles**
- Being an outdoor worker or having had **heavy sun exposure in the past**
- A personal or family **history of skin cancer**
- Taking **more than one immunosuppressant drug**

**If you are of African, Arab, Asian or South-East Asian descent**, you are at lower risk of sunlight-related skin cancers, though sun protection is still advisable.

---

## How to Check Your Skin

Skin cancer is much easier to treat when detected early. **Check your skin all over your body once a month.** Use a mirror to check hard-to-see areas such as your back or the backs of your legs. Ask a friend or family member to help with areas you cannot easily see yourself.

### What to Look For

See your doctor if you notice any marks on your skin that are:
- **Growing**
- **Bleeding**
- **Changing in appearance** in any way
- **Not healing completely**

Also pay attention to:
- New lumps or bumps anywhere on the skin
- Moles or spots that have changed in shape, colour, or size
- Sores that have been present for more than a few weeks

**ABCDE of moles** — a useful check for melanoma:
- **A**symmetry — one half different from the other
- **B**order — irregular, ragged, or blurred edges
- **C**olour — uneven colour, multiple shades
- **D**iameter — larger than 6mm (though melanomas can be smaller)
- **E**volving — any change in size, shape, colour, or new symptoms like bleeding

## Sun Protection — The Key Prevention Strategy

The **main cause of skin cancer** in immunosuppressed patients is **exposure to ultraviolet (UV) radiation** from the sun. This applies all year round — not just in summer — and includes everyday outdoor activities such as gardening, walking, driving, and sport.

### Practical Sun Protection

- **Cover up**: Wear a wide-brimmed sun hat, long-sleeved tops, and trousers in sunny weather
- **Stay in the shade**: Particularly between **11am and 3pm**, when UV radiation is strongest
- **Use sunscreen**: Apply a sunscreen with **SPF 30 or higher** with at least **3–4 stars UVA/UVB protection**. Re-apply every 2–3 hours and after swimming
- **Don't forget**: the backs of hands, ears, the back of the neck, and the top of the feet if wearing sandals
- **Winter sun counts too**: Skiing holidays and other winter outdoor activities can involve significant UV exposure — apply sunscreen as in summer
- **Avoid sun beds and sunlamps** — these are concentrated sources of UV-A radiation and significantly increase the risk of all skin cancers. Do not use them

### UV-Protective Clothing

UV-protective swimwear and beachwear is made from materials that block UVA and UVB rays — it looks like normal swimwear but provides much better protection than standard fabric. This can be helpful when swimming on holiday.

---

## Vitamin D — A Balance

Sun exposure is the main way the body produces vitamin D, which is important for bone health, muscle health, and immune function. The challenge is that it is almost impossible to determine a "safe" amount of sun exposure that provides enough vitamin D without increasing skin cancer risk.

**Practical guidance:**
- Brief, everyday exposure of the face and forearms (without sunscreen) during normal outdoor activities is generally sufficient — but avoid sunburn
- Eating **vitamin D-rich foods** (oily fish, eggs, fortified foods) helps
- If you are concerned about your vitamin D levels — especially if you are also on steroids (which affect bone density) — ask your doctor to check your level with a blood test
- **Vitamin D supplements** can be prescribed or bought over the counter, and are often recommended for people with limited sun exposure or at particular risk of deficiency

## Skin Cancer Diagnosis and Treatment

If your doctor finds a concerning mark on your skin, they may:
- Refer you to a dermatologist for further assessment
- Arrange a **biopsy** — a small piece of skin is removed under local anaesthetic and examined under a microscope
- Alternatively, excise (cut out) the whole area at once

**Types of skin cancer:**

- **Basal cell carcinoma (BCC)** — the most common type; grows slowly and rarely spreads; can be cured in almost all cases if treated. May look like a pearly bump or a flat, flesh-coloured lesion
- **Squamous cell carcinoma (SCC)** — more common in immunosuppressed people than the general population; usually curable if caught early; can spread if neglected
- **Melanoma** — less common but more serious; can spread if not caught early. The ABCDE signs above are particularly relevant

**Skin cancer treatments include:**
- Surgery (the most common)
- Curettage and cautery (scraping and burning)
- Cryotherapy (freezing)
- Topical creams
- Photodynamic therapy
- Radiotherapy
- Chemotherapy (for advanced cases)

In some cases of serious skin cancer, it may be advised to reduce or stop immunosuppressant medication — this requires very careful discussion with both the dermatologist and the neurologist managing MG.

---

## Regular Skin Check-Ups

As well as self-checking monthly, it is worth asking your GP or dermatologist about **regular skin assessments** — particularly if you have been on immunosuppressants for several years or are in a high-risk group. Early detection saves lives.
