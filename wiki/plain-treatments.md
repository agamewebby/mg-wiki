# What Are My Treatment Options?

*A plain-English guide — no medical background needed.*

*Want the full detailed version? See [Treatments Overview](treatments.md)*

---

There is no cure for MG yet — but there are very good treatments. Most people with MG can achieve good control of their symptoms. Some people go into long periods of **remission** (very minimal or no symptoms at all).

This page walks through each treatment approach in plain language. You do not need a medical background to follow it.

---

## Treatment 1: Mestinon (Pyridostigmine)

**What it does:** This is usually the first medication people try. It does not change the underlying problem — but it helps your muscles work better despite the problem.

**How?** Your nerve sends a signal to your muscles by releasing a chemical called acetylcholine. An enzyme called acetylcholinesterase (say it: ah-see-til-KOH-lin-ESS-ter-aze) breaks this chemical down after use. Pyridostigmine slows down this breakdown, so more of the chemical is available to reach the muscles for longer.

Think of it like turning down the speed at which your plug socket leaks electricity — more charge gets through to the muscle.

**What to expect:** It starts working within 30–60 minutes. Effects last 3–6 hours. Most people take it 3–4 times per day. Timing tablets 30–45 minutes before meals can help with swallowing and chewing.

**Side effects:** Because it makes the nerve-to-muscle signal stronger everywhere, it can cause stomach cramps, diarrhoea, increased saliva, and muscle twitching if the dose is too high. These side effects are dose-related and usually manageable.

**Important:** Pyridostigmine manages symptoms but does not address the immune problem causing MG. For many people, it is enough. For others, additional treatment is needed.

## Treatment 2: Calming the Immune System (Immunosuppression)

Because MG is caused by the immune system attacking the body, treatments that calm the immune system can reduce or stop that attack.

### Steroids (Prednisolone)

**What they do:** Steroids are powerful immune system dampeners. They reduce the production of the misfiring antibodies that attack your muscles.

**What to expect:** They often cause an initial temporary worsening of symptoms before improving them — your neurologist will usually start at a low dose and increase gradually to avoid this. Improvement can take weeks to months.

**Side effects:** Steroids have well-known side effects with long-term use: weight gain, mood changes, thinning bones (osteoporosis), raised blood sugar. Your doctor will monitor for these and prescribe bone protection medication (like a calcium/Vitamin D supplement) alongside steroids.

**The aim:** To use the lowest dose that controls your MG, and to reduce and eventually stop steroids if possible, often using a steroid-sparing agent (see below).

### Steroid-Sparing Agents (Azathioprine, Mycophenolate)

These are immune-calming tablets taken alongside — or instead of — steroids. They work more slowly than steroids (it can take 6–18 months to see the full effect), but they have fewer long-term side effects.

- **Azathioprine (Imuran)** — the most commonly used. Taken daily. Requires regular blood tests to monitor liver and blood counts.
- **Mycophenolate mofetil (MMF/CellCept)** — an alternative, sometimes better tolerated.
- **Tacrolimus, ciclosporin, methotrexate** — further alternatives used in specific situations.

## Treatment 3: Biological Therapies (The Newer Treatments)

Biological therapies (sometimes called "biologics") are medicines that target a specific part of the immune system with great precision. They are more targeted than steroids and have transformed MG treatment in recent years.

### FcRn Inhibitors (Efgartigimod, Rozanolixizumab, Nipocalimab)

**What they do:** Your body's misfiring antibodies are a type of protein called IgG (immunoglobulin G). There is a protein in the body called FcRn that recycles these antibodies, keeping them alive in the bloodstream. FcRn inhibitors block this recycler — so the misfiring antibodies are broken down faster and their levels fall significantly.

**In simple terms:** Imagine the misfiring security guards (antibodies) have a rest area where they go to be recharged. FcRn inhibitors close down the rest area. The guards gradually retire without being replaced.

**What to expect:** Rapid action — IgG antibody levels can drop by 50–70% within a few weeks. Currently used in people with moderate to severe MG that has not responded well to conventional treatment.

- **Efgartigimod (Vyvgart)** — given by infusion every 2 weeks
- **Rozanolixizumab (Rystiggo)** — given by injection under the skin
- **Nipocalimab** — in late-stage trials

### Complement Inhibitors (Ravulizumab, Zilucoplan, Eculizumab)

These work differently. They block part of the immune system called the **complement system** — a cascade of proteins that acts like a wrecking crew, destroying the acetylcholine receptors. Blocking this cascade protects the receptors from damage.

- **Eculizumab (Soliris)** — older, infused monthly
- **Ravulizumab (Ultomiris)** — longer-acting, infused every 8 weeks
- **Zilucoplan (Zilbrysq)** — daily self-injection under the skin

These are specifically used in AChR antibody-positive MG (the most common type).

### Rituximab

**What it does:** Rituximab targets and depletes B-cells — the immune cells that actually make the misfiring antibodies. Without B-cells, fewer antibodies are produced.

**What to expect:** Given by infusion. Slow to act (3–6 months), but the effects can last 1–2 years. Particularly effective in MuSK-MG (a specific type of MG).

## Treatment 4: Surgery — Thymectomy

The thymus gland (in the chest, behind the breastbone) plays a central role in MG. In many people with MG, the thymus is abnormal — enlarged or containing abnormal tissue.

**Thymectomy** is the surgical removal of the thymus gland.

**Who benefits?** People with a thymoma (a tumour of the thymus) must have it removed. For people without a thymoma, a large clinical trial showed that thymectomy improves MG outcomes over 3–5 years — better symptom control, lower medication requirements — in people aged under 50 with generalised AChR antibody-positive MG.

**What does surgery involve?** Usually done by minimally invasive keyhole surgery through small incisions. Recovery takes a few weeks. The benefits build over years rather than appearing immediately.

---

## Treatment 5: Fast-Acting Treatments for Flares and Crises

When MG worsens quickly — during a flare or crisis — two fast-acting treatments can rapidly reduce the antibody load.

### Plasma Exchange (Plasmapheresis)

Blood is filtered through a machine that removes the plasma (the liquid part carrying the misfiring antibodies). The plasma is replaced with clean plasma or a substitute. Antibody levels drop rapidly. Effects last 4–8 weeks.

Think of it as a blood clean — removing the troublemakers from the bloodstream quickly.

### IVIG (Intravenous Immunoglobulin)

A preparation of antibodies from thousands of healthy donors is given by drip. These "normal" antibodies swamp the immune system and neutralise the misfiring ones. Effects are similar to plasma exchange but take a few days to kick in.

Both treatments work quickly (days) but temporarily. They are used to stabilise MG before surgery, during a crisis, or as a bridge while longer-term treatments take effect.

## Treatment 6: Complementary Approaches

Some complementary approaches — used **alongside** conventional medication, not instead of it — have genuine evidence supporting their use in MG. These include:

- **Acupuncture:** A 2024 systematic review of 14 clinical trials found significant improvements in MG symptoms when used alongside medication
- **Astragalus-based herbal preparations:** A 2022 analysis of 19 clinical trials (over 1,200 patients) found better outcomes when combined with conventional treatment
- **Stress management (meditation, tai chi, yoga):** Shown to reduce cortisol and inflammation in autoimmune conditions

**Important:** Always discuss any complementary treatment with your neurologist first. Some herbal preparations can interfere with MG medications. See [Complementary Therapies](complementary-therapies.md) for the full evidence base.

---

## What Will My Treatment Look Like?

Most people start with pyridostigmine. If that is not enough, steroids and/or a steroid-sparing agent are added. If these are not enough, a biological therapy may be considered.

Treatment is adjusted based on how you respond. The aim is always to use the minimum treatment needed to achieve good control — "minimum effective dose."

MG is unpredictable. Treatment may need adjusting over time. Regular check-ups with a neurologist experienced in MG are important.
