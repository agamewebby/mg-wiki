# Causes of MG

MG is caused by the immune system mistakenly attacking the neuromuscular junction — the point where nerve signals meet muscle fibres. Understanding why this happens involves looking at the mechanisms of antibody damage, the role of the thymus gland, genetic susceptibility, and the triggers that can tip a predisposed person into developing the disease.

---

## The Autoimmune Mechanism

MG is an **autoimmune disease**: the body's immune system, which normally defends against infection, turns against the body's own tissues. In MG, it specifically attacks proteins at the **neuromuscular junction (NMJ)** — the tiny gap between nerve endings and muscle fibres where the signal to contract is transmitted.

Different forms of MG involve different target proteins, and the damage occurs through different mechanisms. This is why the various subtypes of MG differ in their symptoms and their response to treatment.

---

## AChR Antibodies — The Most Common Form

In approximately **80–85% of generalised MG**, the immune system produces antibodies (specifically IgG1 and IgG3 class antibodies) that target the **acetylcholine receptor (AChR)** — the protein on the muscle surface that receives the nerve signal.

These antibodies cause damage through three overlapping mechanisms:

### 1. Complement-Mediated Destruction (Most Damaging)

The antibodies activate the **complement system** — a part of the immune system that can punch holes in cell membranes. When complement is activated at the NMJ, it forms a structure called the **Membrane Attack Complex (MAC)** that punches holes in the postsynaptic membrane. Over time, this:
- Destroys the deep folds of the junction (which are packed with receptors)
- Reduces the surface area available for signalling
- Depletes the pool of working AChRs

This process, known as "simplification" of the NMJ, is irreversible without treatment. Complement inhibition is now a major therapeutic target (see [Biological Therapies](treatments-biologics.md)).

### 2. Antigenic Modulation

The antibodies cross-link adjacent AChRs on the muscle surface. This cross-linking signals the muscle cell to pull the receptors inside itself (endocytosis) and break them down. This is a normal cellular "housekeeping" process that in MG is activated too rapidly and extensively — receptors are degraded faster than the body can replace them.

### 3. Direct Blockade

Some antibodies physically block the site on the receptor where acetylcholine would normally bind, preventing the receptor from functioning even when it is still intact. Even a small proportion of blocking antibodies can have a significant effect on neuromuscular transmission.

The combined effect of all three mechanisms is a dramatic reduction in the number and function of acetylcholine receptors.

---

## MuSK Antibodies — A Different Attack

In approximately **5–10% of generalised MG** (and up to 40% of AChR-negative cases), the immune system produces antibodies against **Muscle-Specific Kinase (MuSK)** — a protein that is not a receptor itself, but plays a critical role in organising and maintaining the cluster of AChRs at the junction.

MuSK is part of a signalling pathway — triggered by a protein called Agrin, working through LRP4 — that instructs AChRs to cluster together densely at the correct location on the muscle surface.

**Key differences from AChR-MG:**
- MuSK antibodies are predominantly **IgG4** subclass — which do **not** activate the complement system
- Instead of destroying the junction, they disrupt the Agrin-LRP4-MuSK signalling pathway, causing the AChR cluster to **disperse and gradually disappear**
- The architectural deterioration happens without the violent membrane destruction seen in AChR-positive MG
- This mechanistic difference explains why MuSK MG has more bulbar involvement, responds poorly to pyridostigmine, and why complement inhibitors are less effective in this group
- The thymus is typically normal in MuSK MG

---

## LRP4 Antibodies

**Low-Density Lipoprotein Receptor-Related Protein 4 (LRP4)** is the receptor that Agrin binds to in order to activate MuSK. Antibodies to LRP4 disrupt the same signalling pathway but at the upstream step.

- LRP4 antibodies are primarily IgG1 and IgG2 subtypes and appear not to strongly activate complement
- Present in approximately 1–3% of MG cases
- Clinical presentation is often milder and resembles early-onset MG
- Can also be found alongside AChR or MuSK antibodies, and their presence may worsen disease severity

---

## The Role of the Thymus Gland

The **thymus** is a small gland in the chest, behind the breastbone, that plays a central role in training the immune system during childhood and early adult life. It is here that T-cells — a key type of immune cell — learn to distinguish between the body's own proteins ("self") and foreign invaders ("non-self"). T-cells that attack the body's own tissues are normally eliminated in the thymus.

In **AChR-positive MG**, the thymus appears to be the site where this process breaks down:

- The thymus contains unusual "myoid cells" that express AChR-like proteins on their surface
- In some people (possibly triggered by a viral infection or other environmental factor), the thymus develops an inflammatory environment in which these AChR-bearing cells are presented to T-cells in a way that breaks self-tolerance
- The result is that T-cells that recognise AChR as a target survive and are activated, rather than being eliminated
- These T-cells then help B-cells (another type of immune cell) mature into plasma cells that produce large quantities of anti-AChR antibodies

Approximately **75% of people with AChR-positive MG** have thymic abnormalities:
- **Thymic hyperplasia** (an overactive thymus with active antibody-producing germinal centres) in approximately 65% of cases — most common in early-onset MG
- **Thymoma** (a tumour of the thymus) in approximately 10–15% of cases

**Recent research (2024–2025) has refined this picture further.** 

- **TSLP and Treg Maturation**: **Medullary Thymic Epithelial Cells (mTECs)** — the cells responsible for educating T-cells and regulatory cells during their development — significantly overproduce a signalling molecule called **TSLP (Thymic Stromal Lymphopoietin)** in the MG thymus. Excess TSLP interferes with the maturation of regulatory T-cells (Tregs — see below), producing immune cells that are functionally defective and inherently unstable before they ever leave the gland. This helps explain why thymectomy can provide benefit that persists for decades after surgery.
- **STING and Negative Selection (2025)**: Breakthrough research has identified that chronic **overactivity of the STING protein** (Stimulator of Interferon Genes) within the thymic epithelium directly impairs the "negative selection" process. This allows self-reactive T-cells, which would normally be destroyed in the thymus, to escape into the bloodstream. This "false alarm" signaling creates a pro-inflammatory environment that bypasses the immune system's primary checkpoint for preventing autoimmunity.

Once the autoimmune process is established, removing the thymus (thymectomy) helps many people but does not always resolve MG — because autoreactive T-cells and B-cells have already spread beyond the thymus and continue to produce antibodies.

In **MuSK-positive MG**, the thymus is typically normal, which explains why thymectomy is less clearly beneficial in this group.

## T-Cells and Regulatory T-Cells: The Root of the Problem

While the anti-AChR antibodies are what cause the muscle weakness in MG, the latest research is increasingly clear that MG is fundamentally a **T-cell regulatory failure** — the antibodies are a symptom of a deeper breakdown in the immune system's self-restraint mechanisms.

### T-Helper Cells: The Conductors of the Attack

**CD4+ T helper cells** are essential to the MG attack. They provide the signals that instruct B-cells to produce anti-AChR antibodies. Without ongoing T-cell help, B-cells cannot sustain high-affinity antibody production. This is why treatments targeting B-cells (such as rituximab) often work — but also why their effect is temporary: the underlying T-cell dysfunction survives and eventually re-educates new B-cells to repeat the attack.

Two subsets of T helper cells are particularly implicated in MG:
- **Th1 cells** — drive direct cellular autoimmune attacks
- **Th17 cells** — promote antibody production and tissue inflammation; an elevated ratio of Th17 cells relative to regulatory T-cells is now recognised as a reliable indicator of active MG disease

### CD8+ T-Cells at the Junction

**CD8+ cytotoxic T-cells** (which normally kill infected cells) have been found infiltrating both thymic tissue and the neuromuscular junction itself in MG. They may directly damage the junction and amplify the autoimmune response alongside CD4+ cells.

In 2025, researchers identified **QRICH1** (Glutamine-rich protein 1) as a critical "intracellular checkpoint" or molecular "brake" on CD8+ T-cells. A study published in **Science Immunology** (2025) revealed that QRICH1 is recruited to the **CARD11 signalosome** immediately following T-cell receptor engagement. Once bound, it inhibits the signalling pathways (**NF-κB, JNK, and mTOR**) that normally drive T-cell proliferation and the production of inflammatory cytokines. When this protein is deficient or overridden, CD8+ T-cells become hyper-responsive and destroy healthy tissue.

### Metabolic Overdrive in Autoimmune T-Cells

A 2025 study (Nature Communications) identified that overactive T-cells in autoimmune conditions are driven by a mitochondrial protein called **ABHD11** (Alpha/Beta Hydrolase Domain 11). ABHD11 acts as a **noncanonical deglutarylating enzyme** that selectively removes glutaryl adducts from **lipoate** — a vital fatty acid cofactor required for the TCA cycle (specifically for the enzyme α-KGDH).

This protein keeps T-cells in a state of permanent high energy — like a car engine that never drops below full throttle. This "metabolic rewiring" prevents the T-cells from entering the resting states that would normally end an immune response. ABHD11 inhibitors are now being developed as a way to "reprogram" these cells back toward a resting or memory state.

### Regulatory T-Cells (Tregs): The Peacekeepers That Fail

**Regulatory T-cells (Tregs)** are a specialised subset of T-cells whose job is to keep the rest of the immune system in check — they suppress other immune cells that might otherwise attack healthy tissue. They are the immune system's peacekeepers, and their identity depends on a protein called **FOXP3** (the discovery of which was recognised with the 2025 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine).

A key mechanism discovered in 2025 keeps FOXP3 stable: a protein called **Egr-1** actively maintains FOXP3 expression in healthy conditions. When Egr-1 is disrupted — by inflammation, physiological stress, or specific molecular pathways — FOXP3 becomes unstable and the Treg begins to lose its suppressive identity. This provides a molecular explanation for why MG patients often flare during periods of stress: stress hormones (cortisol and adrenaline) suppress Egr-1 and disrupt the chemical "tagging" of FOXP3, causing a transient collapse of Treg function precisely when the immune brakes are most needed. Natural compounds that activate Egr-1 — such as **Calycosin**, a plant flavonoid — are now being explored as potential Treg-stabilising agents.

In healthy people, Tregs create a state of "infectious tolerance," actively encouraging other immune cells toward restraint. In MG, Treg function is severely compromised:

- Multiple studies confirm that people with MG often have **near-normal numbers** of Tregs in their blood — but their ability to actually suppress the autoimmune attack is severely impaired
- A 2024 study identified a specific molecular pathway responsible: Tregs in autoimmune conditions overexpress a protein called **PRDM1-S**, which triggers an enzyme (SGK-1) that directly destabilises FOXP3. The result is that regulatory T-cells "flip" from protective cells into **pro-inflammatory cells** — actively worsening the disease they were meant to prevent
- **The EPO Immune Switch (2025)**: A landmark **Stanford University** study discovered that **Erythropoietin (EPO)** — previously known only for red blood cell production — is actually a master "on-off switch" for immune tolerance. EPO signals through receptors on a specific type of immune cell called **type 1 conventional dendritic cells (cDC1s)**, which then instruct the immune system to produce stable, functional Tregs. When this EPO signaling pathway is disrupted, dendritic cells instead transform into "super-stimulators" that drive autoimmune attacks. This "missing link" discovery was recognized with the 2025 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine (awarded for the discovery of FOXP3 and the mechanisms of Treg development).
- A separate finding (the **GRAIL/RNF128 protein**) showed that Tregs lose their suppressive identity in inflammatory environments — and the MG thymus is exactly such an environment, creating a self-sustaining cycle where inflammation destroys the very cells meant to stop it
- The elevated TSLP in the MG thymus (produced by mTECs) impairs the development of Tregs from the very beginning, causing a **functional defect** that renders them less capable of suppressing autoimmune responses. Experimental use of neutralizing anti-TSLP antibodies has been shown to partially restore the suppressive capacity of these MG-derived Tregs.

The practical implication: simply boosting the *number* of Tregs is not enough. Restoring their *quality and stability* is essential — and this is now a major focus of treatment research.

## Genetic Factors

MG is **not directly inherited** — it does not follow simple Mendelian genetics — but genetic susceptibility plays a role. Specific variants in genes encoding **Human Leukocyte Antigens (HLA)** — proteins that help the immune system recognise self from non-self — are associated with different forms of MG:

| MG Subtype | HLA Association |
|---|---|
| Early-onset AChR+ MG | HLA-B8-DR3-DQ2 haplotype (AH8.1) |
| Late-onset AChR+ MG | HLA-DRB1*15:01 |
| MuSK MG | HLA-DQB1*05:02 / HLA-DRB1*14 |

Other genes associated with MG susceptibility include variants in S100P, GAB2, NFKBIA, TNFAIP3, PPP1R15A (early-onset), and CTLA-4, PTPN22, and TNFRSF11 (late-onset). These genes play roles in T-cell tolerance and immune regulation.

Having a close family member with MG or another autoimmune condition slightly increases risk, but the absolute lifetime risk remains low. Twin and family studies confirm an elevated prevalence of MG and other autoimmune disorders within families.

## Triggers and Exacerbating Factors

Even with an underlying predisposition, specific factors often trigger the onset of MG or cause existing symptoms to worsen.

### Infections

Viral infections — particularly **Epstein-Barr virus (EBV)** — have been studied as potential triggers. The proposed mechanism is **molecular mimicry**: part of the virus closely resembles part of the AChR protein, so an immune response mounted against the virus inadvertently begins attacking the body's own receptors. Bioinformatic studies from 2023–2024 have added new weight to this theory, identifying specific short sequences in several pathogens (including certain herpes viruses and bacteria) that closely resemble portions of the AChR — and confirming that T-cell receptors raised against these pathogen sequences can indeed recognise AChR-derived proteins.

More generally, **any significant infection** can worsen existing MG, even if it did not cause it — particularly respiratory infections, which are both common and particularly hazardous in MG.

### Medications

A range of commonly used medications can unmask latent MG or cause significant deterioration in someone already diagnosed. Key categories include:

- **Certain antibiotics**: aminoglycosides (gentamicin, tobramycin), fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin), macrolides, and especially **telithromycin (Ketek)** — which must be avoided absolutely and has caused deaths in people with MG
- **Beta-blockers** (e.g., propranolol, atenolol)
- **Calcium channel blockers**
- **Antiarrhythmics**: quinidine, procainamide
- **Magnesium** (including in antacids and laxatives)
- **Checkpoint inhibitor cancer immunotherapies** (which can trigger new-onset MG)
- **Penicillamine** (used in rheumatoid arthritis) — can trigger MG-like disease
- Some **anaesthetic agents** and muscle relaxants

Always ensure all healthcare providers know about your MG diagnosis before any new medication is started. See [Drugs to Avoid](drugs-to-avoid.md) for more detail.

### Hormonal Factors

Hormonal fluctuations — particularly involving oestrogen and progesterone — can influence MG activity. **Pregnancy** is particularly unpredictable:
- Symptoms may improve, remain stable, or significantly worsen
- The first trimester and the **postpartum period** carry the highest risk of deterioration
- Careful specialist monitoring throughout pregnancy is essential
- Neonatal MG (temporary transmission of maternal antibodies to the baby) affects approximately 10–15% of babies born to mothers with MG

### Physical and Emotional Stress

Surgery, trauma, extreme heat, and significant emotional stress can all precipitate a myasthenic crisis in susceptible individuals. Research published in 2024 has now identified the molecular mechanism behind this: stress hormones (particularly cortisol and adrenaline) temporarily destabilise the **FOXP3** methylation patterns that keep regulatory T-cells stable, causing a transient drop in suppressive capacity precisely when inflammatory signals are at their highest. This is not a psychological phenomenon — it acts directly on the molecular machinery of immune regulation. Managing these factors is an important part of living well with MG. See [Living with MG](living-with-mg.md) for practical advice.

### The EPO-STING Axis: The "Brake and Accelerator" (2025)

Research is identifying how specific molecular signals integrate to maintain or break immune tolerance. A 2025 **Stanford University** study identified **type 1 conventional dendritic cells (cDC1s)** as the critical cellular "node" where tolerance is decided through two opposing signals:

- **The Brake (EPO)** — **Erythropoietin (EPO)** — previously known only for red blood cell production — acts as a master "on-off switch" for immune tolerance. EPO signaling in **cDC1 dendritic cells** is required to activate and stabilize Regulatory T-cells (Tregs). When this "brake" is applied, it instructs the immune system to tolerate self-proteins like the AChR.
- **The Accelerator (STING)** — The **STING protein** (Stimulator of Interferon Genes) acts as the inflammatory accelerator. When STING is overactive (due to cellular stress or chronic inflammation), it misidentifies ordinary cellular debris as a viral threat, triggering a "false alarm" cascade of inflammatory signals. In the thymus, this overactivity also impairs the "negative selection" process that normally destroys self-reactive T-cells.
- **Signal Integration** — In MG, the "STING accelerator" is stuck in the on position, and the "EPO brake" is failing. This causes cDC1 cells to transform from "peacekeepers" into "super-stimulators" that drive autoimmune attacks instead of suppressing them. This discovery was a key part of the research recognized with the **2025 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine**.
- **Efferocytosis failure** — When cells die, the body normally clears the debris rapidly. When this clean-up is impaired, self-proteins linger in tissues far longer than normal, eventually overwhelming the immune system's tolerance mechanisms. This is particularly relevant at sites of tissue turnover such as the neuromuscular junction.

## Why Does the Immune System Do This?

This is the central question in MG. Our understanding has deepened considerably in recent years. The picture that is emerging is:

- There is a **genetic predisposition** — particularly in HLA genes that affect which proteins T-cells react to — that increases vulnerability
- An **environmental trigger** (possibly a viral infection, or another physiological stress) initiates the breakdown in immune tolerance, likely by exposing hidden portions of the AChR to T-cells in the abnormal thymic environment
- The thymus in AChR-positive MG overproduces TSLP, producing dysfunctional Tregs that cannot maintain self-tolerance; this appears to be the origin of the disease in most early-onset cases
- Once tolerance is broken, a self-sustaining cycle is established: the inflammatory environment destroys the regulatory cells meant to stop it, enabling the autoimmune attack to continue
- The underlying problem is a **T-cell regulatory failure** — the antibodies that cause muscle weakness are a downstream consequence

Research is now focused on interrupting this cycle at specific molecular points — restoring Treg function, stabilising FOXP3, targeting metabolically overactive T-cells, or inducing tolerance to the AChR directly. See [Research and the Future](research-future.md) and [Emerging Treatments](treatments-emerging.md) for what is in development.
