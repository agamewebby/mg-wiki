# Research and the Future of MG

MG research has never been more active or more promising. In the past decade alone, the field has moved from broad immunosuppression to multiple approved targeted biological therapies. The next decade may see approaches that were once science fiction — CAR-T cell therapy, gene therapy, tolerance induction — become clinical reality.

---

## How We Got Here — A Brief History

Research into MG has transformed understanding of the disease over the past 50 years:

- **1970s**: MDA-funded researchers established the autoimmune nature of MG, demonstrating that people with MG have a reduced number of acetylcholine receptors and that antibodies targeting these receptors can induce MG-like symptoms in animals. These discoveries laid the foundation for immunosuppressant treatment
- **1970s–1980s**: Plasmapheresis was pioneered as a treatment for MG, introducing the concept of physically removing harmful antibodies
- **2000s**: MuSK antibodies were identified as a cause of seronegative MG, recognising MG as a family of distinct conditions
- **2010s**: MGTX trial proved the long-term benefit of thymectomy; cell-based assays improved diagnosis; eculizumab became the first complement inhibitor approved for MG
- **2020s**: Explosion of approved biological therapies — efgartigimod, rozanolixizumab, zilucoplan, nipocalimab, inebilizumab — and early CAR-T results showing remarkable promise in refractory disease

## Current Priority Research Areas

### Biomarkers

A major unmet need in MG is the lack of reliable biomarkers that can:
- **Predict which patients will respond to which treatments** — currently, patients often have to try multiple treatments before finding what works, at considerable cost in both financial terms and quality of life
- **Monitor disease activity** more precisely than antibody titres (which correlate poorly with clinical severity)
- **Detect MG earlier** — particularly in seronegative patients
- **Predict who will develop a crisis** before it happens

Studies of genetic polymorphisms, metabolomics, and miRNA profiling have identified some treatment-predictive markers. For example:
- **miRNA-150-5p and miRNA-21-5p**: Elevated in MG and decrease with successful immunotherapy; miRNA-150-5p is specifically reduced by thymectomy, making it a potential objective marker of surgical outcome — useful since clinical response to thymectomy can take months or years to emerge
- **miRNA-30e-5p**: A predictor of **ocular-to-generalised MG progression** — elevated levels in patients presenting with only eye and eyelid symptoms may indicate higher risk of spread to limbs, breathing, or swallowing, allowing earlier preventive treatment
- **miRNA-19b-5p**: Found elevated specifically in MG thymic tissue, acting as a regulator of the TSLP overproduction axis. Particularly pronounced in thymoma-associated MG, connecting the microRNA biomarker field to the thymic dysfunction research

**New immune cell biomarkers (2024–2025):**
- The **Th17/Treg ratio** (the balance between inflammatory and regulatory T-cells) is emerging as a reliable indicator of active disease and may help guide treatment decisions
- **Memory B-cell counts** — combined with low circulating Treg numbers — appear to identify patients unlikely to respond to conventional immunosuppression, pointing toward earlier use of more aggressive "reset" therapies such as CAR-T

Further validation of these and other biomarkers is needed before they can guide clinical practice.

### Understanding the Immune Cell Biology

Research is refining understanding of which immune cells drive antibody production in different MG subtypes:

- In **MuSK MG**: pathogenic antibodies appear to be produced mainly by short-lived plasmablasts — explaining the excellent response to rituximab (which depletes CD20-positive B-cells) and potentially to FcRn inhibitors
- In **AChR MG**: long-lived plasma cells (which have lost CD20 expression) also contribute — explaining the more partial response to rituximab and supporting the development of agents targeting CD19 or plasma cells directly
- Understanding these differences will enable better-targeted treatment selection

**Regulatory T-cell (Treg) biology** has also advanced significantly in 2024–2025. Research has identified specific molecular pathways (PRDM1-S/SGK-1) by which Tregs in autoimmune disease lose their protective function and convert into inflammatory cells. Landmark discoveries in 2025 have provided the "missing links" for how the immune system normally maintains tolerance:

- **The EPO-STING "Master Switch" (2025)**: Landmark research from **Stanford Medicine (published in Nature and Science)** identified a fundamental "Brake and Accelerator" mechanism for immune tolerance. The study discovered that **type 1 conventional dendritic cells (cDC1s)** act as the critical integration node for these signals. **Erythropoietin (EPO)** acts as the brake, inducing a tolerogenic state that activates and stabilizes Tregs, while the **STING protein** acts as the accelerator of inflammatory responses. This discovery, revealing how the body normally "re-teaches" itself tolerance, was recognized with the **2025 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine** (awarded for the mechanisms of Treg development and the discovery of the EPO-STING axis).
- **TSLP and Thymic Education**: Research published in 2024–2025 identified that **medullary thymic epithelial cells (mTECs)** in the MG thymus overproduce **TSLP (Thymic Stromal Lymphopoietin)**. This cytokine excess causes a **functional defect** in developing Tregs, making them inherently less suppressive. Neutralizing TSLP has been shown to partially restore Treg function, making TSLP blockade a major new therapeutic target.
- **Intracellular Checkpoints**: The identification of **QRICH1** as a CARD11-binding intracellular checkpoint (**Science Immunology, 2025**) and **ABHD11** as a mitochondrial deglutarylating enzyme (**Nature Communications, 2025**) represent significant advances in metabolic and checkpoint targeting.

Understanding these differences will enable better-targeted treatment selection. See [Causes](causes.md) for more on the T-cell biology underlying MG.

### Antigen-Specific Tolerance Induction

One of the holy grails of MG research: re-teaching the immune system to tolerate the acetylcholine receptor — correcting the fundamental immune recognition error rather than broadly suppressing immunity.

- Administration of AChR or AChR-derived peptides (orally, nasally, subcutaneously, or by injection) has shown promise in animal models for decades
- A pilot human investigation of an AChR peptide mimic has been completed with a good safety profile (NCT02609022)
- An antigen-specific approach using engineered T-cells expressing a MuSK chimeric antibody receptor is in early Phase 1 evaluation, aimed at selectively eliminating only the B-cells producing MuSK antibodies
- These approaches would potentially allow treatment without broad immunosuppression — with less infection risk and fewer side effects

### Optimising the Neuromuscular Junction Itself

Some research focuses on enhancing the function of the NMJ even when it is under immune attack — by strengthening the junction's architecture or its response to acetylcholine, independent of immune modulation.

### Physiology of the NMJ

Understanding the fundamental processes of NMJ formation and function — using new tools such as functional in vitro NMJs generated from human stem cells — is revealing potential new therapeutic targets and providing platforms for drug screening.

## Clinical Trials — Getting Involved

Clinical trials are the pathway by which new treatments reach patients. Participating in a clinical trial:
- May give you access to treatments not yet commercially available
- Contributes to knowledge that benefits the wider MG community
- Is always voluntary and you retain the right to withdraw

To find clinical trials for MG:
- **ClinicalTrials.gov** (clinicaltrials.gov) — search for "myasthenia gravis" in the condition field
- **UK Clinical Trials Gateway** (ukctg.nihr.ac.uk) — for UK-based trials
- **MDA's Clinical Trial Finder** (mda.org/research/clinical-trials)
- Ask your neurologist whether you might be eligible for any current trials

Trials need participants of all ages, sexes, ethnicities, and disease stages. Your involvement helps make sure that results are applicable to everyone with MG.

- **NINDS / NIH (USA)**: National Institute of Neurological Disorders and Stroke; major funder of MG research; the MGNet Rare Disease Clinical Research Network coordinates multi-site research

- **Myaware (UK)**: UK's dedicated MG charity; supports research and funds patient-focused work

- **George Washington University, Department of Neurology**: Leading academic research centre, home of key researchers including Henry Kaminski and colleagues (authors of the 2024 JCI review "MG: the future is here")

## The Broader Vision

The consensus among MG researchers is cautiously optimistic. Key goals for the next decade include:

1. **Personalised medicine** — selecting the right treatment for the right patient based on biomarkers, genetics, and antibody profile, rather than through trial and error
2. **Disease modification** — treatments that alter the long-term course of the disease, not just control symptoms
3. **Remission induction** — achieving complete, drug-free remission in a higher proportion of patients
4. **Safer treatment** — reducing the side effect burden of long-term immunosuppression through more targeted approaches
5. **Universal access** — ensuring that newer, effective treatments are accessible to all patients regardless of geography or financial means
6. **Cure** — ultimately, through antigen-specific tolerance induction or gene therapy, correcting the fundamental immune error

As one research review (Kaminski et al., JCI, 2024) noted: despite the challenges of a rare disease, rare disease drug incentives and improved understanding of pathophysiology have created a "golden age" of MG clinical trials. The future is genuinely here.
