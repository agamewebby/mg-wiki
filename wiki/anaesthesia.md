# Anaesthesia and Myasthenia Gravis

If you have myasthenia gravis and need an operation — planned or emergency — you deserve to go into theatre feeling informed and prepared. The good news is that anaesthesia is generally safe for people with MG when the team is prepared and your condition is well managed beforehand. This page explains what you and your anaesthetist need to know.

---

## Talk to Your Anaesthetist in Advance

For any planned procedure, request a pre-operative discussion with the anaesthetist as early as possible. This conversation matters because it allows the team to:

- Assess how well controlled your MG is and your overall fitness for anaesthesia
- Choose the safest type of anaesthetic for your procedure
- Arrange an intensive care bed for post-operative monitoring if needed
- Plan your medication management around the operation

Arriving prepared — knowing your medications, your MG subtype, and your recent symptom history — gives both you and the team confidence. That confidence genuinely helps.

---

## Types of Anaesthetic

### Local and Regional Anaesthesia (Preferred Where Possible)

For many operations, local or regional anaesthesia is preferred for people with MG because it avoids the breathing risks associated with some general anaesthetics and allows your normal MG medications to continue.

Common agents used include **lignocaine**, **bupivacaine**, and **mepivacaine** (which is shorter-acting with fewer side effects). These drugs are injected near nerves or nerve roots to block sensation completely for several hours.

Local anaesthesia combined with light sedation is suitable for most operations below the waist — including hip and knee surgery, varicose veins, hernias, and some gynaecological procedures.

**Spinal and epidural anaesthetics** work on the same principle and are often an excellent choice for MG patients having lower-body surgery.

### General Anaesthesia

General anaesthesia is safe for people with MG when managed carefully. The key is ensuring your MG is as well controlled as possible beforehand. If your symptoms are poorly controlled, your specialist may recommend a course of [plasmapheresis or IVIG](treatments-acute.md) approximately two weeks before the procedure to stabilise you.

## Key Drug Considerations

### Muscle Relaxants

This is the most important area for your anaesthetic team to understand.

People with MG have **reduced acetylcholine receptors** at the neuromuscular junction, which means they react very differently to muscle relaxants than most patients:

**Non-depolarising muscle relaxants** (competitive blockers) — such as **atracurium**, **rocuronium**, and **vecuronium** — are used to relax voluntary muscles during deep operations. Because MG patients already have compromised neuromuscular transmission, they are **far more sensitive** to these drugs. The dose typically needs to be reduced by **5 to 10 times** the standard amount.

**Depolarising agents** (such as **suxamethonium**) work differently — they stimulate muscles before paralysing them. MG patients are actually about **2-fold less sensitive** to these agents, because they have fewer receptors for the drug to act on.

The degree of muscle paralysis can be monitored throughout the operation using a **peripheral nerve stimulator** — a simple, inexpensive device that is standard in all operating theatres.

At the end of the operation, **neostigmine** (a short-acting cousin of pyridostigmine) is routinely used to reverse the effects of muscle relaxants.

> If your MG has not been diagnosed before surgery and standard doses of muscle relaxants are given, you may need higher than expected doses of neostigmine to recover — this has occasionally been how undiagnosed MG comes to light.

### Pyridostigmine

**Do not stop pyridostigmine before surgery** — whether local or general anaesthetic. Because pyridostigmine counteracts muscle relaxants, the anaesthetist may need to adjust the relaxant dose slightly (using the nerve stimulator to guide this). This is manageable and well understood.

After the operation, if you are having difficulty swallowing, pyridostigmine can be given through a nasogastric tube (a thin tube into the stomach) until normal swallowing returns.

### Steroids

If you take steroids as part of your MG treatment, your anaesthetist must know. **Do not stop steroids before surgery.**

The body normally produces extra cortisol (a natural steroid) in response to the physical stress of surgery. Long-term steroid treatment suppresses this natural response. To compensate, you will typically be given **additional steroids by injection** before, during, and after the operation — this is called "steroid cover" and is standard practice.

## Pre-operative Optimisation

Before any significant surgery, your MG should be as well controlled as possible:

- **Medication review** — ensure your current regimen is optimised
- **Plasmapheresis or IVIG** — may be recommended approximately 2 weeks before surgery to reduce antibody levels and improve muscle strength going into the operation (see [Acute Treatments](treatments-acute.md))
- **Respiratory assessment** — if you have any bulbar or respiratory weakness, a breathing assessment before surgery is important
- **Arrange post-operative monitoring** — an intensive care or high-dependency bed may be arranged as a precaution, even if not ultimately needed

---

## Emergency Surgery

If you need emergency surgery and have MG, make sure the surgical team is aware of your diagnosis immediately. If you carry a medical alert card or wear a medical alert bracelet, it will help ensure this information reaches the anaesthetic team quickly.

If possible, contact your MG specialist or neurologist — even by phone — so they can advise the surgical team directly.

---

## After the Operation

Recovery from anaesthesia can take longer in people with MG, and temporary worsening of MG symptoms in the post-operative period is possible. This is usually short-lived.

Signs to watch for after surgery:
- Increased difficulty swallowing
- Increased weakness, particularly in the face or eyelids
- Breathing difficulties

Any significant worsening should be reported promptly to the medical team. See [Myasthenic Crisis](myasthenic-crisis.md) for more on recognising and responding to serious worsening.

---

## What to Tell Every Healthcare Team

Before any procedure involving anaesthesia or sedation — including dental treatment under sedation — always tell the team:

- That you have myasthenia gravis
- Which medications you take (especially pyridostigmine and any steroids)
- Your MG specialist's name and contact details
