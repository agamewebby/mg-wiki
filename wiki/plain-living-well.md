# Living Well with MG — Everyday Tips

*A plain-English guide — no medical background needed.*

*Looking for a simpler explanation? You are already on the plain-English page.*
*For more detail, see [Diet and Lifestyle](diet-lifestyle.md), [Living with MG](living-with-mg.md), and [Complementary Therapies](complementary-therapies.md)*

---

MG is a condition you live with every day. Medication is important — but so is how you live, eat, rest, and manage stress. This page gives you practical, everyday advice on what makes a real difference.

---

## Rest is Not Giving Up

This is the single most important thing to understand.

**The more you rest, the more you can do.** In MG, muscles weaken as you use them. Rest allows them to recover. Rest is not laziness — it is medicine.

Practical ways to rest well:
- Plan short breaks of 10–20 minutes between activities. Even lying down briefly restores strength.
- Do not push through severe weakness. Rest before you reach exhaustion — not after.
- Good days are for living well, not for "catching up." Overdoing it on a good day leads to a worse next day.

---

## Work with Your Best Times

Most people with MG are stronger in the morning, after a night's sleep. After taking pyridostigmine (Mestinon — the main MG tablet), there is a window of 1–3 hours where you typically feel stronger.

Use this strategically:
- Book important appointments, phone calls, and demanding activities for mid-morning.
- Leave lighter tasks for the afternoon.
- Know your own pattern — keep a simple diary to track when you feel best.

## Eat Well — and Eat Gently

There is no magic MG diet, but eating well makes a real difference.

**If chewing or swallowing is difficult:**
- Eat little and often — smaller meals are less tiring than large ones.
- Choose soft foods: yoghurt, mashed potato, scrambled eggs, soups, smoothies, well-cooked vegetables.
- Take your pyridostigmine 30–45 minutes before meals — this times the medication effect with mealtimes.
- Sit upright when eating and for 30 minutes afterwards.
- If swallowing is a significant problem, tell your neurologist — a specialist can help.

**Anti-inflammatory eating — simple version:**
Eating more oily fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel), colourful vegetables, olive oil, nuts, and legumes — and less processed food, sugar, and fried food — reduces the background inflammation that drives MG. You do not need to follow a strict diet; just a general shift in this direction helps.

**Gut health matters:**
A healthy gut is good for your immune system. Plain live yoghurt, fermented foods (like kefir, sauerkraut), and eating a wide variety of vegetables and high-fibre foods all support the beneficial bacteria in your gut — which in turn help your immune system work better. A clinical trial at Stanford University found that people eating fermented foods daily reduced 19 different inflammation-causing proteins in their blood. It is a simple, practical change with real evidence behind it.

**Have you heard of the Wahls Protocol?**
The Wahls Protocol is a structured dietary approach — originally developed for multiple sclerosis (a condition that shares some immune mechanisms with MG) — based on eating large amounts of specific vegetables, removing gluten and dairy, and supporting gut health. Some people with MG have explored it. It has some clinical trial evidence in MS. There are no published MG trials yet, but the underlying principles (reducing inflammation, supporting gut bacteria, and immune balance) are relevant.

The simplest version involves eating 9 cups of vegetables and fruit per day across three groups: dark leafy greens, sulphur-rich vegetables (broccoli, cabbage, garlic), and brightly coloured vegetables. This is a significant increase in plant variety for most people — and on its own this shift is likely beneficial regardless of the full protocol. If you are interested, discuss it with your neurologist and a dietitian.

**What about gluten-free or dairy-free diets?**
For most people with MG, eliminating gluten or dairy is not proven to help. If you have coeliac disease (gluten sensitivity) as well as MG, a gluten-free diet may reduce your overall immune activation and secondarily benefit your MG. For everyone else, there is no strong evidence for these restrictions — and avoiding dairy can actually be harmful if you are on steroids, because dairy is an important source of calcium for your bones.

## Keep Cool

Heat makes MG worse. This one is important.

**Hot weather, hot baths, fever, very warm rooms** — all of these can cause a rapid temporary worsening of your symptoms. The reason is that the enzyme that helps nerve signals reach muscles works less efficiently when it is warm.

Practical tips:
- Shower or bathe with cooler water.
- In hot weather: use fans, close curtains during the hottest part of the day, stay hydrated (aim for 2 litres of water a day).
- Apply cool packs or a cool flannel gently to closed eyelids to ease drooping in the heat.
- If you feel your symptoms worsening in the heat — rest and cool down before doing anything else.
- Plan activities for the cooler parts of the day.

## Stress and Your Immune System

Stress worsens MG. This is not "in your head" — it is biology.

Stress causes your body to release a hormone called **cortisol**. Cortisol suppresses the part of the immune system that should be keeping the MG attack in check (regulatory T-cells — the immune system's "off switch"). When cortisol goes up, MG activity tends to follow.

Managing stress is therefore not just about feeling better — it directly affects your MG.

### Mindfulness and Meditation

You do not need to be spiritual or experienced to benefit. Even 10–15 minutes of quiet breathing practice per day has been shown in multiple clinical trials to reduce inflammation and lower stress hormones in people with autoimmune conditions.

Free apps like Headspace, Calm, or Insight Timer offer guided sessions. Start with 5 minutes. It builds up.

### Breathing Exercises

Slow, deliberate breathing activates the part of your nervous system that calms the body down (the parasympathetic system — your "rest and digest" mode). Breathe in for 4 counts, hold for 1, out for 6 counts. Do this for 5–10 minutes. Cortisol drops, muscle tension eases.

### Tai Chi

Tai chi is gentle, slow movement combined with breathing and mindfulness. It is particularly well suited to MG because:
- It does not exhaust muscles — the movements are slow and controlled.
- It can be done seated during a flare.
- It has evidence behind it for improving balance, fatigue, and quality of life in chronic conditions.
- It addresses stress and physical movement at the same time.

Look for beginner classes in your local area, or follow free online videos.

### Yoga — with Modifications

Gentle yoga (especially Restorative or Yin yoga) can help with both physical wellbeing and stress. Important guidance for MG:
- Avoid hot yoga (Bikram yoga) — heat will worsen your symptoms.
- Tell your teacher about your MG — a good teacher will adapt poses.
- Focus on breathing practices (these are safe and beneficial in MG).
- Restorative poses using props and bolsters are excellent — they give the body support while allowing deep rest.

## Complementary Approaches — What Has Evidence?

"Complementary" means used **alongside** conventional medicine, not instead of it. Some complementary approaches have genuine research behind them in MG.

**What the research shows:**

- **Acupuncture:** A review of 14 clinical trials (2024) found significant improvements in MG symptoms when used alongside conventional medication. It appears to work partly by activating a nerve pathway (the vagus nerve) that has a calming effect on the immune system.

- **Astragalus-based herbal preparations:** A review of 19 clinical trials involving over 1,200 people (2022) found better outcomes when these were combined with conventional treatment. Astragalus appears to support the production of regulatory T-cells (the immune "off switch").

- **Ashwagandha:** Multiple clinical trials confirm this Ayurvedic herb reduces stress hormones (cortisol) by 15–32% compared to placebo. Lower cortisol = better Treg function = potentially less MG activity. Generally safe, but monitor alongside pyridostigmine (mild cholinergic effects at high doses).

**Very important:** Always tell your neurologist about any herbal supplement or complementary treatment before starting. Some herbs are dangerous in MG:
- **Huperzine A** (in "brain health" supplements) — must never be combined with pyridostigmine. Risk of serious toxicity.
- **High-dose magnesium supplements** — block nerve signals to muscles. Contraindicated in MG.
- **St John's Wort** — interferes with immunosuppressant drugs (reduces their effectiveness).

See [Complementary Therapies](complementary-therapies.md) and [Drugs to Avoid](drugs-to-avoid.md) for more detail.

## Exercise — Gentle and Consistent

When MG is well controlled, gentle, regular exercise is good for you.

- **Start slow** — short walks, 10–15 minutes, gradually building.
- **Swimming** is excellent (low impact, supports body weight) — just avoid hot pools.
- **Tai chi and gentle yoga** as above.
- **Listen to your body** — if symptoms worsen during exercise, stop and rest.

When MG is not well controlled or during a flare, reduce activity and focus on rest.

---

## Sleep

Muscles recover during sleep. Good sleep is physically therapeutic in MG.

If you find it harder to breathe when lying flat, mention this to your neurologist — it can be a sign that breathing muscles need attention. Extra pillows or an adjustable bed can help.

Short naps (20–30 minutes) during the day can genuinely restore strength.

## You Are Not Alone

MG is rare enough that most people around you will not understand it. That can feel isolating. But there is a community of people who do understand.

- **Myaware** (UK): www.myaware.org — support, information, forums, and welfare advice
- Online communities and social media groups connect you with others who live with MG every day
- Peer support — talking to someone who has navigated what you are navigating — is powerful

Sharing how you feel, accepting help when it is offered, and building a good support network around you makes an enormous difference.
