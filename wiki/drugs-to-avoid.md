# Drugs to Avoid in MG

People with myasthenia gravis need to be careful about certain medications. More than 30 drugs in common use can interfere with nerve-to-muscle transmission — and in MG, where the safety margin at the neuromuscular junction is already reduced, even a small further dip can make things significantly worse, or trigger a myasthenic crisis.

> **Importantly:** drug reactions are specific to the individual. Not everyone with MG will react to every drug on this list. The key principle is **caution, communication, and monitoring**.

---

## The One Drug That MUST Be Avoided Absolutely

> **Telithromycin (brand name: Ketek)** — a ketolide antibiotic — **must never be used in myasthenia gravis. It has caused deaths in people with MG.** This is the only drug that is absolutely contraindicated in all forms of MG.

If a doctor or dentist proposes prescribing Ketek (telithromycin), you must inform them of your MG diagnosis immediately and decline this antibiotic.

---

## How Drugs Can Worsen MG

Nature has made the neuromuscular junction remarkably resilient. Under normal circumstances, even if a drug reduces transmission by up to 50%, there is still enough reserve to prevent significant weakness.

In MG, this reserve is already depleted. The number of functioning acetylcholine receptors is already reduced. A drug that only slightly further impairs transmission may be enough to cause significant worsening — or trigger a crisis.

---

## Drug Categories to Be Aware Of

### Antibiotics

Some antibiotics interfere with neuromuscular transmission:

- **Aminoglycosides** (gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin, neomycin) — significant risk; to be used only when essential and with close monitoring
- **Fluoroquinolones** (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, moxifloxacin) — should be used with caution; commonly prescribed so this is important to know
- **Macrolides** (azithromycin, erythromycin, clarithromycin) — use with caution
- **Telithromycin (Ketek)** — absolutely contraindicated; must never be used

**Safe alternatives:** Many antibiotic classes are generally safe in MG, including penicillins and cephalosporins (for most indications). Always check with your MG team if unsure about a specific antibiotic.

### Beta-Blockers

Beta-blockers (e.g., propranolol, atenolol, metoprolol, bisoprolol) can worsen MG. They are commonly prescribed for heart conditions and high blood pressure. If you need a beta-blocker, your cardiologist and neurologist should be aware, and close monitoring of MG symptoms is needed.

Eye drops containing beta-blockers (e.g., timolol for glaucoma) are absorbed into the bloodstream and can also worsen MG.

### Calcium Channel Blockers

Some calcium channel blockers (e.g., verapamil) may worsen MG. Use with caution and monitoring.

### Antiarrhythmic Drugs

- **Quinidine, procainamide, disopyramide** — can significantly worsen MG
- Discussion with both cardiologist and neurologist is important if these are needed

### Magnesium

Magnesium interferes with neuromuscular transmission by reducing acetylcholine release.

- **Intravenous magnesium** is particularly problematic (can cause severe worsening or crisis)
- Magnesium is present in some **antacids** (e.g., Milk of Magnesia), **laxatives**, and **supplements** — check labels
- Always inform treating teams of your MG before receiving IV fluids or magnesium for any reason

### Penicillamine

Penicillamine, used in rheumatoid arthritis, can actually trigger a form of MG in some people. If you develop MG while taking penicillamine, it may resolve on stopping the drug. Penicillamine should be avoided in established MG.

### Anaesthetic Drugs and Muscle Relaxants

Anaesthesia requires careful planning in MG. See the dedicated [Anaesthesia and MG](anaesthesia.md) page for full guidance — key points are:

- **Non-depolarising muscle relaxants** (e.g., vecuronium, atracurium, rocuronium): MG patients are **5–10 times more sensitive** than usual — doses must be dramatically reduced and neuromuscular monitoring is essential throughout
- **Depolarising agents** (e.g., suxamethonium): Paradoxically, MG patients are about 2-fold *less* sensitive to these, but they can still cause unpredictable responses
- **Pyridostigmine should not be stopped** before surgery — tell the anaesthetic team you take it
- **Steroid cover** is needed if you are on long-term steroids (the body's natural stress response is suppressed)
- Pre-operative stabilisation with [IVIG or plasmapheresis](treatments-acute.md) about two weeks before major surgery is often recommended
- Always ensure your anaesthetist has your MG diagnosis **before** any planned procedure

### Neurological and Psychiatric Medications

Several drugs used in neurology and psychiatry have been reported to occasionally worsen MG:
- **Lithium** — use with caution
- **Chlorpromazine and other phenothiazines** — potential risk
- **Phenytoin** — anticonvulsant; some evidence of worsening MG
- **Certain antidepressants** — individual variation; discuss with your neurologist

The evidence for many of these is limited and they are not absolutely banned. Caution with any new prescription is the key principle.

### Statins

Statins (used to lower cholesterol — e.g., simvastatin, atorvastatin) may rarely worsen MG in some people. This does not mean all people with MG should avoid statins — the cardiovascular benefits are significant — but any worsening of MG symptoms after starting a statin should be reported to your doctor.

### Checkpoint Inhibitor Cancer Immunotherapies

- **Anti-PD-1 and anti-CTLA-4 drugs** (e.g., nivolumab, pembrolizumab, ipilimumab) used to treat cancer can trigger new-onset MG in people who did not previously have it, and worsen existing MG
- If you are considering cancer immunotherapy and have MG, this must be discussed urgently with both your oncologist and neurologist
- This does not necessarily mean you cannot have immunotherapy, but the risks must be carefully assessed and monitoring must be in place

---

## Drugs That Are Generally Safe

Many commonly used medications are generally safe in MG. These include:
- Most **penicillins** and **cephalosporins** (for antibiotics)
- Most **pain relievers** (paracetamol, NSAIDs — though NSAIDs should be discussed if on steroids)
- **Proton pump inhibitors** (omeprazole, lansoprazole)
- **Antihistamines** (for allergy)

However, the safest approach is always to check with your MG specialist or neurologist before starting any new regular medication.

---

## Practical Advice

### Always Tell Every Healthcare Provider

- Your GP, hospital specialists, dentist, pharmacist, and paramedics must know you have MG
- This is especially important before **any surgical procedure** or **emergency treatment**
- Carry a **Myaware Patient Passport** or similar medical alert card at all times

### Before Starting Any New Medication

- Check with your MG neurologist or specialist nurse if you are unsure
- Ask the prescribing doctor or pharmacist to check for interactions with myasthenia gravis

### Over-the-Counter Medications

- Some over-the-counter preparations contain drugs that can worsen MG — particularly those containing **magnesium** or **quinine**
- Always read labels; ask the pharmacist if unsure

### Emergency Situations

- If you are treated in an emergency and cannot communicate, having a medical alert card or bracelet is invaluable
- The Myaware Patient Passport can be shown to paramedics and emergency doctors to quickly communicate your diagnosis and key concerns

---

## Important Note

This page refers to drugs to avoid specifically in relation to **myasthenia gravis (autoimmune MG)** — not to congenital myasthenic syndromes (CMS) or Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome (LEMS), where different considerations apply.
