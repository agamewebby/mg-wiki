# Treatments for MG — Overview

The treatment of MG has transformed dramatically in recent years, moving from broad immunosuppression towards precise, targeted therapies. The good news is that most people with MG can achieve good symptom control with modern treatment. There is no single "right" treatment — the best approach is tailored to your type of MG, the severity of your symptoms, your age, and your individual circumstances.

Treatment is usually a **combination of approaches**, working at different levels:

1. **Symptomatic treatment** — making the neuromuscular junction work better right now
2. **Short-term immune modulation** — rapidly reducing the antibody burden during a crisis or flare
3. **Long-term immunosuppression** — suppressing the underlying immune attack
4. **Surgical treatment** — removing the thymus gland (where appropriate)
5. **Targeted biological therapies** — newer drugs that block specific steps in the autoimmune process
6. **Emerging treatments** — cutting-edge therapies in development or early use

---

## Matching Treatment to Your Type of MG

Because MG is heterogeneous, treatment choices are increasingly guided by antibody status and individual patient factors:

| MG Subtype | First-Line Symptomatic | Immunosuppression | Specific Considerations |
|---|---|---|---|
| AChR+ generalised | Pyridostigmine | Steroids + steroid-sparer | Thymectomy; consider complement inhibitor or FcRn inhibitor for refractory disease |
| MuSK+ generalised | Pyridostigmine with caution | Steroids + mycophenolate | Early rituximab; rozanolixizumab; thymectomy less clearly beneficial |
| Ocular only | Pyridostigmine | Low-dose steroids if needed | Avoid over-treating; monitor for generalisation |
| Thymoma-associated | Pyridostigmine | Steroids + steroid-sparer | Thymoma resection essential; often more refractory |
| Seronegative | Pyridostigmine | Steroids + steroid-sparer | Same general approach; targeted biologics may be less applicable |
| Congenital (CMS) | Condition-specific (NOT standard MG treatment) | Not applicable | No immunosuppression; requires genetic diagnosis |

## Overview of Treatment Pages

### [Symptomatic Treatment](treatments-symptomatic.md)
Pyridostigmine (Mestinon) is almost always the first treatment tried. It does not treat the underlying autoimmune cause but makes the most of the acetylcholine that is available. Learn about how it works, dosing, side effects, and when it is and is not appropriate.

### [Immunosuppression](treatments-immunosuppression.md)
These treatments target the underlying autoimmune disease. They include corticosteroids (prednisolone/prednisone) and steroid-sparing agents such as azathioprine, mycophenolate mofetil, cyclosporine, tacrolimus, and methotrexate. They take weeks to months to reach full effect and are managed alongside symptomatic treatment.

### [Biological Therapies](treatments-biologics.md)
The last decade has seen an explosion of targeted biologics for MG. These include:
- **Complement inhibitors** — eculizumab, ravulizumab, zilucoplan
- **FcRn inhibitors** — efgartigimod, rozanolixizumab, nipocalimab
- **Anti-CD20 therapy** — rituximab

These are particularly important for refractory MG (disease not responding to conventional treatment) and specific subtypes such as MuSK-positive MG.

### [Surgical Treatment — Thymectomy](treatments-surgical.md)
Surgical removal of the thymus gland is recommended for people with thymoma and for most people with AChR-positive generalised MG. The landmark MGTX trial confirmed its benefits. Learn about eligibility, what the surgery involves, and what to expect.

### [Acute and Crisis Treatments](treatments-acute.md)
When rapid stabilisation is needed — during a myasthenic crisis, before surgery, or during a severe flare — two treatments can quickly reduce circulating antibody levels:
- **IVIG** (intravenous immunoglobulin) — pooled human antibodies that modulate the immune response
- **Plasmapheresis (PLEX)** — mechanical removal of antibodies from the blood

### [Emerging and Experimental Treatments](treatments-emerging.md)
The treatment pipeline for MG is exceptionally active. Emerging approaches include CAR-T cell therapy (Descartes-08, KYV-101), gene therapy, inebilizumab, and others. Some are available now for selected patients; others are in clinical trials.

## Key Principles of MG Treatment

### It takes time
Most immunosuppressants take **months** to reach full effect (azathioprine may take 12–18 months). This can be frustrating, but patience is essential — and symptomatic treatment bridges the gap.

### The "steroid dip" and 2025 Dosing
When starting corticosteroids, some people experience a **temporary worsening** of symptoms in the first 1–2 weeks. This is a recognised phenomenon and should not be mistaken for treatment failure. Modern **2025 clinical guidelines** (such as the ABN 2025 update) now prefer **daily steroid dosing** over alternate-day regimens to provide more consistent symptom control.

### Shifting treatment paradigms
Recent evidence (2024–2025) has led to a strategic shift:
- **Early Thymectomy:** For AChR-positive generalised MG, early thymectomy is prioritized to reduce long-term medication needs.
- **Earlier Biologics:** Targeted biological therapies (see [Biological Therapies](treatments-biologics.md)) are increasingly introduced earlier in the disease course, rather than being reserved as a last resort, to achieve faster stability and reduce total steroid exposure.
- **Low Maintenance Dose:** The target maintenance dose for steroids is now ideally **<7.5 mg/day** to minimize long-term side effects.

### Treatment is adjusted over time
MG treatment is not "set and forget." Regular reviews with your neurologist allow dose adjustments as your condition improves or changes. The goal is always the **lowest dose that maintains good control**, to minimise side effects.

### Side effects matter
Long-term immunosuppression carries significant side effects — particularly bone density loss, increased infection risk, and skin cancer risk. These need monitoring and proactive management (see [Skin Care on Immunosuppressants](skin-immunosuppressants.md)). Annual eye exams and regular bone density scans (DEXA) are standard requirements for those on long-term steroids.

### Drugs to avoid
Some commonly used medications can make MG significantly worse — even cause a crisis. Always carry a list and share it with every healthcare provider. See [Drugs to Avoid](drugs-to-avoid.md).

### Mental health matters
The unpredictability and impact of MG on daily life can contribute to anxiety and depression. These are more common in people with MG than in the general population and deserve attention alongside physical treatment.

## Questions to Ask Your Neurologist

- What type of MG do I have (which antibodies)?
- Which treatment approach is recommended for my type and severity?
- What are the latest 2025 guideline recommendations for my situation?
- What side effects should I watch for?
- How long before I should notice improvement?
- Should I be referred for thymectomy?
- Am I eligible for any biological therapies or clinical trials?
- What should I do if my symptoms suddenly worsen?
