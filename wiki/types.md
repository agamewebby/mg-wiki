# Types of MG

MG is not a single uniform disease. It is a family of related conditions, classified by which muscles are affected, which antibodies are present, and the age at which it begins. Understanding your type matters — it influences which treatments are most likely to help, and shapes the overall picture of what to expect.

---

## Classification by Clinical Presentation

### Ocular Myasthenia Gravis (oMG)

In ocular MG, weakness is confined strictly to the muscles controlling the eyelids and eye movements. Symptoms are limited to **drooping eyelids (ptosis)** and **double vision (diplopia)**.

- Affects approximately **15% of people** with MG long-term
- Often the **first presentation** of MG — up to 85% of people with MG first experience ocular symptoms
- Around **50–80% of people with ocular MG** will develop generalised MG within 1–2 years
- If symptoms remain purely ocular for **2–3 years**, they are unlikely to spread further
- Up to 50% of those with ocular MG do not have detectable antibodies in standard blood tests — making diagnosis challenging

### Generalised Myasthenia Gravis (gMG)

Weakness affects muscles beyond the eyes — including those involved in speaking, swallowing, breathing, and limb movement. This accounts for approximately **85% of all MG cases**.

The Myasthenia Gravis Foundation of America (MGFA) classifies generalised MG by severity:

| MGFA Class | Description |
|---|---|
| Class I | Ocular muscles only; all other strength normal |
| Class IIa | Mild weakness; predominantly limbs or axial muscles |
| Class IIb | Mild weakness; predominantly bulbar or respiratory muscles |
| Class IIIa | Moderate weakness; predominantly limbs or axial muscles |
| Class IIIb | Moderate weakness; predominantly bulbar or respiratory muscles |
| Class IVa | Severe weakness; predominantly limbs or axial muscles |
| Class IVb | Severe weakness; predominantly bulbar or respiratory muscles |
| Class V | Requires intubation with or without mechanical ventilation |

## Classification by Antibody Profile

This is increasingly the most important classification for guiding treatment decisions. Different antibodies cause MG through different mechanisms — and respond differently to different treatments.

### AChR Antibody-Positive MG

The most common form. The immune system produces antibodies against the **acetylcholine receptor (AChR)** at the neuromuscular junction.

- Present in **80–85%** of people with generalised MG, and approximately **50%** of those with ocular MG
- Strongly associated with thymus gland abnormalities
- Generally responds well to pyridostigmine (Mestinon)
- Thymectomy is particularly beneficial in this group
- Antibodies damage the NMJ through three mechanisms: complement activation (the most damaging), antigenic modulation (speeding up receptor breakdown), and direct blockade

### MuSK Antibody-Positive MG

Antibodies target **Muscle-Specific Kinase (MuSK)**, a protein essential for maintaining the structure of the neuromuscular junction.

- Present in **5–10%** of people with generalised MG (rising to approximately 40% of those who test negative for AChR antibodies)
- More common in **women**, often with **earlier onset**
- Characteristically causes severe **bulbar symptoms**: facial weakness, difficulty speaking and swallowing, and respiratory distress — with relatively preserved limb strength
- The thymus is typically normal — thymectomy is less clearly beneficial
- MuSK antibodies are predominantly IgG4 subclass, which does **not** activate the complement system — so the mechanism differs from AChR MG
- Responds **poorly to pyridostigmine** (and pyridostigmine may worsen symptoms in some cases)
- Responds well to **rituximab**, which is now recommended early in treatment

### LRP4 Antibody-Positive MG

Antibodies target **Low-Density Lipoprotein Receptor-Related Protein 4 (LRP4)**, which works with MuSK to organise AChRs at the junction.

- Found in approximately **1–3%** of MG cases, particularly those who test negative for both AChR and MuSK antibodies
- Often a **milder clinical presentation**
- Typically lacks thymic abnormalities
- LRP4 antibodies can also be found alongside AChR or MuSK antibodies and may worsen disease severity in those cases
- Still an area of active research

### Seronegative MG (SNMG)

Patients with clear clinical symptoms and electrophysiological evidence of MG but **no detectable antibodies** using standard blood tests.

- Accounts for approximately **5–15%** of people with generalised MG
- Clinical presentation is virtually identical to seropositive MG
- Some patients have antibodies detectable only with more specialised techniques (e.g., cell-based assays for clustered AChRs)
- Diagnosis relies on clinical assessment combined with electrophysiological tests
- Treatment follows the same general approach as AChR-positive MG, though some targeted biologics may be less applicable

## Classification by Age of Onset and Other Factors

### Early-Onset MG

- Onset before approximately age **50** (some definitions use 45)
- More common in **women**
- Strongly associated with **thymic hyperplasia** (an overactive thymus)
- Often AChR antibody positive
- Linked to the HLA-B8-DR3-DQ2 genetic pattern
- The thymus appears to be the originating site of the autoimmune process in this group

### Late-Onset MG

- Onset after approximately age **50**
- More common in **men**
- Often AChR antibody positive; associated with HLA-DRB1*15:01 genetic variant
- Thymic hyperplasia less common; thymus more likely to be normal or atrophied
- **Now the most rapidly growing group**, partly due to an ageing population
- Thymic atrophy means thymectomy may be less clearly beneficial, though it can still help

### Thymoma-Associated MG

- Approximately **10–15%** of people with MG have a thymoma (a tumour of the thymus gland)
- Almost all are AChR antibody positive
- Thymoma MG can be more severe and harder to control
- **Surgical removal of the thymoma is always necessary** — regardless of MG — because thymomas can become cancerous
- Removing the thymoma does not always resolve the MG, as autoreactive immune cells have already spread beyond the thymus

### Juvenile MG

- Autoimmune MG with onset before age **18**
- Roughly 10–15% of autoimmune MG cases in Western populations
- Children who develop MG before puberty (before approximately age 12) are more likely to have purely ocular involvement and higher rates of **spontaneous remission**
- Management must consider long-term effects of immunosuppressant medications on a growing child
- Asian populations have higher rates of juvenile ocular MG

### Neonatal (Transient) MG

- Affects approximately **10–15% of babies** born to mothers with autoimmune MG
- Caused by maternal IgG antibodies crossing the placenta during pregnancy
- Symptoms appear within hours to days of birth: weak sucking, floppy muscle tone (hypotonia), breathing difficulties, a weak cry
- **It is temporary** — symptoms resolve as maternal antibodies naturally clear from the baby's system, usually within **2–4 weeks**
- Not a lifelong condition; does not mean the child will develop MG

### Immune Checkpoint Inhibitor-Induced MG

- A newer, distinct form of MG triggered by **cancer immunotherapy drugs** (checkpoint inhibitors such as anti-PD-1 and anti-CTLA-4 agents)
- Occurs in approximately 0.1–0.2% of patients treated with these drugs, but likely underrecognised
- Often occurs in combination with myositis (muscle inflammation)
- The median age of affected patients is older (around 73); more common in men
- About two-thirds have AChR and striated muscle antibodies
- Mechanistically distinct from other MG subtypes

## Congenital Myasthenic Syndromes (CMS) — Not the Same as MG

It is important to distinguish autoimmune MG from Congenital Myasthenic Syndromes (CMS). CMS is **not an autoimmune disease**.

- CMS is a group of rare **genetic disorders** caused by inherited mutations in proteins at the neuromuscular junction
- Estimated prevalence of 1–2 per million people
- Onset at birth or early childhood
- **Autoantibodies are absent** — the immune system is not involved
- Standard MG immunosuppressants and thymectomy are **ineffective and should not be used**
- Diagnosis requires genetic testing
- Treatment varies significantly by the specific gene mutation; some respond to ephedrine, fluoxetine, or 3,4-diaminopyridine
- Babies with CMS may show decreased movements before birth, weak suck and cry, feeding and breathing difficulties
- Children and adults may experience late walking, difficulty with repetitive movements, frequent chest infections, and droopy eyelids

---

## Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome (LEMS) — Also Different

Another related but distinct condition is Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome (LEMS):

- Caused by antibodies against voltage-gated calcium channels on the **nerve** side of the junction (not the muscle side)
- Often associated with small cell lung cancer, but can also be autoimmune without cancer
- Key symptoms: weakness and heaviness in the legs and arms, together with **autonomic symptoms** such as dry mouth, constipation, and impotence in men
- Unlike MG, strength may temporarily **improve** with repeated activity (the opposite of MG's fatiguability pattern)
- Treatment differs from MG; does not respond to most MG medications
