# Biological Therapies for MG

The last decade has seen a remarkable expansion in targeted biological treatments for MG. These drugs target specific steps in the autoimmune process with much greater precision than broad immunosuppressants. They represent a major advance — particularly for people with refractory MG (disease that has not responded to conventional treatments), for those unable to tolerate standard immunosuppressants, and for specific subtypes such as MuSK-positive MG.

---

## Complement Inhibitors

The complement system's role in destroying the neuromuscular junction in AChR-positive MG makes it a compelling treatment target. Complement inhibitors prevent the formation of the **Membrane Attack Complex (MAC)** that damages the postsynaptic membrane.

All currently approved complement inhibitors in MG target the **C5 convertase** step of the complement cascade.

**Important safety note — meningococcal infection risk:** Blocking complement C5 removes an essential defence against encapsulated bacteria. Patients on C5 inhibitors have a **1,000–2,000-fold increased risk of meningococcal disease** (*Neisseria meningitidis*), which can be rapidly fatal.

All patients **must receive both meningococcal vaccines** at least 2 weeks before starting:
- **MenACWY** vaccine (covers serogroups A, C, W135, Y)
- **Serogroup B vaccine** (Bexsero or Trumenba)

If treatment must start before vaccination has taken full effect, **prophylactic antibiotics** are given for at least 2 weeks. All patients should carry a **patient safety card** and go to A&E immediately for any fever, severe headache, or neck stiffness. Annual meningococcal boosters are required for as long as treatment continues.

### Eculizumab (Brand Name: Soliris)

The first complement inhibitor approved for MG (2017 in the US; 2018 in the EU).

- A monoclonal antibody that binds complement protein C5, preventing the formation of the MAC
- **Administration**: Intravenous (IV) infusion — weekly for 4 weeks (induction), then every 2 weeks (maintenance)
- **Approved for**: Refractory, AChR antibody-positive generalised MG in adults
- **Evidence**: Significant clinical benefit demonstrated in the **REGAIN trial**
- Approximately 30% of patients do not respond well, underscoring the importance of other mechanisms in some people

### Ravulizumab (Brand Name: Ultomiris)

A longer-acting version of eculizumab with the same mechanism but an extended half-life.

- **Administration**: Intravenous infusion — maintenance infusions required only every **8 weeks** (vs. every 2 weeks for eculizumab) — a significant practical advantage
- Approved for AChR antibody-positive generalised MG in adults

### Zilucoplan (Brand Name: Zilbrysq)

A newer C5 inhibitor with a critical practical difference: **it can be self-administered at home**.

- A small peptide administered as a **once-daily subcutaneous (under-the-skin) self-injection**
- The first complement inhibitor for MG that patients can administer themselves, without attending an infusion centre
- **Approved by the FDA in 2023** for AChR antibody-positive generalised MG
- Has a dual mechanism: inhibits C5 cleavage AND blocks C5b from binding to C6 — potentially providing more complete complement blockade
- **Long-term data (2025)**: The RAISE-XT extension trial confirmed sustained clinical response and safety for over 2 years of daily use.
- **Side effects**: Injection site reactions (bruising), upper respiratory tract infections, and diarrhea. Carry a boxed warning for meningococcal infections.

## FcRn Inhibitors (Neonatal Fc Receptor Blockers)

FcRn inhibitors are a newer class of treatments that work by a completely different mechanism from complement inhibitors.

### The FcRn Mechanism

The **neonatal Fc receptor (FcRn)** is responsible for recycling IgG antibodies in the body — it picks up IgG antibodies from the circulation, protects them from degradation, and returns them. This recycling normally extends the lifespan of protective antibodies.

By **blocking FcRn**, these drugs prevent this recycling, causing all IgG antibodies — including the pathogenic autoantibodies causing MG — to be broken down faster than usual. The result is a rapid reduction in circulating antibody levels, including anti-AChR or anti-MuSK antibodies.

FcRn inhibitors are more broadly acting than complement inhibitors (they reduce overall IgG levels, not just complement activity) but are also less specific — all IgG antibodies are reduced, including protective ones.

### Efgartigimod (Brand Names: Vyvgart, Vyvgart Hytrulo)

The first approved FcRn inhibitor for MG (2021 in the US; 2022 in the EU).

- Available as an **intravenous infusion** (Vyvgart) or as a **subcutaneous injection** formulation (Vyvgart Hytrulo, which combines efgartigimod with hyaluronidase to allow faster subcutaneous delivery)
- **Approved for**: Generalised MG — **both AChR antibody-positive and MuSK antibody-positive** patients
- **Treatment schedule**: Cycles of 4 weekly doses (IV or SC), then a break; further cycles given as needed based on response — an intermittent approach rather than continuous maintenance dosing
- **Evidence**: The **ADAPT trial** showed 67.7% of patients achieved clinically meaningful improvement vs. 29.7% on placebo; real-world data (2024–2025) confirmed reduction in hospitalisations
- **Side effects**: Headache, upper respiratory infections, fatigue; **hypogammaglobulinaemia** (reduced IgG) is expected — monitor for infections. No meningococcal risk (unlike complement inhibitors)

### Rozanolixizumab (Brand Name: Rystiggo)

Approved in 2023 for **both AChR antibody-positive and MuSK antibody-positive generalised MG** — an important distinction as it is one of the few approved agents covering both major subtypes.

- **Administration**: Subcutaneous infusion (via a small pump or manual push) — **EU approval for home self-administration** granted in early 2025, significantly reducing clinic visits
- Treatment given once weekly for 6 weeks per cycle
- **Evidence**: 2025 clinical analysis highlighted its particular effectiveness in improving **ocular and bulbar symptoms** (swallowing and speech)
- **Side effects**: Headache (most common), diarrhea, and pyrexia (fever)

### Nipocalimab (Brand Name: Imaavy)

**FDA-approved in April 2025** for generalized MG in adults and adolescents (aged 12+).

- **Administration**: Intravenous infusion given every 4 weeks
- **Mechanism**: Blocks FcRn to reduce circulating IgG levels
- **Efficacy**: In the Phase 3 **VIVACITY-MG3** trial, patients showed a significant 4.70-point improvement in the MG-ADL score. It is noted for providing sustained disease control (up to 24 weeks).
- **Side effects**: Respiratory tract infections, peripheral edema, and muscle spasms.

### Batoclimab

Update (March 2025): The Phase 3 **Flex study** met its primary endpoints (significant MG-ADL improvement). However, development is shifting toward a next-generation version (**IMVT-1402**) which aims to provide similar efficacy without the cholesterol and albumin side effects observed with batoclimab.

## Anti-CD20 Therapy — Rituximab

CD20 is a protein found on the surface of **B-cells** — the immune cells responsible for producing autoantibodies. Rituximab is a monoclonal antibody that targets and depletes CD20-positive B-cells, thereby reducing antibody production.

### Rituximab (Brand Names: MabThera, Rituxan)

Rituximab has become a **standard therapy for MuSK antibody-positive MG**, where it has demonstrated particularly impressive results.

- **Administration**: Intravenous infusion, with doses often repeated every 6–12 months.
- **Timing (2025 Consensus)**: Updated guidelines advocate for the **early use of rituximab** (within the first year of diagnosis) for MuSK-positive MG and in many cases of **Late-Onset MG**, to protect the neuromuscular junction before irreversible damage occurs—a shift away from reserving it as a "last resort."
- **Efficacy (2025 Data)**: A major 2025 meta-analysis showed that **low-dose rituximab** (e.g., a 500mg fixed dose) is highly effective, with over **76% of patients** achieving Minimal Manifestation Status.
- **Mechanism**: Depletes CD20-positive B-cells, reducing autoantibody production.

**Side effects:**
- Infusion reactions (fever, chills, flushing) — managed with pre-medication.
- Increased risk of respiratory infections.
- Rare risk of **Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML)**; regular monitoring required.

---

## Inebilizumab (Brand Name: Uplizna)

**FDA-approved in December 2025** for generalized MG in AChR+ or MuSK+ adults.

- **Mechanism**: Targets **CD19+** B-cells rather than CD20. This is significant because CD19 is expressed on a broader range of B-cells, including some plasma cells that rituximab misses.
- **Administration**: A convenient **biannual (twice-yearly)** dosing schedule after an initial loading dose.
- **Efficacy**: The **MINT trial** showed a major reduction in clinical symptoms and the risk of relapse.
- **Side effects**: Headache, nasopharyngitis, and infusion-related reactions. Patients should be screened for Hepatitis B and Tuberculosis before starting.

## Who Are These Treatments For?

The treatment landscape in 2025 has moved toward a **"Time is NMJ"** philosophy—advocating for the rapid stabilization of MG with biologics to prevent permanent damage to the neuromuscular junction. Biologic therapies are now prioritized for:

1. **Rapid disease stabilization**: Quickly reducing autoantibody levels in patients with severe symptoms.
2. **Early intervention (New for 2025)**: Starting biologics like rituximab within the first year for MuSK-positive or Late-Onset MG, rather than as a "last resort."
3. **Refractory MG**: For those who haven't responded to standard steroid or immunosuppressant therapies.
4. **Steroid-sparing**: Allowing patients to reduce or eliminate high-dose steroids and their associated side effects.


Access to these treatments varies by country. In the UK, most require approval through NHS commissioning pathways; in other countries, insurance approval is typically required.
