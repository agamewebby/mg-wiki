# What is a Crisis? When Should I Go to Hospital?

*A plain-English guide — no medical background needed.*

*Want the full detailed version? See [Myasthenic Crisis](myasthenic-crisis.md)*

---

A **myasthenic crisis** is when MG becomes severe enough to affect your breathing. It is the most serious situation in MG — and it is a medical emergency.

This page explains what a crisis is, how to recognise it, and exactly when to call 999 (or your emergency number) or go straight to hospital.

---

## What Happens in a Crisis

In a myasthenic crisis, the muscles that control breathing become too weak to work properly. Your body cannot get enough oxygen.

This is rare in day-to-day life — most people with MG never have a crisis, or have only one in their lifetime. But because it involves breathing, it is serious when it does happen.

The good news: **if recognised and treated quickly, a myasthenic crisis is survivable and treatable.** Hospital teams are well equipped to handle it.

---

## Warning Signs — When to Get Help Immediately

**Call 999 (or your emergency number) or go straight to A&E if you notice:**

- Shortness of breath that is getting worse quickly
- You cannot take a full breath in
- Breathing feels like hard work — your neck or stomach muscles are working to help you breathe
- Your voice has become very weak or you are struggling to speak
- You cannot swallow at all, or are choking on liquids
- You feel your throat is closing or you cannot clear your airway
- You feel faint, confused, or very drowsy alongside any of the above

**Do not wait to see if it improves on its own. With breathing, time matters.**

Tell the paramedics and emergency team: "I have myasthenia gravis." Show them your MG patient card if you have one.

---

## The Build-Up — Warning Signs Before a Crisis

A crisis rarely comes out of nowhere. There are usually warning signs in the hours or days before. Recognising these early gives you time to seek help before breathing is affected.

Watch for a rapid worsening of your usual symptoms:

- **Swallowing becomes much harder** — more effort, coughing or choking on food or drink
- **Voice changes** — becomes very weak, nasal, or fades in a single sentence
- **Chewing becomes very difficult** — you cannot finish a meal
- **Increasing double vision or drooping eyelids** beyond your usual level
- **Increasing limb weakness** beyond your normal baseline
- **Breathing feels more effortful** when lying flat (you may need extra pillows)

If you notice a rapid deterioration over 24–48 hours, contact your MG specialist team or neurological nurse urgently. Do not wait for your next routine appointment.

## What Causes a Crisis?

The most common triggers are:

- **Infections** — even a chest infection, urinary tract infection, or stomach bug can cause a significant MG flare. This is the single most common cause of a crisis.
- **Medications** — certain drugs can worsen MG. See [Drugs to Avoid](drugs-to-avoid.md). Importantly, tell any doctor or dentist treating you about your MG before they prescribe anything.
- **Surgery and anaesthesia** — planned surgery requires careful preparation. See [Anaesthesia and MG](anaesthesia.md).
- **Stress** — severe emotional or physical stress
- **Stopping MG medications** — do not stop your medications suddenly without discussing it with your neurologist
- **Pregnancy and childbirth** — a period of particular risk requiring close specialist monitoring

---

## What Happens in Hospital

If you go to hospital with a severe MG worsening or crisis, the team will:

1. **Monitor your breathing** — they may check how strong your breathing muscles are with a simple bedside test
2. **Support your breathing if needed** — in a crisis, a breathing machine (ventilator) may be used temporarily while treatment takes effect
3. **Give fast-acting treatment** — either **plasma exchange** (filtering the blood to remove the misfiring antibodies) or **IVIG** (a drip of healthy antibodies). Both work within days to reverse the crisis.
4. **Find and treat the trigger** — particularly if an infection is involved

A crisis is treatable. The breathing support buys time while the treatment works. Most people recover well.

## A Different Kind of Crisis — Cholinergic Crisis

There is one other type of crisis worth knowing about — the **cholinergic crisis**. This is actually caused by **too much** pyridostigmine (Mestinon), not too little.

Signs of a cholinergic crisis include:
- Muscle twitching or fasciculations (small rapid flickers under the skin)
- Excessive sweating, drooling, or tearing
- Stomach cramps and diarrhoea
- **Worsening weakness** despite being on pyridostigmine

If you suspect this — for example, if you have recently significantly increased your pyridostigmine dose — seek medical advice. The treatment is to temporarily stop pyridostigmine and provide breathing support if needed.

If you are unsure whether your worsening is due to too much or too little pyridostigmine, **go to hospital rather than taking more tablets.**

---

## Preparing for an Emergency

You cannot always prevent a crisis. But you can be prepared:

- **Carry a patient passport or medical alert card** at all times that identifies your MG diagnosis and your key medications
- **Carry at least one dose of your pyridostigmine** with you when you go out
- **Tell people around you** about your MG — family, close friends, and colleagues should know the warning signs and what to do
- **Have your MG team's contact number saved** in your phone for urgent queries
- **Know your nearest major hospital** with a neurology department
