# Diet, Alcohol, and Lifestyle

Living well with MG involves more than just medication. What you eat, how you manage your energy, and how you structure your day can make a meaningful difference to your quality of life. This page covers practical guidance on diet, alcohol, exercise, and fatigue management — all of which are areas where small adjustments can have a real impact.

---

## Diet

### General Principles

A healthy, well-balanced diet supports overall health and helps your body function as well as possible. The main principles are the same as for anyone:

1. **Base meals on starchy carbohydrates** — try to choose wholegrain and high-fibre options
2. **Eat plenty of fruit and vegetables** — aim for at least 5 portions per day
3. **Choose lean protein sources** — fish, poultry, beans, lentils, eggs
4. **Include dairy** (or alternatives) for calcium and bone health — important if you are on steroids, which can weaken bones
5. **Limit foods high in fat and sugar** — small amounts are fine for variety

**Is there a specific MG diet?** No — as of yet, there is no medically proven specific diet that alleviates MG symptoms. General healthy eating principles apply.

### Eating Little and Often

MG can cause fatigue with chewing and difficulty swallowing. With this in mind:

- **Eat little and often** rather than three large meals — this helps maintain energy levels and avoids exhausting the muscles involved in eating
- **Smaller portions** are easier to manage and less tiring to eat
- **Prepare food in advance** when you have more energy, or choose foods that are quick and easy to prepare
- **Take your pyridostigmine 30–45 minutes before meals** if swallowing or chewing are affected — this times the peak medication effect with mealtimes

### Soft Foods

If chewing or swallowing is difficult:

- Choose **soft foods** that require less effort: mashed potato, scrambled eggs, yoghurt, soup, soft fish, well-cooked vegetables, smoothies
- Avoid tough, chewy, or dry foods that are harder to manage (e.g., steak, crusty bread, dry crackers)
- Softer textures reduce the fatigue of eating and the risk of choking
- Myaware (the UK MG charity) has a soft food recipe section on their website

**Seek help if needed:** If you are having significant difficulty swallowing, eating, or maintaining your weight, speak to your neurologist or GP. Referral to a **dietitian** and/or **speech and language therapist** can provide in-depth individual advice and is very worthwhile.

### Nutrition and Immunosuppressants

If you are on long-term steroids, pay particular attention to:
- **Calcium** — dairy foods, fortified plant milks, leafy greens; supplements are often prescribed alongside steroids
- **Vitamin D** — important for bone health and immune function; your doctor may arrange a blood test and prescribe supplements
- **Controlling weight gain** — steroids can cause significant weight gain; a diet lower in refined carbohydrates and sugars can help manage this

## Alcohol

**The position from the Myaware Medical Committee is that moderate alcohol intake will not worsen myasthenia.** Some people may experience minor side effects such as flushing, and individuals should judge for themselves if they have any unpleasant reactions. However, serious problems from moderate alcohol are very unlikely.

### Things to Keep in Mind

- **Moderation is key.** Excessive alcohol intake is not advisable for anyone, and particularly for people on medications with potential liver effects (such as azathioprine or methotrexate)
- **Alcohol and medication interactions**: Some MG medications (particularly azathioprine and methotrexate) can interact with alcohol, increasing the risk of liver damage. If you are on these medications, it is worth discussing alcohol limits with your specialist
- **Alcohol and fatigue**: Alcohol can worsen fatigue, which may in turn make MG symptoms feel more pronounced
- **Heat**: Alcohol causes blood vessel dilation and can make you feel warmer — relevant because heat can worsen MG symptoms
- **Individual variation**: As with most things in MG, individual responses vary. If you notice that alcohol consistently makes your symptoms worse, it is worth noting and discussing with your medical team

## Exercise

### When MG is Not Well Controlled

When MG is active and not well controlled, exertion weakens muscles further. In this phase:
- Exercise within limits that **do not exceed your current capability**
- Avoid pushing through significant weakness — rest before you reach the point of exhaustion
- Gentle, low-effort activities are more appropriate: short walks, gentle stretching, relaxation

### When MG is Well Controlled

As MG becomes better controlled, the aim is to become as active as possible within your capacity:
- **You are encouraged to exercise as much as anyone else** — there are examples of professional athletes who have returned to their former level of performance after an MG diagnosis
- Regular, gentle exercise supports general health, muscle tone, and mental wellbeing
- **Gentle options**: walking, swimming (with care in heat), Tai Chi, yoga

### Types of Exercise That May Be Particularly Helpful

- **Tai Chi** — gentle, low-impact, focuses on controlled movement and balance
- **Yoga** — can build strength gently and also supports relaxation
- **Swimming** — low-impact and supports the body's weight (note: heat from very warm pools can worsen symptoms)
- **Walking** — the most accessible; build up gradually

### Exercise and Heat

**Heat worsens MG** — temperature affects the efficiency of the acetylcholinesterase enzyme and neuromuscular transmission. When exercising:
- Choose cool environments (early morning, air-conditioned gyms)
- Stay well hydrated
- Avoid very hot pools, saunas, or exercising in direct sun
- If you feel your symptoms worsening with heat, rest and cool down

## Energy Management and Pacing

This is perhaps the most important practical skill for living well with MG.

### The Core Principle

**The more you rest, the more you can do.** This is counterintuitive but true in MG. Rest is not giving up — it is a strategic investment that allows you to do more overall.

### Practical Pacing Strategies

- **Plan your day** around your strongest time — for many people with MG, this is mid-morning after pyridostigmine has taken effect
- **Focus important activities** — make phone calls first thing; plan demanding tasks for your peak window
- **Schedule regular rest breaks** throughout the day — even short rest periods allow muscles to recover
- **Spread activities** throughout the day rather than doing everything at once
- **Listen to your body** — learn to recognise the early signs of fatigue (this is when symptoms are likely to be triggered) and rest before you reach exhaustion
- **Plan ahead**: prepare food when you have energy; choose rest-friendly activities for difficult days

### Good Days and Bad Days

Accept that with MG there will be good days and bad days. On a bad day:
- Reduce activity and rest more
- Do not try to "catch up" on a good day by overdoing things — this leads to a worse next day
- It is better to take small, consistent steps than to swing between doing too much and recovering

## Temperature and Weather

- **Avoid extreme temperatures** — both hot and cold can affect muscle function
- **Heat** is generally more problematic — warm weather, hot baths, fever, and hot drinks can all temporarily worsen MG symptoms
- Some people find **cold** also affects their symptoms — layer appropriately and avoid prolonged cold exposure

### Coping in Hot Weather — Practical Tips

Hot weather requires active management in MG. Here are practical strategies recommended by Myaware:

**Keeping your environment cool:**
- Close windows, blinds, and curtains during the hottest part of the day; air rooms by opening them in the early morning or late evening when it is cooler
- Use fans or air conditioning where available

**Staying hydrated:**
- Aim for **2–4 litres of water-based fluids per day** — hydration is key to keeping your core temperature lower
- If swallowing liquids is difficult: blend a partially-thawed frozen banana with a little Greek yoghurt as a cooling snack; freeze juices, yoghurts, or flavoured waters as homemade ice pops
- Try taking medication with a spoonful of yoghurt or custard if swallowing becomes harder in the heat; pre-prepared thickened drinks are portable options if you are on thickened fluids

**Managing food and meals:**
- Prepare food and drinks the evening before when temperatures are cooler
- If a main meal feels too much, switch to 6 smaller meals throughout the day

**Cooling your body:**
- Run cold water over your wrists and use a bowl of cold water to cool your feet (safely, seated, with someone nearby if needed)
- Ice or cool packs can provide **temporary relief from ptosis** (drooping eyelids) — apply gently to closed eyelids
- Gel pillows or cold flannels can be placed on the lap, chest, forehead, or eyes for short-term relief
- Wear **loose clothing made from natural fibres** — these allow better airflow and are more comfortable

**Out and about:**
- Eyes may be more sensitive to light in hot weather — take sunglasses, an eye patch, a hat, and sunscreen; keep a spare set in the car
- Keep an unopened bottle of water and snacks in the car in case of delays or breakdown, so you can take medication safely
- Carry at least one dose of medication in an airtight container in your bag or car for emergencies

**At work:**
- Employers have a legal responsibility to provide a safe working temperature. If heat at work is affecting your MG, raise this with your employer. Reasonable adjustments should be made for disabled workers — see [gov.uk guidance on workplace temperatures](https://www.gov.uk/workplace-temperatures) and [reasonable adjustments for disabled workers](https://www.gov.uk/reasonable-adjustments-for-disabled-workers).

## Sleep and Rest

Good quality sleep is particularly important in MG:
- Sleep is when muscles recover — getting enough sleep allows the body to reset
- Some people with MG have difficulty sleeping due to breathing changes when lying flat (orthopnoea) — discuss this with your neurologist
- Naps can be genuinely therapeutic — even a short rest in the afternoon can restore significant strength

---

## Swallowing Difficulties — Practical Tips

If you struggle with swallowing:
- **Eat slowly** and take small mouthfuls
- Choose **soft, moist foods** that slide down easily
- **Avoid rushing meals**
- Sit upright when eating and for 30 minutes afterwards
- Stay hydrated — dehydration makes swallowing harder
- Report significant swallowing difficulties to your medical team promptly — aspiration (food or liquid entering the lungs) can cause pneumonia
