# Symptoms of MG

MG produces a distinctive pattern of muscle weakness. Understanding what symptoms to expect — and why they behave the way they do — can help you communicate with your medical team, manage your energy, and recognise warning signs early.

---

## The Defining Feature: Fatiguable Weakness

The hallmark of MG is **weakness that worsens with sustained or repeated use of a muscle, and improves with rest**. This is called **fatiguability**.

A person with MG might:
- Feel relatively well in the morning but significantly weaker by the afternoon
- Start a conversation clearly but find their speech becoming increasingly difficult to understand
- climb stairs easily at the start of a walk but struggle by the end
- After a short rest or nap, experience a partial or full return of strength

This pattern distinguishes MG from most other causes of muscle weakness, where weakness tends to be constant. If you notice that your symptoms get worse as the day goes on or as you use a particular muscle repeatedly, and better after rest, this is a very important clue.

---

## Eye and Eyelid Symptoms

These are the **most common initial symptoms**, occurring in approximately 85% of people when MG first presents.

### Ptosis (Drooping Eyelids)
- One or both eyelids droop
- May affect one eye more than the other, and can switch sides
- Typically worsens as the day progresses or with prolonged upward gaze
- A helpful diagnostic clue: when you hold one drooping eyelid up, the other may droop more (Cogan's lid twitch sign)

### Diplopia (Double Vision)
- Caused by weakness of the muscles that move the eyes (extraocular muscles)
- The eyes fall out of alignment, producing two overlapping images
- May be horizontal, vertical, or diagonal depending on which muscles are affected
- Can make reading, driving, and other tasks very difficult

**Note:** In ocular MG, symptoms remain limited to the eyes and eyelids for the entire course of the disease. In generalised MG, the eyes are usually where symptoms begin before spreading.

---

## Bulbar Symptoms (Speech, Swallowing, Chewing)

"Bulbar" refers to muscles in and around the throat, mouth, and face that are controlled by nerves originating in the brainstem. These are particularly affected in **MuSK-positive MG** and in more severe forms of the disease.

### Dysarthria (Difficulty Speaking)
- Slurred, nasal-sounding, or indistinct speech
- Speech may start clearly but become harder to understand as a conversation continues
- The voice may take on a "nasal" quality as the soft palate weakens
- On the telephone this can be particularly noticeable

### Dysphagia (Difficulty Swallowing)
- Difficulty swallowing food or liquid
- Can cause choking, coughing, or food/liquid going the wrong way (aspiration)
- May require eating very slowly or in smaller amounts
- Severe dysphagia can lead to weight loss and aspiration pneumonia (a lung infection from inhaled food or liquid)
- **Seek medical advice promptly if swallowing is significantly affected**

### Jaw Fatigue and Chewing Difficulty
- Difficulty chewing, particularly with tough foods like steak or crusty bread
- The jaw may feel too tired to stay closed towards the end of a meal
- Some people need to support their chin with their hand while eating

### Facial Weakness
- The facial muscles may weaken, producing a flat or expressionless appearance
- Attempting to smile may produce a horizontal "snarl" (sometimes called a "myasthenic snarl") rather than a normal smile
- Difficulty closing the eyes tightly

### Nasal Regurgitation
- Liquids may come back up through the nose when swallowing, due to weakness of the soft palate

---

## Neck and Trunk Symptoms

### Neck Extensor Weakness (Dropped Head Syndrome)
- Difficulty holding the head upright
- The head falls forward involuntarily, especially when tired
- People may need to support their head with a hand, or use a chin rest
- Can be very uncomfortable and tiring to compensate for

### Truncal Weakness
- Weakness of the muscles supporting the spine and torso
- Contributes to fatigue when sitting or standing for long periods without support

---

## Limb Weakness

- Typically affects **proximal muscles** (those closest to the body's centre — hips, thighs, shoulders, upper arms) more than **distal muscles** (hands and feet)
- Arms are often more affected than legs
- Common complaints include:
  - Difficulty raising the arms above the head (hair washing, reaching shelves)
  - Climbing stairs or rising from a low chair
  - Carrying shopping or heavy items
  - In more severe cases, difficulty walking distances
- Weakness tends to fluctuate — you might manage something in the morning that you cannot do in the afternoon

---

## Respiratory Symptoms

Weakness of the muscles that control breathing — the diaphragm and the muscles between the ribs (intercostal muscles) — can cause:

- **Shortness of breath**, especially during activity
- **Difficulty breathing when lying flat** (orthopnoea), because the diaphragm has to work harder against gravity
- A feeling of not being able to take a satisfying breath

Respiratory involvement is the **most dangerous aspect of MG** and can progress to a myasthenic crisis if untreated.

---

## Myasthenic Crisis — When to Seek Emergency Help

A **myasthenic crisis** is a life-threatening emergency in which respiratory muscle weakness becomes severe enough to cause respiratory failure, requiring mechanical ventilation in hospital.

**Signs of a crisis include:**
- Severe shortness of breath or inability to catch your breath
- Difficulty swallowing even liquids, or inability to swallow at all
- Very weak or absent voice
- Rapid breathing or feeling of being unable to breathe deeply enough

**If you experience these symptoms, call 999 (or your local emergency number) immediately.** Do not wait. A myasthenic crisis is treatable but requires urgent medical intervention.

See the [Myasthenic Crisis](myasthenic-crisis.md) page for more detail on recognition, triggers, and management.

## What MG Does NOT Cause

It is reassuring to know the boundaries of MG's effects:

- **No pain** — MG itself is painless (though secondary pain from abnormal posture or compensatory strain is possible)
- **No numbness or tingling** — MG does not affect sensation
- **No cognitive effects** — MG does not affect the brain, memory, or thinking
- **No pupil changes** — MG does not affect the pupils (important in excluding other conditions)
- **No effect on the heart** — the heart is smooth muscle and not affected by MG
- **No digestion problems from MG itself** — though medications used to treat MG (especially pyridostigmine) can cause gastrointestinal side effects

---

## How Symptoms Progress Over Time

MG does not follow a single predictable path:

- Most people have **ocular symptoms first**; roughly half to four-fifths will develop generalised symptoms within 1–2 years
- If MG remains purely ocular for 2–3 years, it is likely to stay that way
- Severity typically **peaks within the first 2–3 years** of diagnosis
- Many people experience fluctuating periods of relative remission and worsening (exacerbations) throughout their lives
- Spontaneous complete remission occurs but is not common; it is more likely in children with ocular-onset MG
- With treatment, the **majority of people with MG can achieve a good quality of life**

---

## Fatigue Beyond Muscle Weakness

Many people with MG report a more general fatigue — a tiredness that goes beyond the specific muscles being weak. Research confirms this is real and distinct from the neuromuscular fatiguability. It may be related to:
- Sleep disturbance (difficulty breathing lying flat, or poor sleep quality)
- The psychological impact of living with a chronic unpredictable illness
- The broader systemic effects of an overactive immune system (fatigue is common in many autoimmune conditions)

This type of fatigue deserves attention and support in its own right.
