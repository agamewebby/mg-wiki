# Symptomatic Treatment — Pyridostigmine (Mestinon)

Symptomatic treatment makes the neuromuscular junction work better without addressing the underlying autoimmune cause. It is usually the **first treatment started** when MG is diagnosed, and many people continue it alongside immunosuppression for years. For some people with mild MG, it may be the only treatment needed.

---

## Pyridostigmine (Brand Name: Mestinon)

Pyridostigmine is an **acetylcholinesterase inhibitor**. It does not cure MG or slow the underlying immune attack, but it reduces symptoms by increasing the effectiveness of the acetylcholine that is available at the neuromuscular junction.

### How It Works

Normally, after acetylcholine (ACh) is released from the nerve and binds to receptors on the muscle, an enzyme called **acetylcholinesterase** quickly breaks it down. This resets the junction ready for the next signal.

In MG, the number of working receptors is reduced. Pyridostigmine **inhibits acetylcholinesterase**, slowing the breakdown of ACh. This means ACh stays in the junction longer and has more chances to find and activate the reduced number of functioning receptors. The effect is a partial boost in muscle strength.

### Typical Dosing

- Standard tablets: **30–60 mg** taken every **4–6 hours** during waking hours
- Most people start at a lower dose and adjust based on response
- A slow-release version (**Mestinon Timespan, 180 mg**) can be taken at night to help with morning weakness
- **Timing is important**: taking pyridostigmine 30–45 minutes before meals can help if swallowing or chewing is affected
- **Focus activities at your peak**: the drug takes effect within 30–60 minutes and wears off over 4–6 hours — plan demanding tasks for when the medication is working well

### Side Effects

Because pyridostigmine increases acetylcholine activity throughout the body — not just in skeletal muscle — it can cause effects in other systems:

**Gastrointestinal (most common):**
- Nausea and stomach cramps
- Increased bowel movements or diarrhoea
- Excess saliva

**Other:**
- Increased sweating
- Muscle twitching (fasciculations) — a "buzzing" sensation in muscles
- Bradycardia (slow heart rate) at higher doses
- Eye: small pupils, slightly blurred vision

These side effects are dose-related. If they are troublesome, your doctor may lower the dose, adjust timing, or prescribe a medication to reduce gastrointestinal effects (such as loperamide or propantheline).

### Contraindications & Precautions

Pyridostigmine should **not** be used in certain conditions:
- **Mechanical obstruction:** strictly contraindicated if there is a known mechanical blockage of the gastrointestinal or urinary tract.
- **Hypersensitivity:** known allergy to pyridostigmine bromide or to **bromides**.
- **Peritonitis:** active inflammation of the abdominal lining.

### Drug Interactions

Several medications can interact with pyridostigmine, either reducing its effect or increasing side effects:
- **Antimuscarinics (e.g., Atropine, Glycopyrrolate):** Often used to manage GI side effects, but they can mask the signs of **cholinergic crisis** (overdose).
- **Neuromuscular Blockers:** Pyridostigmine may **prolong** the effects of depolarizing agents (like succinylcholine) used during surgery.
- **Antibiotics:** Certain antibiotics (e.g., aminoglycosides like neomycin or streptomycin) have a mild muscle-weakening effect that can counteract pyridostigmine.
- **Corticosteroids:** Taking these together may initially increase muscle weakness, particularly when starting or increasing steroid doses.
- **Magnesium:** Magnesium supplements can worsen MG symptoms and reduce the perceived effectiveness of the drug.

### Special Populations

- **Pregnancy:** Pyridostigmine crosses the placenta. It is generally considered safe but requires careful monitoring; 10–20% of newborns may experience transient neonatal myasthenia. Intravenous (IV) use should be avoided in the third trimester as it may induce uterine contractions.
- **Breastfeeding:** Excreted in breast milk in negligible amounts; generally considered compatible with breastfeeding.
- **Elderly:** Dosing should be cautious (starting low), as older adults are at higher risk of bradycardia and may have reduced renal function.
- **Renal Impairment:** Since the drug is primarily excreted by the kidneys, dose adjustments are necessary for those with moderate to severe renal impairment (often reducing the dose by 50–80% depending on Creatinine Clearance).
- **Hepatic Impairment:** Caution is advised, though the liver is not the primary route of metabolism.

### What It Does and Doesn't Do

- **Does help**: reduces weakness, improves strength during the working period of the drug
- **Does not help**: address the underlying autoimmune cause, prevent disease progression, reduce antibody levels
- **Works best**: in AChR antibody-positive MG

### Important: Pyridostigmine in MuSK-Positive MG

Pyridostigmine works less well — and in some people can actually **worsen symptoms** — in MuSK antibody-positive MG. The reason relates to how acetylcholine itself may interfere with the clustering process disrupted by MuSK antibodies. For MuSK MG, pyridostigmine is used with caution at lower doses, or sometimes avoided altogether, with early progression to immunosuppression and rituximab.

### Maximum Dose and Slow-Release Options

- Standard dose range: **30–60 mg every 4–6 hours**
- Maximum dose: typically **480 mg/day** — higher doses increase cholinergic side effects without meaningful additional benefit
- **Mestinon Timespan (180 mg slow-release)** can be used overnight to help with morning weakness on waking
- **Renal impairment note:** pyridostigmine is renally excreted; dose reduction is needed in moderate-to-severe kidney disease to prevent accumulation and cholinergic toxicity

## Cholinergic Crisis — Overtreatment

A **cholinergic crisis** occurs when too much pyridostigmine is taken, causing an **excess of acetylcholine** in the body. This can paradoxically cause muscle weakness (the muscles become constantly stimulated and fail). A useful memory aid for the symptoms is the **SLUDGE** acronym:

| Letter | Symptom |
|---|---|
| **S** | Salivation (excessive drooling) |
| **L** | Lacrimation (watering eyes) |
| **U** | Urination (urgency or incontinence) |
| **D** | Defaecation (urgency, diarrhoea) |
| **G** | GI distress (nausea, cramps, vomiting) |
| **E** | Emesis (vomiting) |

Additional signs include **muscle twitching and fasciculations**, **small (constricted) pupils**, and **slow heart rate (bradycardia)**.

This is distinct from a **myasthenic crisis** (too little acetylcholine effect — no SLUDGE symptoms). The distinction matters because treatments are opposite: cholinergic crisis requires dose *reduction* (and sometimes atropine to block excess cholinergic activity), while myasthenic crisis requires urgent immunotherapy.

**Signs that your dose may be too high:** If your gastrointestinal side effects are very severe, or if you notice increased muscle twitching, discuss with your doctor before increasing your dose further.

## Other Symptomatic Approaches

### 3,4-Diaminopyridine (3,4-DAP)
- Sometimes used in Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome (LEMS) and certain types of congenital myasthenic syndromes (CMS)
- Works by increasing the release of acetylcholine from the nerve (pre-synaptic effect)
- **Not typically used in autoimmune MG** but mentioned here for completeness

### Lifestyle Adjustments as Symptomatic Management
- **Timing of activities** around peak medication effect
- **Rest** — the more you rest, the more you can do
- **Temperature management** — avoiding overheating (heat worsens acetylcholinesterase function)
- **Pacing** — spreading activities through the day rather than pushing through fatigue

These are explored further in [Living with MG](living-with-mg.md) and [Diet and Lifestyle](diet-lifestyle.md).
