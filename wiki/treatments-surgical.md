# Thymectomy — Surgical Treatment for MG

Thymectomy is the surgical removal of the thymus gland. For many people with AChR antibody-positive MG, it is one of the most important long-term treatments available — offering the potential for sustained improvement or even complete stable remission.

---

## What is the Thymus, and Why Remove It?

The **thymus** is a small gland in the chest, behind the breastbone, that plays a central role in training the immune system. In AChR-positive MG, the thymus is believed to be the site where the breakdown in immune tolerance begins — where T-cells that attack the body's own acetylcholine receptors are generated and activated, rather than eliminated.

Approximately **75% of people with AChR-positive MG** have an abnormal thymus:
- **65%** have thymic hyperplasia — an overactive thymus with active antibody-producing germinal centres
- **10–15%** have a thymoma — a tumour of the thymus gland

By removing the thymus, it is hoped that the antigenic stimulus driving antibody production is reduced. While this does not immediately or completely switch off the autoimmune process (autoreactive cells have already left the thymus), it appears to tip the balance over time, allowing the immune attack to gradually diminish.

Research published in 2024–2025 has identified an additional mechanism: **medullary thymic epithelial cells (mTECs)** in the MG thymus overproduce a signalling molecule called **TSLP (Thymic Stromal Lymphopoietin)**, which actively impairs the development of regulatory T-cells (Tregs — the immune system's peacekeepers). Thymectomy removes not just the source of self-attacking cells, but also this ongoing source of functionally defective regulatory cells — which helps explain why its benefits can persist for many years after surgery.

## Who Should Have Thymectomy?

### Strongly Recommended

**People with a thymoma:**
- Surgical removal of the thymoma is **always necessary**, regardless of MG status
- Thymomas can become malignant (cancerous) and must be removed
- This is both a cancer treatment and an MG treatment

**People with AChR antibody-positive generalised MG who:**
- Are fit for surgery
- The upper age limit has traditionally been around 65, but **2025 long-term data confirmed that thymectomy continues to provide therapeutic benefit in patients aged 50 and above** — challenging the previous assumption that the thymus was immunologically inert by middle age. The age threshold is therefore less rigid than previously thought, and discussion with your neurologist is important if you are older.

### Evidence Base

The landmark **MGTX trial** (published in the New England Journal of Medicine) provided robust evidence that thymectomy in non-thymomatous, AChR-positive generalised MG significantly improves clinical outcomes over 3 years — and these benefits continue for at least **5 years**. Participants who had thymectomy:
- Needed **lower doses of prednisone**
- Had **better quality of life** scores
- Were more likely to achieve minimal manifestation status or complete stable remission

### When to Do It

Current guidance suggests thymectomy is most beneficial when performed **within the first 2–3 years** of diagnosis, before the disease becomes more established. However, the timing decision is complex and individual.

### Less Clear Benefit

- **Late-onset MG** (over 65): The thymus has often involuted by this age, and there is less clear evidence of benefit, though some centres still consider it
- **MuSK-positive MG**: The thymus is typically normal in MuSK MG, and thymectomy is generally not recommended
- **Ocular-only MG**: Evidence is less clear; may be considered in some centres
- **Seronegative MG**: May benefit, but evidence is less robust

## The Surgical Approach

### Minimally Invasive Techniques (Standard of Care)

Minimally invasive surgery is now standard in most specialist centres:

- **Video-Assisted Thoracoscopic Surgery (VATS)**: Small incisions in the side of the chest, camera-guided removal of the thymus
- **Robotic-Assisted Surgery**: Similar to VATS but using robotic instruments for greater precision
- Benefits over open surgery: **shorter hospital stay**, less pain, faster recovery, smaller scars

### Open Surgery (Median Sternotomy)

- Involves opening the chest through the breastbone
- Now less commonly performed for uncomplicated thymectomy
- May still be necessary for very large thymomas or when extensive resection is required

## What to Expect

### Before Surgery
- Full pre-operative assessment, including lung function tests and heart tests
- Your MG should ideally be as well controlled as possible before surgery — poorly controlled MG increases the surgical risk
- You may be given IVIG or plasmapheresis before surgery to stabilise your condition
- Your surgical team must know about all your medications, including pyridostigmine

### After Surgery
- Recovery period typically **1–3 days** in hospital for minimally invasive surgery; longer for open surgery
- Activity is restricted for several weeks while the chest heals
- Some people experience a temporary worsening of MG symptoms in the immediate post-operative period — this is normal and managed by your medical team
- MG medications are usually continued; adjustments may be made during recovery

### Timeline of Benefits

Thymectomy is **not a quick fix**. The immune system changes gradually:
- Benefits typically become apparent over **months to years**
- Some people achieve improvement or stabilisation within 6–12 months
- Maximum benefit may not be seen for **2–5 years** after surgery
- A significant proportion of people reduce or eliminate their need for immunosuppressants over time

### Realistic Expectations

Outcomes vary considerably between individuals:
- Some achieve **complete stable remission** (no symptoms, no medications)
- Many experience **significant improvement** in symptoms and reduction in medication requirements
- A minority see little benefit
- Response is generally harder to predict in late-onset MG
- Response is less clear in MuSK-positive MG

Factors associated with better outcomes include: younger age, shorter disease duration, AChR antibody-positive status, and the absence of thymoma.

---

## A Note on Thymectomy and Cancer Risk

There has been discussion in the medical literature about whether removing the thymus in adults might lead to increased risk of certain cancers or autoimmune conditions in later life. However, this remains controversial — expert commentators have challenged the methods and conclusions of studies suggesting this risk. The benefits of thymectomy for appropriately selected MG patients are well established, and the decision should be made with your specialist neurologist.

---

## Questions to Ask Your Surgeon and Neurologist

- Am I a candidate for thymectomy based on my MG type and antibody status?
- What surgical approach would be used?
- What are the risks and benefits specific to my situation?
- What outcome can I realistically expect?
- How will my MG medications be managed around the time of surgery?
- What monitoring will I need after surgery?
