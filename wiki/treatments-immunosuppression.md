# Immunosuppression for MG

Immunosuppressant treatments target the underlying autoimmune disease in MG. Rather than just compensating for reduced acetylcholine (as pyridostigmine does), they aim to reduce or suppress the immune attack on the neuromuscular junction. These treatments are slower to work than symptomatic treatments — often taking weeks to months to reach full effect — but they address the root cause of the problem.

Most people with generalised MG will need at least one immunosuppressant. The goal is always the **lowest dose that maintains good disease control**, to minimise side effects. Recent **2024-2025 clinical guidelines** (including ABN 2025 and Nordic 2024) have shifted towards earlier introduction of steroid-sparing agents and targeted biologics to reduce the long-term steroid burden.

---

## Corticosteroids (Prednisolone / Prednisone)

Corticosteroids are potent anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressant drugs, and remain the **most widely used** first-line immunosuppressant in MG worldwide.

### How They Work
They broadly suppress immune system activity, reducing production of the autoantibodies that attack the NMJ and dampening the inflammatory environment in the thymus.

### Clinical Course & Dosing
- **Dosing strategy:** Current 2025 guidelines now prefer **daily dosing** over the older alternate-day regimens, as it provides more stable symptom control.
- **Maintenance goal:** The long-term goal is the lowest effective dose, ideally aiming for **<7.5 mg/day** of prednisolone.
- Most people begin to improve within **2–4 weeks**.
- Maximum benefit is usually seen over **3–6 months**.
- A **temporary worsening** of symptoms can occur in the first 1–3 weeks after starting — this is known as the **"steroid dip"**, affecting approximately **30–50%** of patients. It is more likely in people with significant bulbar or respiratory involvement; some neurologists initiate steroids during a brief hospital admission for safety.

### Contraindications & Precautions
- **Absolute contraindications:** Systemic fungal infections or known hypersensitivity to the drug.
- **Relative contraindications (requiring careful management):** 
    - Uncontrolled diabetes (steroids raise blood sugar significantly)
    - Severe hypertension
    - Active infections (e.g., active Tuberculosis or systemic viral infections)
    - Severe psychiatric disorders (history of depression or psychosis can be aggravated)

### Side Effects of Long-Term Steroid Use
This is the main limitation of steroids. Long-term use carries significant risks, which is why the goal is always to find the lowest effective dose:

- **Weight gain** and redistribution of body fat (moon face, central obesity)
- **Osteoporosis** (bone thinning) and **avascular necrosis** (bone death due to low blood supply) — vitamin D, calcium, and bisphosphonates are usually required.
- **Metabolic changes:** Raised blood glucose (risk of diabetes) and fluid retention.
- **High blood pressure**
- **Ocular effects:** Cataracts and glaucoma — annual eye exams are essential.
- **Infection risk:** Increased susceptibility to infections, including opportunistic infections.
- **Skin changes:** Thinning skin, easy bruising, slow wound healing, and acne.
- **Gastrointestinal:** Increased risk of peptic ulcers, especially if used alongside NSAIDs (like ibuprofen).
- **Mood changes:** Insomnia, anxiety, or mood swings.
- In children: growth suppression

### Pregnancy
Prednisolone is generally considered the **safest immunosuppressant** for MG during pregnancy, though it does carry an increased risk of gestational diabetes and preeclampsia for the mother.

### Bone Protection Protocol

Everyone taking prednisolone at **>7.5 mg/day for more than 3 months** should receive bone protection. Your doctor should prescribe:

- **Calcium** (1000–1200 mg/day) — from diet or supplements
- **Vitamin D3** (800–1000 IU/day)
- **Bisphosphonate** (e.g., alendronate 70 mg once weekly or risedronate) to actively protect bone density — unless there is a specific reason not to
- A **DEXA bone density scan** at baseline and every 1–2 years

If you are on long-term steroids and have not been prescribed these, raise it with your doctor or specialist nurse.

### Monitoring
Regular monitoring is mandatory for those on long-term steroids:
- **Blood pressure & Weight:** Monthly initially.
- **Blood Glucose/HbA1c:** Every 3–6 months.
- **Bone Density (DEXA):** Every 1–2 years (see bone protection protocol above).
- **Ophthalmology Exam:** Annual checks for cataracts and intraocular pressure.

## Steroid-Sparing Immunosuppressants

These agents allow steroid doses to be reduced while maintaining immune control. They are generally slower to work than steroids (often 6–18 months for full effect) and are typically added alongside steroids to facilitate a gradual "steroid taper."

### 2024-2025 Clinical Practice Trends
Current management (per 2024-2025 international consensus and ABN 2025 guidelines) prioritises:
- **Early IST Introduction:** Starting a non-steroidal agent (like azathioprine or mycophenolate) early in the disease course to minimize cumulative steroid exposure.
- **Biologic Escalation:** For patients who do not achieve "Minimal Manifestation" status after 12 months on a conventional IST, early transition to **targeted biologics** (e.g., efgartigimod, ravulizumab) is now preferred over switching between multiple conventional agents.
- **MuSK-Specific Strategy:** **Rituximab** is firmly established as the early-line therapy for MuSK-antibody positive MG, often bypassing conventional agents entirely.

---

### Azathioprine (Brand: Imuran)
The most widely used first-line non-steroidal agent globally.

- **Mechanism of Action:** A purine analogue that is converted to 6-mercaptopurine in the liver. It inhibits DNA and RNA synthesis, specifically blocking the proliferation of T-cells and B-cells.
- **Onset of Effect:** Very slow; clinical benefit manifests in **6–18 months**.
- **Side Effects:**
    - **Common:** Nausea, vomiting, and "flu-like" hypersensitivity syndrome (fever, malaise) in ~10%.
    - **Serious:** Leukopenia (low white blood cells), hepatotoxicity (elevated liver enzymes in ~23%), and a small increased risk of non-melanoma skin cancer and lymphoma with long-term use.
- **Contraindications:** Hypersensitivity to azathioprine or 6-mercaptopurine.
- **Drug Interactions:**
    - **Allopurinol or Febuxostat:** **CRITICAL INTERACTION.** These drugs inhibit xanthine oxidase (the enzyme that breaks down azathioprine metabolites), leading to life-threatening bone marrow toxicity. If unavoidable, reduce AZA dose by 75%.
    - **ACE Inhibitors:** May increase the risk of leukopenia.
    - **Warfarin:** Azathioprine may decrease the anticoagulant effect.
- **Monitoring Requirements:**
    - **Pre-treatment:** **Mandatory TPMT (thiopurine methyltransferase)** testing to identify patients at high risk for severe myelosuppression.
    - **Ongoing:** Full Blood Count (FBC) and Liver Function Tests (LFTs) (weekly for 4–8 weeks, then every 3 months once stable).
- **Special Populations:**
    - **Pregnancy:** Generally considered the safest steroid-sparing agent if therapy is essential.
    - **Elderly:** May require lower starting doses due to age-related changes in metabolism and renal clearance.

---

### Mycophenolate Mofetil (Brand: CellCept)
Frequently used as a first-line alternative to azathioprine, especially for its faster onset.

- **Mechanism of Action:** Selectively and reversibly inhibits inosine monophosphate dehydrogenase (IMPDH), the rate-limiting enzyme in *de novo* purine synthesis. Since lymphocytes rely almost exclusively on this pathway, it selectively blocks T- and B-cell proliferation.
- **Onset of Effect:** Faster than azathioprine; improvement typically seen within **3–6 months**.
- **Side Effects:**
    - **Common:** Gastrointestinal upset (diarrhoea in ~14%, nausea, stomach cramps) — taking the dose with food can help.
    - **Serious:** Increased risk of opportunistic infections (e.g., CMV, BK virus) and progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML).
- **Contraindications:** Known hypersensitivity; **Pregnancy** (absolute contraindication).
- **Drug Interactions:**
    - **Antacids (containing Al/Mg):** Decrease mycophenolate absorption.
    - **Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs):** May reduce drug levels.
    - **Rifampin:** Decreases mycophenolate levels.
- **Monitoring Requirements:** Baseline and periodic FBC and LFTs.
- **Special Populations:**
    - **Pregnancy & Lactation:** **Highly Teratogenic.** Must be stopped at least 6 weeks before conception. Female patients must use two forms of reliable contraception and participate in a risk management program (REMS).
    - **Elderly:** No specific dose adjustment but monitor closely for GI tolerability.

---

### Tacrolimus (Brand: Prograf, Envarsus)
Gaining prominence as a second-line agent, particularly for rapid steroid-sparing and in specific patient groups.

- **Mechanism of Action:** A calcineurin inhibitor that binds to FKBP12, suppressing T-cell activation by inhibiting the production of interleukin-2 (IL-2).
- **Onset of Effect:** Rapid; clinical benefit can be observed as early as **1–2 months**.
- **Side Effects:**
    - **Common:** Tremor, headache, and paraesthesia (tingling).
    - **Serious:** Nephrotoxicity (kidney damage), hypertension, and new-onset diabetes after transplant (NODAT/hyperglycemia).
- **Contraindications:** Hypersensitivity to tacrolimus or macrolide antibiotics.
- **Drug Interactions:** **CYP3A4 Substrate.** 
    - **Inhibitors (e.g., Grapefruit juice, Erythromycin, Azole antifungals):** Significantly increase levels, leading to toxicity.
    - **Inducers (e.g., St. John's Wort, Phenytoin, Rifampin):** Decrease levels, leading to disease relapse.
- **Monitoring Requirements:** Requires **Therapeutic Drug Monitoring (TDM)**. Target trough levels are typically **5–10 ng/mL**. Regular monitoring of renal function (Creatinine), blood pressure, and blood glucose.
- **Special Populations:**
    - **Pediatric (Juvenile MG):** Evidence from 2025 meta-analyses confirms its effectiveness and safety in children.
    - **Elderly:** Often preferred for rapid steroid tapering in the elderly to avoid steroid-related fractures and metabolic issues.

---

### Ciclosporin (Brand: Neoral, Sandimmum)
Similar to tacrolimus, but reserved for refractory cases due to a more challenging side-effect profile.

- **Mechanism of Action:** Calcineurin inhibitor that blocks T-cell activation via cyclophilin binding, inhibiting IL-2 production.
- **Onset of Effect:** Moderate; typically **2–4 months**.
- **Side Effects:**
    - **Common:** Gingival hyperplasia (gum overgrowth), hirsutism (increased hair growth), and tremor.
    - **Serious:** Significant risk of nephrotoxicity and severe hypertension.
- **Contraindications:** Impaired renal function (Creatinine > 50% above baseline), uncontrolled hypertension, or malignancy.
- **Drug Interactions:** Similar to Tacrolimus (CYP3A4 interactions). Avoid grapefruit juice and St. John's Wort. Note: Use **brand-name prescribing** (e.g., Neoral) to ensure consistent bioavailability.
- **Monitoring Requirements:** Frequent monitoring of renal function, blood pressure, and trough drug levels.
- **Special Populations:**
    - **Pregnancy:** Permissible if therapy is essential, though associated with lower birth weight.
    - **Renal Impairment:** Strictly contraindicated in patients with CrCl < 50 mL/min.

---

### Methotrexate
An effective alternative for patients who do not tolerate azathioprine or mycophenolate.

- **Mechanism of Action:** A folate antagonist that inhibits dihydrofolate reductase, interfering with DNA synthesis and cellular repair in immune cells.
- **Onset of Effect:** Slow; clinical response typically takes **6–10 months**.
- **Dosing:** Administered **once weekly**, always accompanied by **Folic Acid (5 mg)** on a different day to reduce mucosal and liver toxicity.
- **Side Effects:**
    - **Common:** Fatigue (reported in ~18%), nausea, and stomatitis (mouth ulcers).
    - **Serious:** Hepatotoxicity, pulmonary fibrosis (rare), and bone marrow suppression.
- **Contraindications:** Pregnancy, breastfeeding, chronic liver disease, and severe renal impairment.
- **Drug Interactions:**
    - **NSAIDs:** High-dose NSAIDs can decrease MTX excretion, increasing toxicity.
    - **Trimethoprim/Sulfamethoxazole:** Increases risk of severe bone marrow suppression.
- **Monitoring Requirements:** Baseline and regular FBC, LFTs, and renal function.
- **Special Populations:**
    - **Pregnancy:** **Highly Teratogenic and Abortifacient.** Must be avoided by both women and men planning to conceive (discontinue 3–6 months prior).
    - **Hepatic Impairment:** Contraindicated in active liver disease.

## Monitoring on Immunosuppressants

All immunosuppressant medications require regular monitoring. Your doctor or specialist nurse will arrange:

- Regular **blood tests** (full blood count, liver function, kidney function, as appropriate to your medication)
- **Blood pressure** monitoring
- **Bone density** checks if on long-term steroids
- Assessment for **infection** — be vigilant about reporting new infections promptly
- Regular **skin checks** for skin cancer — immunosuppressants increase the risk of skin cancer (see [Skin Care on Immunosuppressants](skin-immunosuppressants.md))

---

## Reducing Medications

Once MG is well controlled, your neurologist may gradually reduce your immunosuppressant doses. This process must be done **slowly and carefully** — reducing too quickly can trigger a relapse. Do not reduce or stop any MG medication without medical supervision.
