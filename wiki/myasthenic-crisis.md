# Myasthenic Crisis

A myasthenic crisis is a medical emergency. It occurs when the muscles that control breathing become so weak that the person can no longer breathe adequately on their own. Without urgent treatment, it can be fatal. With prompt medical care, most people recover fully.

Understanding what a crisis looks like, what triggers it, and what to do is essential knowledge for anyone with MG and for their family and carers.

---

## What is a Myasthenic Crisis?

A myasthenic crisis is defined as a **severe exacerbation of MG leading to respiratory failure** — the breathing muscles (diaphragm and intercostal muscles) become too weak to sustain adequate breathing. This may require:

- **Intubation** (a tube inserted into the airway)
- **Mechanical ventilation** (a machine to breathe for the person)
- Intensive care admission

It is the most dangerous complication of MG and represents a **life-threatening emergency**.

**How common is it?** Approximately **15–20% of people with MG** will experience at least one crisis at some point in their illness. Up to 50% of those may not be able to identify a specific trigger.

---

## Warning Signs — Act Early

A crisis rarely develops instantly. There is usually a period of worsening symptoms before breathing fails. Recognising this early allows time to seek help before the situation becomes critical.

**Warning signs that a crisis may be developing:**

- **Increasing shortness of breath**, especially when lying flat
- Difficulty breathing deeply enough — a feeling of not being able to fill the lungs
- **Worsening swallowing** — difficulty with liquids as well as solids; choking or coughing with drinks
- **Very weak or absent voice** — being unable to speak above a whisper
- **Extreme generalised weakness** — much worse than usual, affecting arms, legs, and neck
- Rapid breathing rate
- Using accessory muscles to breathe (visible effort of neck and shoulder muscles during breathing)
- Unusual anxiety or agitation — sometimes a sign of inadequate oxygen

**If you or someone with MG is experiencing these symptoms:**

> **Call 999 (or your local emergency number) immediately. Do not wait to see if things improve. Tell emergency services that the person has myasthenia gravis.**

---

## Common Triggers of Crisis

Many crises are triggered by an identifiable event. Common triggers include:

- **Infection** — particularly **respiratory infections** (colds, flu, chest infections, COVID-19). Even a seemingly minor infection can trigger a significant exacerbation. Treat infections promptly
- **Surgery or anaesthesia** — physical stress and certain drugs used in surgery can worsen MG; always inform the anaesthetic team of your MG diagnosis
- **Certain medications** — some drugs worsen MG and can trigger a crisis. See [Drugs to Avoid](drugs-to-avoid.md). The most dangerous is **telithromycin (Ketek)**, which must never be used in MG
- **Emotional or physical stress**
- **Extreme heat** — high temperatures can worsen acetylcholinesterase enzyme function, reducing the effectiveness of the nerve signal
- **Tapering immunosuppressants too quickly** — reducing treatment too fast can allow the immune attack to rebound
- **Pregnancy or the postpartum period** — hormonal changes and the physical demands of pregnancy and delivery can destabilise MG
- **Stopping MG medications abruptly** — never stop pyridostigmine or other MG medications suddenly without medical advice

## What Happens in Hospital

Once admitted as an emergency:

**Assessment and monitoring:**
- Continuous monitoring of breathing (oxygen levels, breathing rate)
- Assessment of swallowing safety
- Measurement of **vital capacity** (lung function) — a key indicator of respiratory reserve
- Blood tests and investigation for triggers (especially infection)

**If breathing is compromised:**
- **Non-invasive ventilation** (a tight-fitting mask that assists breathing) may be tried if the situation is not yet critical
- **Intubation and mechanical ventilation** if breathing cannot be maintained safely

**Treating the crisis:**
- **IVIG** (intravenous immunoglobulin) or **plasmapheresis (PLEX)** — to rapidly reduce circulating antibodies. These are the core treatments for a myasthenic crisis (see [Acute Treatments](treatments-acute.md))
- **Pyridostigmine** may be paused or reduced during a crisis — too much acetylcholine can worsen breathing (a cholinergic effect)
- **Immunosuppressants** may be adjusted once the person is stabilised
- Treatment and control of any triggering infection

**Recovery:**
Most people recover from a myasthenic crisis with good medical care, though recovery may take **weeks to months**, particularly after prolonged ventilation. Rehabilitation with physiotherapy and occupational therapy is often needed.

## Distinguishing Myasthenic Crisis from Cholinergic Crisis

Two types of crisis can look similar but have opposite treatments:

| Feature | Myasthenic Crisis | Cholinergic Crisis |
|---|---|---|
| Cause | Too little acetylcholine effect (disease worsening) | Too much acetylcholine (too much pyridostigmine) |
| Pupils | Normal or dilated | Constricted (small) |
| Sweating | Normal | Excessive |
| Saliva/secretions | Normal | Excessive |
| Muscle twitching | May be present | More pronounced fasciculations |
| Bowel/bladder | Normal | Diarrhoea, increased urination |

If there is any doubt, **seek emergency medical help** — getting this distinction wrong can be dangerous.

## Prevention — Reducing Crisis Risk

Many crises are preventable with good management:

- **Treat infections promptly** — do not wait and see. Report any new infection to your medical team, especially if you feel your MG is also worsening
- **Know your drugs to avoid** — carry a list and ensure all healthcare providers see it before prescribing anything new (see [Drugs to Avoid](drugs-to-avoid.md))
- **Never stop MG medications abruptly** — always seek medical advice before any medication change
- **Keep your immunosuppression stable** — taper doses only as advised by your neurologist
- **Inform surgical teams** well in advance of any planned procedure
- **Carry a medical ID** — a Myaware Patient Passport or medical alert card identifying your MG and your medications
- **Manage heat exposure** — avoid overheating; use cooling strategies in hot weather
- **Know your warning signs** — recognise the early symptoms of a deterioration and seek help before it becomes a crisis
- **Flu and COVID-19 vaccination** — immunosuppressed patients should discuss appropriate vaccinations with their medical team

---

## For Carers and Family Members

If you live with or care for someone with MG:

- **Learn the warning signs** of a crisis and take them seriously
- Know the person's diagnosis, medications, and any drugs to avoid
- Keep emergency contact numbers for their neurologist and neurology department
- Be ready to call emergency services promptly — **do not delay**
- After a crisis, be aware that psychological recovery may take time. The experience can be frightening for everyone involved
