# Living with MG

Living with myasthenia gravis means adapting — adapting your routines, your expectations, and your relationship with your body. It is not always easy, but with the right approach and the right support, the majority of people with MG live full and meaningful lives. This page brings together the most practical advice for day-to-day life.

> **"I have myasthenia, it doesn't have me."** — a sentiment shared by many in the MG community

---

## Accept — and Adapt

One of the most important shifts in living well with MG is accepting that there will be good days and bad days. This is not giving up — it is being realistic. A realistic and flexible approach allows you to make the most of good periods without being devastated by the bad ones.

MG treatments generally work well, and MG does not necessarily get worse over time. It probably will not be curable in the short term, so the best strategy is to learn your condition, adapt your lifestyle to accommodate it, and keep talking to your medical team about optimising treatment.

---

## Pace Yourself

This is the single most important practical skill for living with MG.

- **The more you rest, the more you can do.** Rest is not wasted time — it is recovery time that enables activity
- **Plan regular rest periods** throughout the day — even 10–20 minute rests between activities can restore significant strength
- **Spread activities** through the day rather than doing everything in one go
- **Prioritise**: identify the things that matter most and focus your energy on those; let go of things that matter less
- **Use a diary** to track your energy, symptoms, and when you feel strongest

---

## Focus Activities at Your Peak Time

Most people with MG are stronger earlier in the day and after taking their medication. Use this strategically:

- **Schedule demanding tasks** — important phone calls, appointments, physical activities — for when you are at your strongest, typically mid-morning after pyridostigmine has taken effect
- **Avoid saving the hardest things for later in the day** when you are likely to be more fatigued
- Let people know when you are at your best if you need to discuss important matters

---

## Listen to Your Body

Learning to recognise the early signs of fatigue is vital — this is when symptoms are likely to be triggered. The goal is to stop and rest **before** you reach the point of exhaustion, rather than after.

- **Do not push through severe weakness** — this can lead to a worse crash and even trigger an exacerbation
- If you notice your symptoms worsening (voice going, swallowing becoming harder, vision blurring, limbs becoming weak), that is the signal to rest
- It is a balance, and it takes time to learn — but achieving it is worth the effort

---

## Keep Warm, Keep Cool

Temperature extremes affect MG symptoms significantly:

- **Heat worsens MG** — hot weather, hot baths, fever, and overheating can all temporarily make symptoms worse. Acetylcholinesterase enzyme works less efficiently at higher temperatures
  - Practical strategies: cooling vests, fans, air conditioning, cool drinks, avoiding the hottest part of the day for outdoor activities, cooler bath or shower water
  - **Ice or cool packs** applied gently to closed eyelids can give temporary relief from ptosis in the heat
  - See [Diet and Lifestyle](diet-lifestyle.md) for detailed hot-weather tips including cooling foods, hydration, medication management, and workplace rights
- **Cold can also affect symptoms** in some people — dress in layers and avoid prolonged cold exposure

---

## Eat Well, Rest Well

- A well-balanced diet supports overall health (see [Diet and Lifestyle](diet-lifestyle.md) for detailed guidance)
- **Eat little and often** to maintain energy — large meals can be exhausting to eat and may cause a post-meal dip in energy
- If chewing or swallowing is difficult, choose softer foods
- Take pyridostigmine 30–45 minutes before meals if mealtimes are a challenge

---

## Exercise Wisely

When MG is well controlled, exercise is encouraged and beneficial. When it is not well controlled, be gentle:

- **Within limits, not beyond them** — exercise at a level that does not exceed your current capability
- Build up gradually as control improves
- Tai Chi, gentle yoga, swimming, and walking are often well suited to people with MG
- Regular gentle exercise supports muscle tone, cardiovascular health, and mental wellbeing

See [Diet and Lifestyle](diet-lifestyle.md) for more detail.

---

## Manage Stress

Stress can worsen MG — both physical stress and emotional stress. While it is impossible to eliminate stress entirely, there are things that can help:

- **Relaxation techniques**: mindfulness, meditation, and breathing exercises can be helpful
- **Talking**: sharing how you feel with someone you trust — a friend, family member, or support group — can lighten the load
- If stress or anxiety is significantly affecting your life, your GP can help with referral to talking therapies or other support
- **Myaware** (UK) offers emotional support: contact support@myaware.org

---

## Carry a Medical ID

- Always carry a **Patient Passport** or similar medical alert card that identifies your MG diagnosis and lists key medications and drugs to avoid
- In the UK, the Myaware Patient Passport is a personalised card provided to members that can be shown to any medical professional — including paramedics in an emergency
- A medical alert bracelet can also be helpful

This is particularly important in emergency situations where you cannot speak for yourself.

---

## Stay Connected

You are not alone. MG can feel isolating, especially during bad periods — but there is a large community of people who understand exactly what you are going through.

- **Myaware** (UK's dedicated MG charity) offers support, information, and community: www.myaware.org
- Online communities and social media groups provide connection and shared experience
- Peer support from others with MG can be invaluable — finding strategies that work, normalising the experience, and simply feeling understood

---

## Keep a Diary

Keeping a diary of your symptoms, energy levels, activities, and medication timing is useful for several purposes:

- Helps you identify patterns — when you feel better or worse, what triggers worsening
- Provides useful information for your neurologist at appointments
- Can be very helpful when applying for disability benefits or adaptations
- Helps you track the effect of any medication changes
- Myaware provides a diary for members

---

## Home Adaptations and Practical Aids

Small changes to your home can make a real difference:

- **Grab rails** in the bathroom and on stairs
- **Raised toilet seat** if getting up is difficult
- **Kitchen aids**: jar openers, electric tin openers, lightweight pots and pans
- **Seating**: having chairs at the right height throughout the home; a perching stool in the kitchen
- **Eye patching**: one eye covered can eliminate double vision and allow you to read or watch TV
- **Recliner chairs** for rest periods
- **Bed support**: adjustable beds or extra pillows can help with overnight breathing comfort

Benefits and welfare advice may be available to help fund adaptations. In the UK, Myaware has a benefits and welfare officer who can advise.

---

## Swallowing Difficulties — Practical Tips

- Eat slowly, take small mouthfuls, and chew thoroughly
- Choose soft, moist foods
- Sit upright during meals and for 30 minutes afterwards
- Sip water between mouthfuls if needed
- Report significant difficulties to your medical team — aspiration is a risk that should be taken seriously

---

## Work and Employment

Many people with MG are able to work, though some adaptations may be needed. Consider:

- **Discussing your condition with your employer** under the disability provisions of employment law
- **Reasonable adjustments**: flexible hours, the ability to rest during the day, working from home, reduced physical demands
- Energy management: knowing that employment requires pacing just like any other activity

If MG significantly affects your ability to work, disability benefits may be available — seek advice from Myaware's welfare team or a benefits advisor.

---

## Mental Health

The psychological impact of living with a chronic, unpredictable condition should not be underestimated. Anxiety and depression are more common in people with MG than in the general population — and they are not a weakness; they are a natural response to a challenging situation.

- **Acknowledge how you feel** — it is okay to find this hard
- **Seek support early** — speaking to your GP, a counsellor, or a support helpline before things become very difficult is much easier than trying to manage alone
- **Connect with others** who understand — peer support is powerful
- **Stay positive where you can** — a bright outlook genuinely helps many people manage MG better, though this should not mean suppressing legitimate worries
- **The MG community** understands in a way others may not

---

## A Little Help from Friends and Family

Accept help when it is offered — this is not weakness, it is wisdom. Having supportive, understanding, and patient people in your life makes an enormous difference. Helping others understand MG — what it is, why you can do something one day and not the next, and what they can do to help — is part of building a good support network.
