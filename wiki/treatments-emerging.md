# Emerging and Experimental Treatments for MG

The treatment pipeline for MG is exceptionally active — reflecting the condition's clear autoimmune mechanism, the availability of well-validated clinical trial outcome measures, and growing pharmaceutical interest in rare disease research. This is an exciting time for MG. Several treatments that were experimental just a few years ago are now approved; others in the pipeline today may transform care within the next decade.

---

## CAR-T Cell Therapy

Chimeric Antigen Receptor T-cell (CAR-T) therapy is one of the most exciting frontiers in MG treatment. Originally developed for blood cancers, CAR-T involves engineering a patient's own T-cells to target and destroy specific immune cells.

### The "Immune Reset" Paradigm (2023–2025)

Between 2023 and 2025, a major shift occurred in how CAR-T is viewed for autoimmunity. Rather than just "killing cancer," the goal in MG and other autoimmune diseases is a **total immune reset**. By deeply depleting CD19+ B-cells or BCMA+ plasma cells, researchers aim to remove the autoreactive memory cells. Once the CAR-T cells diminish, the B-cell compartment repopulates with **naïve B-cells**, effectively "rebooting" the immune system without the "memory" of the attack on the AChR.

### Descartes-08 (BCMA-targeted mRNA CAR-T)

This is one of the most anticipated developments in MG therapy.

- Unlike traditional CAR-T therapies (which permanently engineer T-cells and require dangerous lymphodepleting chemotherapy), Descartes-08 uses **mRNA to transiently (temporarily) engineer T-cells** to target **BCMA (B-Cell Maturation Antigen)** — a protein found on plasma cells that produce antibodies
- The mRNA degrades after a few days, so the T-cells only have CAR activity temporarily — making this approach much **safer and less toxic** than standard CAR-T
- **Does not require lymphodepleting chemotherapy**
- Phase 2 trial results showed meaningful and sustained reductions in symptom burden in refractory MG
- **Phase 3 AURORA trial results (2025)** showed sustained remission in patients with refractory disease — a major milestone

### KYV-101 (CD19-targeted CAR-T) — Kyverna Therapeutics

- Targets CD19, depleting B-cells broadly (a similar mechanism to inebilizumab, but using engineered T-cells rather than a monoclonal antibody)
- Unlike Descartes-08, uses conventional lentiviral CAR-T technology — requires lymphodepletion conditioning before infusion
- Received **FDA Regenerative Medicine Advanced Therapy (RMAT) designation** in late 2024, accelerating its development pathway
- **KYSA-6 Phase 2/3 data (2025):** A single treatment with KYV-101 produced **drug-free remission** in a subset of MG patients, with restored Treg balance observed at follow-up — the first strong clinical evidence that the immune system can genuinely be "reset" in this disease
- The mechanism: eliminating essentially all B-cells forces the immune system to rebuild from scratch; in doing so it re-establishes a more balanced immune repertoire that, critically, includes a healthier regulatory T-cell population

### CABA-201 (CD19-targeted CAR-T) — Cabaletta Bio

- A separate CD19-targeted CAR-T programme (distinct from Descartes-08, which targets BCMA)
- Aims to achieve long-term sustained remission through complete B-cell depletion and immune reconstitution
- Currently in the **RESET-MG Phase 1/2 trial**; includes both AChR+ and MuSK+ patients
- Represents a potential "functional cure" approach — one treatment course could offer years of remission

### In Vivo CAR-T (The 2025 Frontier)

A major emerging trend in 2025 is the development of **In Vivo CAR-T therapy**. Unlike current CAR-T (which requires removing a patient's cells, engineering them in a lab, and re-infusing them), this approach uses **mRNA-loaded lipid nanoparticles (LNPs)** — similar to COVID-19 vaccine technology — to deliver the CAR instructions directly to T-cells *inside* the patient's body.

- **Potential for "Off-the-Shelf" treatment**: Could be administered as a simple injection in a clinic
- **Reduced Toxicity**: Creates transient CAR activity, potentially avoiding the severe cytokine release syndrome seen with permanent CAR-T
- **Democratising Access**: If successful, would significantly lower the cost and complexity of immune-resetting therapies

## Gene Therapy

Early-stage research is exploring several gene therapy approaches for MG:

### AAV-Based Decoy Receptors
- Gene therapy using adeno-associated viruses (AAV) to deliver "decoy" AChR proteins to tissues
- The decoy receptors would absorb circulating autoantibodies before they reach the real receptors at the neuromuscular junction, effectively neutralising the attack
- Preclinical research stage

### Gene Editing
- Approaches to modify B-cells to prevent them from producing pathogenic antibodies
- Could offer a more permanent solution if successful

### Neonatal Tolerisation
- Research into whether the immune system could be re-educated to tolerate AChR as a "self" protein
- Would aim to correct the fundamental immune recognition error that underlies MG
- A pilot investigation of an AChR peptide mimic approach has been completed with promising safety data

---

## Inebilizumab (Brand Name: Uplizna)

While now approved (FDA approval December 2025), inebilizumab represents a step beyond rituximab in B-cell targeting:

- Targets **CD19** rather than CD20
- CD19 is expressed on a broader range of B-cells, including some plasma cells that have lost CD20 expression — meaning it may deplete antibody-producing cells that rituximab misses
- Convenient **twice-yearly dosing** — a major practical advantage
- Currently being adopted into practice for generalised MG

---

## Satralizumab (IL-6 Inhibitor)

- An inhibitor of **IL-6**, an inflammatory cytokine
- Currently in **Phase 3 clinical trial** for both MuSK and AChR antibody-positive MG
- IL-6 signalling supports B-cell differentiation and antibody production; blocking it may reduce the autoimmune attack
- Already approved for neuromyelitis optica spectrum disorder, another antibody-mediated neurological condition

---

## Inverse Checkpoint Inhibitors (Checkpoint Agonists)

This represents a entirely new class of therapy emerging in 2023–2025. While traditional "checkpoint inhibitors" (like anti-PD-1) are cancer drugs that *unleash* the immune system (and are a known **cause** of trigger-induced MG), **Checkpoint Agonists** aim to *activate* these inhibitory signals to turn the immune system **off**.

### Peresolimab (PD-1 Agonist)

- A first-in-class monoclonal antibody that stimulates the **PD-1 receptor** — the "off-switch" on T-cells
- In 2023, Phase 2a results published in the *New England Journal of Medicine* showed significant clinical efficacy in refractory Rheumatoid Arthritis
- Represents a **paradigm shift** toward restoring immune balance rather than simply suppressing the whole system
- Clinical evaluation for broader autoimmune applications is ongoing as of 2025

### Rosnilimab (PD-1 Agonist / Depleter)

- A more complex approach that both stimulates PD-1 and selectively depletes the T-cells that express high levels of PD-1
- Achieved a major Phase 2b win in **Rheumatoid Arthritis** (early 2025)
- Under evaluation for other T-cell mediated autoimmune conditions
- **Crucial Distinction**: Patients must not confuse these *agonists* (which treat autoimmunity) with the *inhibitors* (which treat cancer but can cause MG)

---

## Next-Generation FcRn Inhibitors

### IMVT-1402 (Successor to Batoclimab)

Batoclimab showed promising Phase 3 results but was found to raise cholesterol and lower albumin levels — limiting its clinical acceptability. Development has now shifted to **IMVT-1402**, an engineered next-generation FcRn inhibitor that retains the antibody-lowering efficacy of batoclimab without the metabolic side effects.

- Phase 2/3 trials ongoing
- If successful, would add another self-injectable FcRn inhibitor option with a potentially cleaner safety profile

---

## Complement Pathway Research — Beyond C5

All currently approved complement inhibitors target C5 (at the bottom of the complement cascade). Research is now exploring **C3 inhibitors** — which block complement *upstream* of C5 and may offer broader inhibition of the complement-mediated damage at the NMJ. These remain investigational.

---

## Telitacicept (BAFF/APRIL Dual Inhibitor)

- A fusion protein that inhibits both **BAFF (BLyS)** and **APRIL** — two survival factors that B-cells and plasma cells depend on
- By targeting both factors simultaneously, telitacicept may deplete antibody-producing cells more completely than anti-CD20 or anti-CD19 approaches, while avoiding the direct cell-killing mechanisms of those agents
- Phase 2 trials in MG ongoing, predominantly in Asia; emerging data
- Under **Phase 3 evaluation** for MG

---

## Cladribine

- A synthetic compound that inhibits B-cell (and T-cell) replication
- A pilot study suggested potential efficacy in MG
- **Phase 3 trial is planned**

---

## CAR-Treg Therapy

A step further than conventional CAR-T, **CAR-Treg** therapy involves engineering **regulatory T-cells** (Tregs) — the immune system's own "peacekeepers" — with chimeric antigen receptors to specifically suppress the autoimmune response targeting the AChR. Rather than destroying immune cells (as CAR-T does), this approach aims to restore the body's own immune self-regulation.

- Very early-stage; primarily preclinical work
- Could theoretically offer a more "natural" route to tolerance without the risks of broad immune depletion
- **SBT-7771 (CAR-Treg)** — Sonoma Biotherapeutics is advancing CAR-Tregs that migrate to specific tissues (like the joints in RA) and secrete anti-inflammatory cytokines; Phase 1 trials are proving the feasibility of this "cell-based peacekeeper" approach as of 2024
- **QEL-001 (CAR-Treg)** — Quell Therapeutics is developing similar Treg-based approaches specifically designed to prevent organ rejection and treat T-cell mediated autoimmunity

---

## Allogeneic Tr1 Cell Therapy

- **TRX319 (Tr1X Bio)** — Developing "off-the-shelf" **Type 1 regulatory (Tr1) cells**. Unlike patient-derived CAR-T, these are allogeneic (ready-to-use) and designed to directly suppress B-cell mediated autoimmunity without the need for patient-specific engineering
- Phase 1 trials initiated in 2024–2025

## Targeted T-Cell Modulation

### Non-depleting Anti-CD4

Research in 2023–2024 has focused on using **non-depleting anti-CD4 antibodies**. Unlike traditional drugs that kill T-cells, these antibodies simply bind to the CD4 receptor on helper T-cells to **induce tolerance**. This re-educates the immune system to accept the acetylcholine receptor as "self" again, potentially offering a long-term solution without compromising the body's ability to fight infection.

### Low-dose IL-2 (Iltolizumab, NKTR-358)

Low-dose **interleukin-2 (IL-2)** therapy exploits the fact that Tregs express far higher levels of the high-affinity IL-2 receptor (CD25) than effector T-cells. At carefully calibrated low doses, the signal is strong enough to activate Treg receptors but falls below the threshold needed to activate inflammatory cells — a selectivity window that makes therapeutic Treg expansion possible.

**Iltolizumab (ILT-101)** — The landmark **TRANSREG "basket" trial** (2024) administered iltolizumab across patients with 13 different autoimmune diseases simultaneously. Treg expansion was universally achieved across all 13 conditions, validating the mechanism and establishing that the selective effect works regardless of the specific autoimmune condition. Phase 2 trials specifically targeting refractory MG are now underway.

> **Note on naming:** Iltolizumab (the low-dose IL-2) is a completely different drug from itolizumab (an anti-CD6 antibody). The names are easily confused in the literature.

**Rezpegaldesleukin (NKTR-358, Nektar Therapeutics)** takes an engineered approach: the IL-2 molecule is modified with PEG chains that physically block the region that binds to low-affinity receptors on inflammatory cells, making it structurally incapable of activating effector T-cells regardless of dose. It also has a much longer half-life (7–13 days), allowing fortnightly dosing. 2025 Phase 2b results:
- **REZOLVE-AD** (atopic dermatitis): Met its primary endpoint with high statistical significance; FDA Fast Track designation granted
- **REZOLVE-AA** (alopecia areata): Significant hair regrowth in treated patients; core biological data confirmed substantial Treg expansion

Success across multiple conditions has accelerated interest in applying this approach to MG specifically.

## Restoring the EPO-STING Balance: "Brake and Accelerator" Therapy

Based on the 2025 Stanford research, a new category of "precision tolerance" therapies is emerging. These aim to simultaneously apply the immune system's natural "brake" (EPO) and release the inflammatory "accelerator" (STING).

### Targeted Tolerance via the EPO Axis
EPO signaling in **cDC1 dendritic cells** is required to induce the production of Regulatory T-cells (Tregs) that suppress autoimmune attacks. 
- **The Concept**: Future therapies may involve "turning the EPO switch on" in specific dendritic cells to re-establish tolerance to self-proteins like the AChR.
- **CNP-106 (Nanoparticle Tolerance)**: This approach uses nanoparticles coated with AChR peptides to present these antigens in a context that promotes tolerance rather than attack — effectively "teaching" the immune system to recognize AChR as self again by activating the tolerogenic cDC1 pathway.

### STING-Targeting Therapies: "Turning Off the False Alarm"
The STING protein (Stimulator of Interferon Genes) becomes chronically overactive in autoimmune diseases. 2025 research has provided new ways to target this "accelerator":
- **STING PROTACs**: These represent a superior strategy to traditional inhibitors. Instead of just blocking the protein's activity, PROTACs flag overactive STING for complete **degradation** by the cell's own waste-disposal system.
- **STING Oligomerization Blockers**: 2025 breakthroughs revealed that human STING requires a specific clumping step to become active. New agents are being developed to block this step, offering a more precise treatment for the "false alarm" inflammation that drives chronic MG damage.

## Antigen-Specific Approaches (Tolerance Induction)

One of the most exciting long-term goals in MG research is restoring **immune tolerance** — re-teaching the immune system to stop attacking the AChR — rather than broadly suppressing immunity.

- Approaches include oral, nasal, or subcutaneous administration of AChR or AChR peptide fragments to induce tolerance
- Some success has been achieved in animal models
- A pilot human investigation of an AChR peptide mimic has been completed with a good safety profile
- An engineered T-cell approach targeting MuSK-specific B-cells (expressing a MuSK chimeric antibody receptor with CD137-CD37) is in Phase 1 dose-finding evaluation
**CNP-106 (COUR Pharmaceuticals)** is the most advanced antigen-specific tolerance approach in MG. It uses biodegradable nanoparticles loaded with **seven specific AChR protein fragments** that are naturally cleared by the liver and spleen. In these organs, immune cells encounter the AChR fragments during routine cellular cleanup — a non-inflammatory context that triggers tolerance rather than attack, promoting the development of AChR-specific Tregs.

Key milestones:
- A multicenter **Phase 1b/2a clinical trial (NCT06106672)** began dosing patients in late 2024, evaluating safety and impact on clinical MG scores
- **FDA Orphan Drug Designation** was granted in October 2025
- Research published in *Science Advances* (early 2026) confirmed the mechanism depends on the **STING/type I interferon pathway** to induce T-cell tolerance — explaining how liver and spleen processing creates tolerance rather than inflammation

**MuSK-CAART (Cabaletta Bio)** — For patients with **MuSK-positive MG**, an even more targeted approach called **CAAR-T therapy** is in development. Rather than engineering T-cells to eliminate all B-cells, CAAR-T cells carry the MuSK protein on their surface as bait. Only B-cells that specifically target MuSK will bind to them and be destroyed — every other B-cell in the body is left completely untouched. This is arguably the most surgically precise immune intervention ever attempted in autoimmune disease. MuSK-CAART is in the **MusCAARTes Phase 1 trial** and has received **FDA Fast Track designation**.

These approaches represent a fundamentally different treatment paradigm — targeting the underlying immune error rather than managing its consequences.

## Metabolic Targeting: ABHD11 Inhibitors

A 2025 discovery (Nature Communications) identified that overactive T-cells in autoimmune disease are driven by a mitochondrial protein called **ABHD11**, which keeps them in a state of permanent high metabolic activity — effectively preventing them from ever "switching off." 

The mechanism involves a **"Sterol Shunt"** pathway: when ABHD11 is inhibited (for example, by small molecules such as **ML-226**), the compromised TCA cycle leads to an accumulation of acetyl-CoA and lactate. This activates the regulator SREBP2, driving the synthesis of **24,25-epoxycholesterol (24,25-EC)**. This molecule acts as a ligand for the **Liver X Receptor (LXR)**, which ultimately **suppresses T-cell effector functions**, significantly reducing the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines like IFN-γ and TNF.

By targeting this pathway, researchers aim to selectively "cool down" only the overactive T-cells that drive autoimmune attacks, while leaving resting immune cells needed for normal defence intact. ABHD11 inhibitors represent a genuinely new mechanism of action for T-cell targeted immunotherapy and are in early-stage development as of 2025–2026.

## Thymic Targeting: TSLP Blockade

Research published in 2024–2025 identified that **medullary thymic epithelial cells (mTECs)** in the MG thymus overproduce **TSLP (Thymic Stromal Lymphopoietin)**, a signalling molecule that impairs the development of regulatory T-cells within the thymus itself. This excess TSLP causes a **functional defect** in Tregs, rendering them less capable of suppressing autoimmune responses.

TSLP-blocking antibodies — including **tezepelumab** — are being investigated as a way to restore normal Treg maturation in MG. This approach would address the disease at its thymic origin rather than targeting downstream immune activity. 

- **Tezepelumab (Tezspire)**: Already FDA-approved for severe asthma. Major trials (WAYFINDER and COURSE, 2024–2025) have demonstrated its efficacy in reducing "upstream" inflammation and corticosteroid dependence, providing a clear safety profile and proof-of-concept for its potential repurposing in MG. 
- **Emerging Pipeline (2025)**: Other TSLP-targeting agents, such as **HBM9378** and **CM326**, are in active development (Phase 1/2) as of 2025, reflecting a broader industry shift toward targeting the thymic/epithelial drivers of autoimmunity.

## Stem Cell Transplantation (HSCT)

- **Haematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT)** is being explored in very severe, refractory cases as a means of "resetting" the immune system
- Involves ablating (destroying) the patient's existing immune system with chemotherapy and rebuilding it from stem cells
- Carries significant risks and is only considered in extreme cases
- Limited data available; promising early results in small case series

## Biomarker Research

A major goal in MG research is identifying **biomarkers** — biological signs — that can:
- Predict which patients will respond to which treatments (treatment-predictive markers)
- Diagnose MG earlier and more accurately (diagnostic markers)
- Monitor disease activity more precisely (disease activity markers)

Currently, antibody levels do not reliably correlate with clinical severity. Better biomarkers would enable personalised treatment selection and reduce the current problem of patients trying multiple treatments before finding what works.

Studies of genetic polymorphisms, metabolomics, and miRNA profiles have identified some promising treatment-predictive markers — but further validation is needed before these can guide clinical practice.

---

## The Bigger Picture

The field of MG treatment has transformed in just a decade. A decade ago, the main options were pyridostigmine, steroids, azathioprine, and IVIG/plasmapheresis for crises. Today, there are multiple approved biological therapies, with more arriving. The next decade may bring:

- CAR-T therapies that offer remission without long-term immunosuppression
- Antigen-specific tolerance approaches that target the underlying immune error
- Personalised medicine guided by biomarkers — so the right treatment is chosen first time
- Treatments available to broader groups of patients, including those currently excluded from trials

For the latest information on clinical trials, visit [clinicaltrials.gov](https://clinicaltrials.gov/) and search for "myasthenia gravis".
