# Immune System, T-Cells, and Autoimmune Disease: Latest Research (2023–2026)

*Research compiled: April 2026. Focuses on findings from 2023–2026 relevant to autoimmune neuromuscular disease, with specific connections to Myasthenia Gravis (MG).*

---

## Overview

The period from 2023 to 2026 has produced landmark advances in understanding why the immune system attacks the body's own tissues. The field has moved decisively away from the older model of autoimmunity as simply an "overactive" immune system toward a far more nuanced picture: a series of specific molecular failures in the mechanisms that normally maintain immune self-restraint. For Myasthenia Gravis patients in particular, these advances are translating into a new generation of targeted treatments that go beyond symptom management toward genuine disease modification and, in some cases, drug-free remission.

---

## Part 1: How Autoimmune Disease Works — The Immune System's Role

### The Normal Immune Balance

The immune system must perform two contradictory tasks simultaneously: aggressively attack foreign pathogens while leaving the body's own tissues untouched. It achieves this through a layered system of "checkpoints" and "peacekeepers."

**Central tolerance** occurs in the thymus, where developing T-cells are tested against the body's own proteins. Those that react too strongly against self-tissue are deleted. This process, called negative selection, eliminates approximately 95% of developing T-cells.

**Peripheral tolerance** operates throughout the rest of the body, using regulatory mechanisms to suppress any self-reactive T-cells that escaped the thymus. This second line of defense is increasingly understood as the key failure point in most autoimmune diseases.

### New Understanding of Autoimmune Triggers (2023–2026)

Recent research has identified several molecular "master switches" whose disruption can initiate autoimmune disease:

**The EPO Immune Switch (2025 — Stanford University)**
A major discovery revealed that Erythropoietin (EPO), long known only for its role in red blood cell production, is actually a master regulator of immune tolerance. EPO signaling in type 1 conventional dendritic cells (cDC1s) is required to activate Regulatory T-cells (Tregs). When this signaling pathway is disrupted, dendritic cells transform into "super-stimulators" that drive autoimmune attacks. This finding opens a new avenue for understanding why autoimmune disease can be triggered by seemingly unrelated stressors that affect EPO signaling.

**STING Protein Overactivity (2025 — published in Cell)**
The STING (Stimulator of Interferon Genes) protein is designed to detect viral DNA. New research identified that when STING becomes chronically overactive, it misidentifies normal cellular debris and self-DNA as threats, releasing a continuous stream of inflammatory cytokines. This "false alarm" cascade is now recognised as a significant driver of chronic autoimmune damage and is an active drug target.

**The Microbiome Connection (2023–2024)**
High-confidence evidence has established a direct link between microbiome dysbiosis — particularly a lack of early-life bacterial diversity — and the induction of autoantibodies. Specific environmental exposures (mercury, silica, certain infections) can also trigger the formation of autoantibodies in genetically susceptible individuals. The gut microbiome appears to influence the thymus itself, affecting which T-cells survive development.

**Efferocytosis Failure (2024)**
When cells die normally, the body rapidly clears the debris. New insights show that when this "clean-up" process (efferocytosis) is impaired, self-antigens linger in tissues far longer than normal, eventually overwhelming the immune system's tolerance mechanisms and provoking a sustained autoimmune response. This has particular relevance at sites of tissue turnover, including the neuromuscular junction.

## Part 2: T-Cell Dysfunction in Autoimmune Disease

### What Goes Wrong Inside T-Cells

Modern research has shifted from simply counting T-cells to understanding their internal "wiring." The discoveries of 2023–2026 have been particularly striking in revealing specific molecular brakes that, when broken, allow T-cells to attack healthy tissue.

### The QRICH1 Brake (2025)

Scientists identified QRICH1 as a critical intracellular brake for CD8+ cytotoxic T-cells. When this protein is deficient or overridden, T-cells become hyper-responsive and aggressively destroy healthy tissue. This discovery is significant because it identifies a cell-specific target — blocking or enhancing QRICH1 in specific T-cell populations may allow selective suppression of damaging cells without compromising general immunity.

### Metabolic Rewiring: The ABHD11 Discovery (2025 — Nature Communications)

Overactive T-cells in diseases such as Type 1 Diabetes, and by extension other autoimmune conditions, are driven by a protein called ABHD11 in the mitochondria. This protein keeps T-cells in a permanent state of high metabolic activity — effectively revving the engine continuously. This "metabolic rewiring" sustains the autoimmune attack because the T-cells never enter the normal resting states that would normally terminate an immune response. ABHD11 inhibitors are now being investigated as a way to "cool down" only overactive T-cells while leaving normal immune function intact.

### T Helper Cell Imbalance: Th1/Th17 vs. Th2

A well-established finding confirmed across multiple 2023–2025 studies is the imbalance between pro-inflammatory T helper subsets. In most organ-specific autoimmune diseases including MG:

- **Th1 cells** (driven by the cytokine IFN-gamma) promote cellular autoimmune attacks
- **Th17 cells** (driven by IL-17) promote antibody production and inflammatory tissue damage
- **Th2 cells** (driven by IL-4 and IL-13) are typically suppressed

Recent research has added nuance to this picture by identifying "pathogenic Th17" cells — a subset that expresses both Th17 and Th1 markers and appears to be particularly destructive in neuromuscular disease.

### CD4+ T Helper Cells and Autoantibody Production

In MG specifically, CD4+ T helper cells play a central role because they provide the "help" that B-cells need to produce AChR antibodies. Without CD4+ T-cell assistance, the B-cells cannot sustain high-affinity antibody production. This explains why treatments targeting B-cells (such as rituximab) are effective — they eliminate B-cells, but the underlying T-cell dysfunction remains and will eventually regenerate a new population of antibody-producing B-cells unless the T-cell compartment is also addressed.

### CD8+ T-Cells: More Than Cytotoxic Killers

Recent data suggests CD8+ T-cells play a more complex role in autoimmune disease than previously recognised. In MG, CD8+ T-cells have been found infiltrating thymic tissue and the neuromuscular junction itself, where they may directly damage tissue and also interact with CD4+ cells to amplify the autoimmune response. The identification of the QRICH1 brake as specific to CD8+ cells opens targeted intervention possibilities.

## Part 3: Regulatory T-Cells (Tregs) and Immune Tolerance

### What Tregs Do

Regulatory T-cells (Tregs) are the immune system's dedicated peacekeepers. They are defined by expression of the transcription factor FOXP3 and function primarily by suppressing the activity of other immune cells through:

- Direct cell-to-cell contact (expressing CTLA-4 and PD-1, which deliver "stop" signals)
- Secretion of suppressive cytokines (IL-10, TGF-beta, IL-35)
- Metabolic deprivation of effector T-cells (consuming IL-2 from the local environment)

A healthy Treg population creates what researchers call "infectious tolerance" — the process by which Tregs actively convert other T-cells to a tolerant state, spreading self-restraint throughout the immune system.

### Landmark Discoveries in Treg Biology (2024–2025)

**FOXP3 Recognition (2025)**
The discovery and characterisation of FOXP3 as the master regulator of Treg identity was recognised with the 2025 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine. This recognition reflects a decade of work confirming that virtually all Treg function depends on FOXP3 stability and expression levels.

**The Egr-1 Switch (2025)**
Scientists identified Egr-1 as a critical molecular switch that enhances FOXP3 expression. When Egr-1 activity is supported, Tregs maintain their suppressive identity. When it is suppressed — as occurs during periods of inflammation or stress — Tregs begin to lose their function. Natural compounds that activate Egr-1 (such as Calycosin, investigated in 2025 studies) are now being explored as Treg-stabilising agents.

**The PRDM1-S/SGK-1 Destabilisation Circuit (2024)**
In many autoimmune conditions including MG, Tregs overexpress a protein called PRDM1-S. This triggers the enzyme SGK-1, which directly destabilises FOXP3 at a biochemical level. The result is that Tregs "flip" from protective cells into pro-inflammatory cells — actively worsening the disease they were meant to prevent. This finding explains observations that Tregs in MG patients are not merely absent or insufficient in number, but may be actively counterproductive.

**GRAIL Protein Loss and Treg Instability (2024)**
The GRAIL (RNF128) protein is required to maintain Treg identity under inflammatory conditions. Without it, Tregs lose their suppressive characteristics and convert into conventional effector T-cells. Inflammatory environments — including the thymic tissue seen in MG — actively promote GRAIL loss, creating a cycle where inflammation destroys the Tregs meant to stop it.

**Selective Suppression Mechanism (Science, 2025)**
A key study revealed exactly how healthy Tregs target only self-reactive immune cells while leaving normal immunity intact. The mechanism relies on Tregs preferentially accumulating at sites where self-antigens are presented and selectively competing with self-reactive T-cells for survival signals. In autoimmune disease, this selectivity breaks down and Tregs either suppress too broadly (contributing to immune deficiency) or fail to suppress at all (allowing autoimmune damage to continue).

### Tregs in Myasthenia Gravis: Specific Findings

**Functional Rather Than Numerical Deficiency**
Multiple recent studies confirm that MG patients often have near-normal numbers of Tregs in their blood, yet their suppressive capacity is severely compromised. They fail to effectively control AChR-specific effector T-cells. This explains why simply trying to boost Treg numbers is insufficient — the quality and stability of the Treg population must also be addressed.

**Thymic Origin of the Problem**
2025 research into the MG thymus revealed that Thymic Epithelial Cells (TECs) — the cells responsible for educating both T-cells and Tregs during their development — overproduce a signalling molecule called TSLP (Thymic Stromal Lymphopoietin). Excess TSLP interferes with the maturation of Tregs in the thymus, producing cells that are inherently unstable before they ever reach the bloodstream. This finding helps explain why thymectomy (surgical removal of the thymus) is therapeutically effective in MG and why its benefits can persist for decades after the surgery.

**Th17/Treg Imbalance**
An elevated ratio of Th17 (inflammatory) to Treg (suppressive) cells is now considered a reliable biomarker of active MG disease. New work from 2024–2025 has characterised the specific cytokine environment in MG thymic tissue that drives this imbalance, identifying IL-6 and IL-21 as key drivers that simultaneously promote Th17 development and suppress Treg stability.

## Part 4: What Triggers Autoimmune Disease — Latest Theories

### The "Multiple Hit" Model (Refined 2023–2025)

Current consensus supports a multiple hit model in which autoimmune disease requires the convergence of several factors:

1. **Genetic predisposition** — HLA genes (particularly HLA-DQ and HLA-DR variants) that determine which self-antigens are presented to T-cells and how strongly they respond
2. **Epigenetic priming** — Environmental exposures that alter how genes are expressed without changing DNA sequence, often establishing risk in early life
3. **Triggering event** — An infection, hormonal change, significant physiological stress, or toxic exposure that tips a primed system into active disease
4. **Failure of resolution** — The immune system's normal post-infection "off switch" fails to terminate the immune response

### Molecular Mimicry: Refined Evidence (2024)

The molecular mimicry hypothesis — that immune responses to infectious agents accidentally target self-proteins with similar shapes — has received significant new support from bioinformatic studies comparing pathogen and human proteomes. In MG, several pathogens including certain herpes viruses and bacteria carry short peptide sequences that closely resemble portions of the AChR. New structural immunology work has confirmed that T-cell receptors raised against these pathogen peptides can indeed recognise AChR-derived peptides.

### Cryptic Antigen Exposure

Recent work highlights the role of "cryptic" antigens — protein fragments that are normally hidden within cells and only exposed when cells are damaged or die abnormally. In MG, evidence points to abnormal thymic cell death exposing normally hidden portions of AChR proteins, which then trigger an immune response that, once established, is maintained by the genuine AChR at the neuromuscular junction.

### The Stress-Autoimmunity Connection (2024)

Research has better characterised how physiological stress (infection, surgery, physical trauma, psychological stress) triggers autoimmune flares. The mechanism involves stress hormones (particularly cortisol and adrenaline) temporarily disrupting the FOXP3 methylation patterns that stabilise Tregs, causing a transient drop in suppressive capacity precisely when inflammatory signals are high. This molecular explanation aligns with the well-documented clinical observation that MG patients often experience flares after infections, surgery, or significant physical or emotional stress.

## Part 5: Emerging Treatments Targeting T-Cells

### From Broad Suppression to Precise Targeting

Traditional immunosuppression (corticosteroids, azathioprine, mycophenolate) works by reducing immune activity broadly. This is effective but creates significant side effects and leaves patients vulnerable to infection. The 2023–2026 period has seen a decisive shift toward treatments that target specific immune cell populations or molecular pathways.

### CAR-T Cell Therapy for Autoimmune Disease

Originally developed for cancer, CAR-T (Chimeric Antigen Receptor T-cell) therapy has been adapted for autoimmune disease in a conceptually different way. Rather than targeting cancer cells, it is used to deplete specific B-cell and plasma cell populations that produce pathogenic antibodies.

**KYV-101 (CD19-targeted CAR-T, Kyverna Therapeutics)**
In 2025, Phase 2/3 clinical trial data (KYSA-6) showed that a single treatment with KYV-101 produced drug-free remission in a subset of MG patients, with restored Treg balance observed at follow-up. The treatment works by eliminating essentially all B-cells, forcing the immune system to "reboot" from scratch — and in doing so, re-establishing a more balanced immune repertoire that, critically, includes a healthier Treg population.

**Descartes-08 (mRNA CAR-T, Cartesian Therapeutics)**
This approach uses mRNA rather than viral vectors to produce CAR-T cells, meaning the engineered cells are transient and self-limiting. Phase 2b data showed sustained symptom reduction with a safer profile than traditional CAR-T, making it potentially more suitable for a non-life-threatening condition where the risk-benefit calculation is different from cancer treatment.

### CAR-Treg Therapy: Engineering Peacekeepers

A conceptually different CAR-T approach engineers Regulatory T-cells rather than cytotoxic ones. CAR-Tregs are designed to home to specific tissues (such as the neuromuscular junction) and deliver targeted suppression without depleting the immune system broadly. This approach is primarily in preclinical and early Phase 1 stages for autoimmune disease, but early results are encouraging. The challenge is maintaining the stability of the Treg phenotype in the highly inflammatory environment of active disease — exactly the problem identified by the GRAIL and PRDM1-S/SGK-1 research described above.

### IL-2 Pathway Targeting: Selective Treg Expansion

IL-2 (interleukin-2) is the primary growth signal for Tregs. However, IL-2 also activates effector T-cells and NK cells, meaning simple IL-2 supplementation would worsen autoimmune disease rather than improve it.

**Low-dose IL-2 (Iltolizumab, NKTR-358)**
Low-dose IL-2 exploits the fact that Tregs are exquisitely sensitive to IL-2 signalling (expressing much higher levels of the high-affinity IL-2 receptor CD25) compared to effector T-cells. Phase 2 clinical trials in 2024–2025 have shown that carefully calibrated low-dose IL-2 or engineered IL-2 "mimetics" can selectively expand Tregs while avoiding the activation of inflammatory cells. This represents one of the most promising approaches for restoring immune balance without broad immune suppression.

### Metabolic Targeting: ABHD11 Inhibitors

The discovery of ABHD11 as a driver of T-cell metabolic overdrive in autoimmune disease has opened a new therapeutic avenue. By inhibiting this mitochondrial protein specifically in activated T-cells, researchers aim to selectively reduce the energetic sustaining of autoimmune attacks without affecting resting immune cells. ABHD11 inhibitors are in early-stage development as of 2025–2026.

### Antigen-Specific Tolerance Induction

The most targeted approach to treatment aims to induce tolerance specifically to the self-antigen being attacked, leaving all other immune responses intact.

**CNP-106 (Nanoparticle Tolerance)**
This therapy uses nanoparticles coated with AChR peptides to present these antigens in a context that promotes tolerance rather than attack. By delivering AChR antigens via the STING pathway in a non-inflammatory context, the therapy aims to "teach" the immune system to recognise AChR as self again. This approach is conceptually the closest to a genuine cure and is in preclinical and early clinical development.

### Targeting the Thymic Problem

Since the thymus appears to be the origin of the dysfunctional Tregs in MG, researchers are exploring interventions that address thymic health directly.

**TSLP Blockade**
Given the finding that excessive TSLP production by thymic epithelial cells impairs Treg development in MG, blocking TSLP signalling is being investigated as a way to restore normal Treg maturation. TSLP-blocking antibodies (such as tezepelumab, already approved in asthma) are candidate agents for repurposing.

**Thymectomy Evidence Expanded**
Long-term data from 2025 confirmed that thymectomy continues to provide therapeutic benefit even in patients aged 50 and above — challenging the previous assumption that the thymus was immunologically inert by middle age. The mechanism appears to be removal of the ongoing source of dysfunctional Tregs and AChR antigen presentation.

## Part 6: Connections to Myasthenia Gravis — Summary

MG provides an unusually clear window into autoimmune mechanisms because its target antigen (the acetylcholine receptor at the neuromuscular junction) is well-characterised and testable. The latest research has deepened understanding of MG pathogenesis in several ways:

**The Disease is More Than an Antibody Problem**
While AChR antibodies cause the muscle weakness, the underlying disease is a T-cell regulatory failure. The antibodies are a symptom of this failure. This is why treatments targeting only antibodies (such as plasma exchange or complement inhibitors like eculizumab) improve symptoms but do not modify the underlying disease.

**The Thymus as the Epicentre**
MG increasingly looks like a disease that originates in the thymus, where abnormal AChR antigen presentation and impaired Treg maturation create a lifelong susceptibility to autoimmune attack. The thymic microenvironment in MG patients is characterised by:
- Abnormal expression of AChR by thymic epithelial cells
- Excess TSLP production impairing Treg development
- Germinal centre formation (normally found in lymph nodes, not the thymus) producing autoreactive B-cells in situ

**The Immune Signature of Refractory MG**
A distinct immune profile has been identified in patients with treatment-resistant MG: low circulating Treg numbers combined with high numbers of memory B-cells. This profile predicts which patients are unlikely to respond to conventional immunosuppression and may need early intervention with more aggressive reset therapies such as CAR-T.

**Flare Mechanisms Clarified**
The molecular pathway by which stress, infection, and surgery provoke MG flares is now better understood: stress hormones destabilise FOXP3 methylation in Tregs, causing transient Treg dysfunction precisely when inflammatory signals are at their highest. This explains the clinical pattern and suggests that protecting Treg stability during known high-risk periods (planned surgery, infection, significant stressors) could reduce flare risk.

## Implications for People Living with MG

While much of this research is still translating from bench to bedside, several practical implications are already apparent:

1. **Treatments are becoming more targeted.** The pipeline of T-cell and Treg-focused therapies means that within 5–10 years, patients may have access to treatments that address the root cause rather than just the symptoms.

2. **The stress-immune connection is real and molecular.** Protecting Treg stability through stress management, adequate sleep, and rapid treatment of infections is not merely psychological — it acts on specific molecular pathways that determine disease activity.

3. **Biomarkers for disease severity are improving.** The Th17/Treg ratio and memory B-cell counts are emerging as clinically useful indicators of disease activity and treatment response.

4. **Drug-free remission is becoming achievable.** Early CAR-T data showing durable remission in MG patients provides the first strong evidence that the immune system can genuinely be "reset" in this disease.

5. **Thymectomy remains underutilised.** Evidence continues to support thymectomy as a disease-modifying intervention, and the newer data extending its benefit to older patients suggests its use should be considered more broadly.
