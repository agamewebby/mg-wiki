# Nutrition and Diet in Autoimmune Disease — Wahls Protocol, Anti-Inflammatory Diets, and Myasthenia Gravis

*Research compiled: 2026-04-12*
*Queries: 8 focused research areas*

---

## Executive Summary

1. **Vitamin D and omega-3 fatty acids have the strongest clinical evidence** for autoimmune disease management. The VITAL trial (2022, n=25,871) found that Vitamin D (2,000 IU/day) reduced new autoimmune disease incidence by 22% and omega-3 supplementation by a further 15% over five years.

2. **The gut-immune axis is central to MG pathology.** MG patients show consistent dysbiosis — reduced butyrate-producing bacteria (*Clostridia*, *Bifidobacterium*, *Lactobacillus*) — which impairs the SCFA-driven induction of Foxp3+ Regulatory T-cells (Tregs), the key "peacekeepers" that are deficient or dysfunctional in active MG.

3. **The Mediterranean diet is the best-supported overall dietary pattern** for autoimmune disease. It reduces IL-6, TNF-alpha, and CRP, restores the Th17/Treg ratio, and is specifically recommended for MG patients based on its gut and immune effects.

4. **Ketogenic diets show emerging but compelling evidence**, particularly through beta-hydroxybutyrate (BHB) inhibiting the NLRP3 inflammasome and mTOR pathway, suppressing Th17 cells, and stabilising Foxp3 expression. The Mya-Keto RCT at Charité Berlin is ongoing (2025–2026).

5. **Patients on long-term corticosteroids require active nutritional management**: bone protection (calcium + Vitamin D), blood sugar monitoring, gut protection, and awareness of specific drug-nutrient interactions — particularly the contraindication of high-dose therapeutic magnesium in MG, and potential azathioprine interaction with high-dose curcumin supplements.

## 1. The Wahls Protocol

### Overview and Three Levels

The Wahls Protocol is a multimodal therapeutic lifestyle intervention developed by Dr. Terry Wahls MD, a clinical professor of medicine at the University of Iowa, who reversed her own secondary progressive Multiple Sclerosis using these dietary principles. It is structured in three escalating levels:

**Level 1 — The Wahls Diet (Modified Paleo)**
- Nine cups of produce daily: 3 cups dark leafy greens (vitamins A, C, K), 3 cups sulfur-rich vegetables (broccoli, cabbage, onions, mushrooms), 3 cups deeply coloured fruits and vegetables (antioxidants and polyphenols)
- Strict elimination of gluten and dairy (identified as inflammatory triggers)
- Emphasis on grass-fed meat and wild-caught fish

**Level 2 — Wahls Paleo**
- Adds organ meats (twice weekly) for concentrated micronutrients, seaweed (once weekly) for iodine, and fermented foods for gut microbiome support
- Eliminates all non-gluten grains (rice, corn), legumes, and white potatoes
- Introduces soaked seeds and nuts

**Level 3 — Wahls Paleo Plus (Ketogenic)**
- A ketogenic adaptation: vegetable intake reduced to 6 cups to maintain ketosis, with heavy emphasis on healthy fats (coconut oil, full-fat coconut milk)
- Incorporates intermittent fasting (12–16 hour windows) and eating twice daily
- Designed to shift the body into fat-burning metabolism

### Scientific Rationale

The protocol targets three biological systems:

**Mitochondrial function:** The protocol provides specific cofactors for the Electron Transport Chain (ETC) to optimise ATP production. This includes B-vitamins, CoQ10, and sulfur — the latter essential for synthesising glutathione, the body's primary antioxidant that protects mitochondria from oxidative damage.

**Myelin support:** To support repair of the myelin sheath (the fatty insulation around nerve fibres), the diet emphasises omega-3 fatty acids (DHA/EPA) from wild fish, iodine and selenium from seaweed, and Vitamin B12 and folate, which are critical for the methylation cycle and synthesis of myelin basic protein.

**Anti-inflammatory mechanisms:** By eliminating common allergens (gluten and dairy) and flooding the system with phytonutrients, the protocol aims to reduce intestinal permeability ("leaky gut") and systemic molecular mimicry — the process whereby the immune system mistakenly attacks host tissue after being exposed to similar-looking foreign proteins.

### Clinical Evidence

- **Pilot Studies (2014–2017):** Early research at the University of Iowa demonstrated that patients with progressive MS following the protocol showed significant reductions in fatigue (Fatigue Severity Scale) and improved gait and quality of life. Evidence quality: uncontrolled pilot studies.

- **The WAVES Trial (2021):** A randomised controlled trial (RCT) comparing the Wahls Protocol to the Swank Diet (the traditional low-saturated-fat MS diet). Both diets produced significant reductions in fatigue and improved quality of life. The Wahls Protocol showed greater improvements in some functional scores and mental health measures. Evidence quality: RCT, though modest sample size.

- **Nutritional Adequacy Study (2019, *Nutrients*):** Confirmed that the Wahls elimination diet meets or exceeds the Recommended Dietary Allowance for most micronutrients, despite eliminating grains and dairy, provided Vitamin D and calcium are supplemented as specified.

The Wahls Protocol is increasingly adopted as a foundational functional medicine approach for other autoimmune conditions — Rheumatoid Arthritis, Lupus, IBD — based on the principle that addressing root-cause mitochondrial and gut health can stabilise immune dysregulation regardless of specific diagnosis.

**Evidence note:** Most clinical data is from MS populations. There are no published RCTs of the Wahls Protocol specifically in Myasthenia Gravis. Its relevance to MG is extrapolated from shared mechanisms (Th17/Treg imbalance, gut dysbiosis, mitochondrial stress).

## 2. Mediterranean Diet and Autoimmune Disease

### Evidence for Inflammation Reduction

Systematic reviews (notably 2021 and a 2023 review in *Autoimmunity Reviews*) have consistently shown that high adherence to the Mediterranean diet — rich in monounsaturated fats (olive oil), omega-3s (oily fish), and polyphenol-rich plants — significantly reduces key inflammatory markers:

- **IL-6 and TNF-alpha:** These cytokines are primary drivers of the inflammatory process in autoimmune conditions. Mediterranean diet adherence is associated with lower circulating levels of both, directly counteracting the mediators elevated during flares.
- **C-Reactive Protein (CRP):** Well-documented reductions in CRP correlate with disease severity improvements in Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA) and Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE).
- **NF-κB pathway:** The EPA and DHA from oily fish inhibit NF-κB (the "master switch" of inflammation), reducing production of IL-1β, TNF-alpha, and IL-6.

### Th17/Treg Effects

A critical feature of autoimmune disease — including MG — is the Th17/Treg imbalance, where pro-inflammatory Th17 cells outweigh suppressive Regulatory T-cells (Tregs). The Mediterranean diet addresses this through several mechanisms:

- **SCFA-mediated Treg induction:** High fibre intake (from vegetables, legumes, and whole grains) promotes the growth of beneficial gut bacteria (*Clostridia*, *Bifidobacterium*). These bacteria ferment fibre into short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), particularly butyrate, which are among the most potent known natural inducers of colonic Foxp3+ Tregs.
- **Th17 suppression:** Mediterranean-pattern eating is associated with lower Th17 activity markers, partly through reducing the IL-6 and IL-21 signals that drive Th17 differentiation.
- **Foxp3 stability:** Nutrients common in this diet — Vitamin D and various polyphenols — support stability of the FOXP3 transcription factor, the "master switch" that keeps Tregs functional and prevents them from flipping into inflammatory cells (a process driven by the PRDM1-S/SGK-1 pathway in active disease).

### Neurological Autoimmune Conditions

While much early data came from RA and SLE, recent research has focused on neurological autoimmunity:

- **Myasthenia Gravis:** 2024–2025 research indicates MG patients often show gut dysbiosis with a reduction in the SCFA-producing bacteria that the Mediterranean diet supports. Adherence is linked to better disease control and a lower Th17/Treg ratio — a reliable biomarker for active MG.
- **Multiple Sclerosis:** Broader neurological studies show that the anti-inflammatory properties of the Mediterranean diet may protect against neurodegeneration by reducing oxidative stress and stabilising the blood-brain barrier, which is compromised by high levels of TNF-alpha and IL-6.
- **Neuromuscular junction (NMJ) protection:** In neuromuscular conditions, pathogenic Th17 cells are particularly destructive. By cooling the systemic cytokine environment, the Mediterranean diet reduces the background inflammatory tone that worsens muscle weakness and fatigue.

| Component | Biological Target | Immune Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Olive oil / nuts | NF-κB pathway | Reduces TNF-alpha, IL-6 |
| Oily fish (omega-3) | IL-17 / Th17 cells | Shifts Th17/Treg ratio toward tolerance |
| High fibre / legumes | Gut SCFAs (butyrate) | Induces Foxp3+ Tregs |
| Polyphenols / vegetables | Oxidative stress | Lowers CRP and systemic inflammation |

## 3. Gut Microbiome, SCFAs, and MG

### Dysbiosis in MG

Studies including Zhou Y et al. (2024) have documented distinct gut microbiome patterns in MG patients compared to healthy controls:

- **Reduced butyrate-producing bacteria:** MG patients show significantly lower populations of beneficial bacteria from the *Clostridia*, *Bifidobacterium*, and *Lactobacillus* genera.
- **Correlation with disease severity:** Lower levels of butyrate-producing bacteria correlate inversely with disease activity — lower populations are associated with more severe symptoms.
- **Pro-inflammatory shift:** A corresponding increase in bacterial populations associated with systemic inflammatory responses contributes to the Th17/Treg imbalance characteristic of MG.

### SCFA-Treg Pathway

The primary mechanism linking gut health to immune function in MG is the SCFA pathway:

- **Fermentation:** SCFAs are produced when beneficial gut bacteria ferment dietary fibre.
- **Treg induction:** Butyrate is one of the most potent known natural inducers of colonic Foxp3+ Regulatory T-cells.
- **Systemic regulation:** Gut-derived Tregs enter the systemic circulation where they suppress the autoantibody-producing B-cells responsible for generating the anti-AChR and anti-MuSK antibodies that drive MG. In MG, this pathway is often impaired, resulting in either insufficient Treg numbers or Tregs prone to "flipping" into pro-inflammatory cells (via the PRDM1-S/SGK-1 pathway).
- **Thymic connection:** The gut microbiome appears to influence the thymus itself, affecting T-cell development and maturation — particularly relevant to MG, in which thymic abnormalities are central to disease pathology.

### Dietary Interventions for Gut Health

Targeted interventions to restore the SCFA-Treg pathway:

- **Mediterranean diet:** Associated with higher circulating Treg counts and lower Th17 inflammatory markers.
- **Prebiotic fibre:** Specifically supports the growth of *Clostridia* and other SCFA-producing species. Diverse plant fibre sources (vegetables, legumes, oats, flaxseed) are preferred over single supplements.
- **Probiotic foods and supplements:** Strains of *Lactobacillus* and *Bifidobacterium* support gut-derived Treg induction, though food-based sources (kefir, yoghurt, fermented vegetables) may be more effective than capsule supplements for systemic immune modulation (see Stanford 2021 study, Section 4).
- **Astragaloside IV (AS-IV):** This compound from the Astragalus herb (used in traditional Chinese medicine) has been shown to act as a prebiotic, selectively enriching SCFA-producing bacteria and driving systemic Treg expansion. Preliminary research; requires more clinical data.
- **Omega-3 fatty acids:** Help reduce IL-17 and TNF-alpha, supporting a healthier Th17/Treg balance independently of the gut route.
- **Vitamin D:** Vital for MG management — it binds to the FOXP3 promoter, directly inducing and stabilising Tregs. Deficiency is highly prevalent in MG cohorts and correlates with higher disease activity.

## 4. Key Anti-Inflammatory Foods and Compounds

### Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA and DHA)

**Mechanism:** Beyond reducing pro-inflammatory prostaglandins, omega-3s produce Specialised Pro-resolving Mediators (SPMs) — resolvins and protectins — that actively switch off inflammation and signal immune clearance and return to homeostasis. They reduce production of IL-6, TNF-alpha, and IL-1β.

**Clinical evidence:**
- VITAL Trial (RCT, n=25,871, 2022): 1g/day EPA+DHA reduced autoimmune disease incidence by 15% over five years.
- Rheumatoid Arthritis: Consistent reductions in joint pain, morning stiffness, and NSAID requirement across multiple meta-analyses.
- Lupus (SLE): Evidence of improved disease activity scores and reduced inflammatory markers.

**Practical note:** Aim for 1–2g combined EPA+DHA daily. Food sources include sardines, mackerel, anchovies, and salmon ("SMASH" fish). Caution at very high doses (>3g/day) with anticoagulant medications.

### Polyphenols (Resveratrol, Quercetin, EGCG)

Polyphenols act as biological response modifiers by targeting intracellular signalling pathways.

**Resveratrol** (grapes, berries, red wine):
- Activates SIRT1, which inhibits NF-κB. Clinical pilot studies in Ulcerative Colitis showed significantly decreased disease activity at 500mg/day. Evidence quality: small RCTs and pilot studies.

**Quercetin** (onions, apples, capers):
- Activates the Nrf2 pathway, enhancing endogenous antioxidant defences. Small RCTs in Rheumatoid Arthritis demonstrated reduced morning pain and stiffness at 500mg/day. Evidence quality: small RCTs.

**EGCG** (green tea):
- Works through epigenetic modulation (DNA methylation) to increase Treg counts. Trials (2024–2026) in Lupus patients showed significant improvements in IL-6 and TNF-alpha levels at 1,000mg/day green tea extract. Evidence quality: small RCTs; more data needed.

### Sulforaphane

**Source:** Broccoli sprouts (highest concentration in 3-day-old sprouts), broccoli, Brussels sprouts, cabbage.

**Mechanism:** The most potent natural activator of the Nrf2 pathway. In autoimmunity, it creates a "hormetic stress" in T-cells that specifically suppresses Th17 cells — the primary drivers of tissue damage in MS, RA, and MG — while leaving other immune functions intact.

**Clinical evidence:** Primarily preclinical for specific autoimmune conditions. Clinical trials in healthy subjects show significant reduction in systemic inflammatory markers and enhanced phase II detoxification. Evidence quality: preclinical strong; human autoimmune data limited.

**Practical note:** Cooking significantly reduces sulforaphane content; broccoli sprouts are most effective raw or lightly steamed.

### Curcumin

**Source:** Turmeric root. Bioavailability is markedly improved when taken with black pepper (piperine) or as a phospholipid complex formulation.

**Mechanism:** Broad-spectrum inhibitor of NF-κB and the NLRP3 inflammasome. Modulates the eicosanoid pathway similarly to some pharmaceutical anti-inflammatories.

**Clinical evidence:**
- Ulcerative Colitis: High-quality evidence as adjunct to mesalamine to maintain remission.
- Rheumatoid Arthritis: Trials indicate efficacy comparable to certain NSAIDs for joint swelling, with fewer GI side effects.

**Safety note for MG patients:** High doses (above 2g/day of curcumin supplements) may inhibit thiopurine S-methyltransferase (TPMT), potentially increasing the toxicity of azathioprine. Culinary use of turmeric is safe; always discuss high-dose supplementation with your neurologist.

### Vitamin D

**Mechanism:** Acts as a steroid hormone through Vitamin D Receptors (VDR) on immune cells. Directly supports FOXP3 expression — critical for stability and function of Tregs. Inhibits differentiation of pro-inflammatory Th1 and Th17 cells. Binds to the FOXP3 promoter to directly induce and stabilise Tregs.

**Clinical evidence:**
- VITAL Trial (RCT, 2022): 2,000 IU/day reduced confirmed autoimmune disease incidence by 22% over five years. This is the single largest clinical trial of dietary supplementation in autoimmune disease.
- MS and RA: Deficiency is a documented risk factor for flares in both conditions.
- MG specific: 2024–2025 research documents widespread Vitamin D deficiency in MG cohorts, with deficiency correlating with higher disease activity and lower Treg stability.

**Practical note:** Aim for serum 25-OH Vitamin D above 75 nmol/L. Patients on long-term corticosteroids should be supplemented at 800–1,000 IU/day as standard bone protection; higher doses may be appropriate based on blood levels. Monitor levels if taking high doses.

### Fermented Foods

**Mechanism:** Rather than simply adding good bacteria, fermented foods increase overall gut microbial diversity. This produces short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) like butyrate, which cross the gut barrier and signal the bone marrow to produce more anti-inflammatory Tregs.

**Clinical evidence:**
- Stanford Study (RCT, 2021): A 10-week intervention with a diet high in fermented foods (6 servings per day: yoghurt, kefir, fermented cottage cheese, kimchi, vegetable brine drinks, kombucha) increased microbiome diversity and decreased 19 different inflammatory proteins, including IL-6. This is notable because a high-fibre diet in the same trial increased microbiome diversity but did not show the same reduction in inflammatory proteins, suggesting fermented foods may be more effective than fibre alone for acute systemic immune modulation.
- Food-based probiotics appear more effective than capsule supplements for systemic immune effects based on this and related evidence.

**Note for MG patients:** Some fermented foods (mature cheese, certain fermented meats) contain high histamine, which may trigger symptoms in histamine-sensitive individuals. Fermented dairy (kefir, yoghurt) also provides calcium beneficial for patients on corticosteroids.

## 5. Elimination Diets in Autoimmune Neurological Disease

### Gluten-Free Diets

The evidence for gluten-free diets (GFD) in autoimmune neurological conditions is mixed and largely context-dependent.

**Key principle:** Before starting a GFD, patients should be tested for coeliac disease (tissue transglutaminase antibody, tTG-IgA) while still eating gluten, as these tests become inaccurate once gluten is removed from the diet.

**Evidence in Multiple Sclerosis:**
- Short-term trials (typically under 1 year) generally show no significant difference in MRI lesion activity or relapse rates.
- Some longitudinal studies (4+ years) found that strict adherence to a GFD was associated with slower disability progression and fewer new brain lesions, though these are observational studies subject to confounding.
- The Wahls Protocol (which is strictly gluten-free) has shown statistically significant reductions in fatigue in clinical trials, but it is not possible to isolate the GFD component from other dietary changes.

**Molecular mimicry hypothesis in MS:** Gliadin peptides (from gluten) share structural similarities with some myelin proteins, potentially contributing to immune cross-reactivity in genetically susceptible individuals. This remains a hypothesis rather than established mechanism.

### Dairy-Free Diets

**Evidence in MS:** Butyrophilin (a cow's milk protein) may share structural similarity with myelin-associated glycoprotein (MAG). Some researchers propose that the immune system may cross-react, attacking the nervous system in dairy-sensitive individuals — the "molecular mimicry" model for dairy. Evidence quality: preclinical and theoretical; human trial data is limited and conflicting.

**Other neuromuscular conditions:**
- **ALS:** Research by ALSUntangled suggests GFD benefit is largely limited to ALS-like syndromes that are actually neurological manifestations of coeliac disease. Strict elimination diets in true ALS carry a risk of weight loss, which is a significant negative prognostic factor — high-caloric intake is generally prioritised over restriction.
- **CIDP:** Evidence is limited to case reports where patients with comorbid coeliac disease achieved remission only after adding a GFD to standard immunotherapy (IVIG).
- **GBS:** Negligible evidence linking GF or DF diets to GBS recovery, which is typically an acute monophasic event rather than a chronic condition.

### Evidence Specifically in MG

The evidence for elimination diets specifically in MG is the most limited:

- **Scientific consensus:** There is currently no medically proven specific elimination diet (including GF or DF) that alleviates MG symptoms for the general population. The Myasthenia Gravis Foundation of America (MGFA) focuses on balanced nutrition and managing medication side effects rather than specific dietary restrictions.
- **Where elimination may help:** Documented improvements are almost exclusively in patients with comorbid coeliac disease or non-coeliac gluten sensitivity (NCGS). In these cases, removing gluten reduces systemic immune activation, which can secondarily improve MG control.
- **The dairy question in MG:** Unlike some other autoimmune conditions where dairy is restricted, in MG, dairy is often encouraged for calcium and Vitamin D content — critical for bone health in patients taking long-term corticosteroids.
- **Preferred evidence-based approach:** Mediterranean diet adherence, rather than elimination, based on its ability to increase Tregs and reduce pro-inflammatory cytokines.

**Practical recommendation:** For patients who want to trial elimination, a 4–6 week elimination period (systematic removal, then reintroduction) is the most rational approach to identify individual triggers for non-specific symptoms (fatigue, brain fog, GI distress), rather than lifelong restriction without confirmed benefit.

## 6. Ketogenic Diet and Neuroinflammation

### Mechanisms

Research from 2024–2026 has established a clear molecular framework for how the ketogenic diet (KD) and its primary metabolite, beta-hydroxybutyrate (BHB), influence autoimmune pathology:

**mTOR pathway:** BHB directly downregulates the mTOR pathway (specifically p70S6K and S6 proteins). Since mTOR is the master regulator of pro-inflammatory Th17 cell differentiation, its inhibition reduces IL-17 production.

**Metabolic reprogramming:** Effector T-cells depend on glycolysis for energy. A 2025 study showed that ketogenic diet shifts immune cell metabolism away from glycolysis toward fatty acid oxidation, depriving inflammatory cells of the lactate and pyruvate needed for their effector functions.

**Th17/Treg balance:** KD inhibits RORγt (the master transcription factor for Th17) and reduces circulating IL-17A. Simultaneously, BHB stabilises Foxp3 expression via the AHCY-DNMT1-Foxp3 axis, promoting expansion and suppressive quality of Tregs. 2025 clinical data indicates a shift from inflammatory memory CD8+ cells toward naïve CD8+ cells.

**Neuroinflammation:**
- Microglial polarisation: Studies show shift from M1 (pro-inflammatory) to M2 (anti-inflammatory) microglial phenotype.
- Inflammasome inhibition: BHB is a known inhibitor of the NLRP3 inflammasome and the NF-κB pathway, reducing IL-1β and TNF-alpha.
- Pyroptosis inhibition: 2024 research identified that KD inhibits Gasdermin D (GSDMD), a key driver of inflammatory cell death in microglia.

**Gut-microbiome-brain axis:** A 2024 study in *Cell Reports* identified that KD promotes growth of *Lactobacillus murinus*, which produces Indole-3-Lactic Acid (ILA). ILA acts as a signalling molecule directly inhibiting Th17 cell differentiation — a gut-mediated pathway for systemic neuroprotection.

### Evidence in Neurological Autoimmune Conditions

| Condition | Research |
|---|---|
| Multiple Sclerosis | ACTRIMS 2025: A 6-month trial of modified Atkins diet showed reduced B-cell activation, lower plasma IL-6, and significant improvements in fatigue and quality of life. Evidence quality: small RCT. |
| Myasthenia Gravis | **Mya-Keto Trial** (ongoing 2025–2026, Charité Berlin): RCT investigating KD's impact on the gut-muscle-brain axis and autoantibody titres. Evidence quality: ongoing; no published results yet. |
| Autoimmune Encephalitis | Case reports demonstrating KD success in managing super-refractory status epilepticus and preserving cognitive function where standard immunotherapy failed. Evidence quality: case series. |
| Autoimmune Uveitis | Single-cell RNA sequencing (2024) revealed KD restores Th17/Treg balance in the retina and reduces CD4+ T-cell pathogenicity. Evidence quality: preclinical. |

**Important caveats for MG patients considering ketogenic diets:**
- The ketogenic diet is nutritionally restrictive and should be initiated with dietitian support.
- It reduces the variety of plant foods, which could negatively affect gut microbiome diversity — a particular concern given the documented dysbiosis in MG.
- The Wahls Paleo Plus Level 3 is a plant-rich ketogenic diet that attempts to address this tension, maintaining 6 cups of vegetables daily while achieving ketosis.
- Any significant dietary change should be discussed with the treating neurologist.

## 7. Nutrition Specifically in Myasthenia Gravis (2024–2025 Research)

### Nutritional Deficiencies in MG

Recent cohort and clinical data have established the following patterns:

**Vitamin D:** Widespread deficiency is documented across MG cohorts. Beyond bone health (critical for steroid users), Vitamin D directly binds to the FOXP3 promoter, stabilising Treg function. Correcting deficiency is now considered part of optimising immunosuppressant treatment. Deficiency correlates with higher disease activity and lower Treg stability. Blood level monitoring (25-OH Vitamin D) is recommended, with a target above 75 nmol/L.

**Zinc:** Frequently depleted by long-term steroid use. Zinc supports immune regulation and Treg function. Supplementation at standard doses (up to 25mg/day) is considered safe and beneficial for maintaining immune balance.

**Vitamin B12:** Standard clinical monitoring focus due to its role in nerve health and the potential for overlap with other autoimmune conditions (pernicious anaemia occurs more frequently in people with one autoimmune condition). Azathioprine commonly causes macrocytosis (raised MCV) which must be distinguished from B12 deficiency.

**Selenium:** Required as a cofactor for glutathione peroxidase enzymes that protect against oxidative stress. Some evidence of lower selenium levels in autoimmune thyroid disease (which co-occurs more frequently in MG). Dietary sources include Brazil nuts (1–2 daily is sufficient), fish, and poultry.

**Magnesium — important contraindication:** High-dose therapeutic magnesium supplementation is **contraindicated** in MG because magnesium inhibits acetylcholine release at the neuromuscular junction, worsening muscle weakness. Dietary magnesium from food is safe. Patients should avoid magnesium-based laxatives, high-dose supplements, and IV magnesium in non-emergency settings.

### Dietary Patterns and Disease Activity

**Th17/Treg ratio as biomarker:** A major finding from 2024–2025 research is that the Th17/Treg ratio is a reliable biomarker for MG disease activity. Dietary patterns that restore this ratio are of direct clinical relevance.

**Key dietary findings relevant to MG:**
- Omega-3 fatty acids (1–2g/day EPA+DHA): Reduces Th17 cell differentiation and pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β, TNF-alpha, IL-6), which are central to MG pathology.
- Mediterranean diet: Systematic reviews show reduction in CRP and IL-6 that are elevated in MG. Best-supported overall dietary pattern for MG.
- High-fibre diet: Supports SCFA production and Treg induction through the gut microbiome pathway.
- Astragaloside IV (AS-IV): Shows prebiotic activity, selectively enriching SCFA-producing bacteria. Preliminary evidence; clinical translation awaited.
- Calycosin (plant flavonoid from Astragalus): 2025 studies identify potential Treg-stabilising activity through activation of the Egr-1 switch, which maintains FOXP3 expression. Preliminary; not yet ready for clinical recommendation.

**Stress, diet, and MG flares:** Molecular research (2025) explains that physiological stress — and certain dietary stressors — can suppress the Egr-1 switch, causing Tregs to flip into pro-inflammatory cells via the PRDM1-S/SGK-1 pathway, potentially contributing to clinical flares. This provides mechanistic support for stress reduction and anti-inflammatory diet adherence as part of MG management.

### Latest Clinical Findings

**Refractory MG signature (2024):** A signature for treatment-resistant MG was identified — low circulating Tregs combined with high memory B-cells — which helps predict early who will fail standard treatment. This biomarker framework supports the therapeutic rationale for dietary interventions that boost Treg counts.

**Mya-Keto Trial (Charité Berlin, 2025–2026):** Ongoing RCT specifically investigating ketogenic diet in MG, examining impact on the gut-muscle-brain axis and autoantibody titres. This represents the first dedicated dietary RCT in MG.

**CAR-T and nanoparticle tolerance trials (2025):** Early results from KYV-101 (CAR-T) and CNP-106 (antigen-specific tolerance) trials show immune resetting to a "drug-free" state often accompanied by restoration of the natural Treg balance. These findings highlight that the Treg deficit in MG is potentially reversible — supporting diet as a complementary strategy to support this restoration alongside medical treatment.

## 8. Nutritional Considerations on MG Medications

### Long-Term Corticosteroids (Prednisolone)

Long-term corticosteroid use is the most nutritionally demanding aspect of MG management, with multiple overlapping effects:

**Bone health:**
- Mandatory bone protection for patients on more than 7.5mg/day prednisolone for more than 3 months.
- Target intake: 1,000–1,200mg calcium daily (dairy, fortified plant milks, leafy greens) and 800–1,000 IU Vitamin D3 daily.
- DEXA bone density scan recommended at baseline and annually or biennially.
- Regular Vitamin D blood test monitoring.
- Note: Vitamin D also supports Treg function, making its supplementation doubly important in MG.

**Blood sugar management:**
- Corticosteroids raise blood glucose, increasing risk of steroid-induced diabetes.
- Dietary focus: reduce refined carbohydrates and sugary foods.
- Monitor blood glucose, particularly on higher doses.
- A lower glycaemic index eating pattern is well-supported as a preventive measure.

**Weight and appetite:**
- Steroids increase appetite and cause Cushingoid features (moon face, central adiposity, buffalo hump).
- Monthly weight monitoring.
- Dietary strategy: prioritise protein and fibre-rich foods for satiety; reduce ultra-processed foods.

**Fluid and electrolytes:**
- Steroids cause sodium retention (contributing to blood pressure and oedema) and potassium depletion.
- Monitor salt intake; ensure adequate potassium from dietary sources (not high-dose supplements, which require monitoring).

**Gut protection:**
- Corticosteroids increase risk of GI ulceration, especially combined with NSAIDs. Ibuprofen and similar anti-inflammatory painkillers should generally be avoided in MG on steroids.
- Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) are often co-prescribed as bone and gut protection.

### Azathioprine and Mycophenolate

**Azathioprine (Imuran/Azapress):**
- Nausea and vomiting common initially; taking with food and splitting doses reduces GI side effects.
- Causes macrocytosis (raised MCV on blood count) — a benign marker of drug activity, but must be monitored and distinguished from B12 deficiency.
- **Curcumin interaction:** High-dose curcumin supplements (above 2g/day) may inhibit thiopurine S-methyltransferase (TPMT), potentially increasing azathioprine toxicity. Culinary turmeric use is safe; high-dose curcumin supplements should be discussed with the neurologist.
- **Grapefruit:** May affect drug metabolism via CYP enzymes — discuss with pharmacist.
- Hepatotoxicity risk: Alcohol should be kept to a minimum (well within the recommended weekly limit); regular liver function monitoring is standard.

**Mycophenolate mofetil (MMF / CellCept):**
- Frequently causes diarrhoea (~14% of patients), nausea, and abdominal cramps. Taking with food significantly reduces these side effects.
- **Antacid interaction:** Antacids containing aluminium or magnesium (e.g., Gaviscon Advance, some Rennie formulations) can reduce mycophenolate absorption. Take at least 2 hours apart from the MMF dose.
- **Iron supplements:** Can also reduce MMF absorption; timing separation is recommended.
- The microbiome-disrupting effects of long-term MMF make proactive fibre and fermented food intake particularly relevant.

### Micronutrient Monitoring Summary

| Nutrient | Risk Factor | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Vitamin D** | Steroids reduce absorption; deficiency common in MG | Supplement 800–1,000 IU/day minimum; monitor blood levels |
| **Calcium** | Steroid-induced bone loss | 1,000–1,200mg/day from diet and supplements |
| **Zinc** | Depleted by long-term steroids | Supplement at standard dose (up to 25mg/day) if deficient |
| **Vitamin B12** | Azathioprine causes macrocytosis (distinguish from deficiency) | Monitor FBC; B12 test if MCV raised without known cause |
| **Folate** | Required if on methotrexate (sometimes used in MG) | 5mg/week folic acid if on MTX |
| **Potassium** | Steroid-induced depletion | Monitor electrolytes; ensure adequate dietary intake |
| **Magnesium** | **CONTRAINDICATED in MG at therapeutic doses** | Avoid high-dose supplements and magnesium-based laxatives |
| **Selenium** | Oxidative stress; co-morbid thyroid disease | 1–2 Brazil nuts daily; avoid megadose supplementation |

## References and Study Quality Notes

### Key Trials Cited

**VITAL Trial (2022)** — RCT, n=25,871, USA. Vitamin D 2,000 IU/day reduced autoimmune disease incidence by 22%; omega-3 1g/day reduced by 15%. *New England Journal of Medicine* and follow-up publications. Highest-quality evidence in this document.

**WAVES Trial (2021)** — RCT comparing Wahls Protocol vs Swank Diet in progressive MS. University of Iowa. Both diets reduced fatigue; Wahls showed some advantages in functional and mental health scores. Modest sample size.

**Stanford Fermented Foods Trial (2021)** — RCT, 10-week dietary intervention. 6 servings/day fermented foods vs high-fibre diet. Fermented food group showed reduced 19 inflammatory proteins including IL-6 and increased microbiome diversity. Published in *Cell*.

**Zhou Y et al. (2024)** — Cohort study documenting gut dysbiosis in MG patients, with reduced butyrate-producing bacteria correlating with disease severity.

**Mya-Keto Trial (Charité Berlin, 2025–2026)** — Ongoing RCT of ketogenic diet in MG. No published results at time of compilation.

**ACTRIMS 2025 Modified Atkins/MS Trial** — 6-month RCT; modified Atkins diet in MS showed reduced B-cell activation, lower IL-6, improved fatigue. Small sample size.

**Wahls Nutritional Adequacy Study (2019, *Nutrients*)** — Confirmed Wahls elimination diet meets RDA for most micronutrients despite eliminating grains and dairy.

### Evidence Quality Notes

- **Strongest evidence (RCT-level):** Vitamin D and omega-3 (VITAL trial), Mediterranean diet and inflammatory markers (multiple RCTs and systematic reviews), curcumin in UC and RA, fermented foods (Stanford 2021).
- **Moderate evidence (small RCTs, cohort studies):** Quercetin and resveratrol (small RCTs), gut dysbiosis in MG (cohort data), Wahls Protocol in MS (small RCTs), Mediterranean diet in neurological conditions (systematic reviews with heterogeneous populations).
- **Preliminary evidence (preclinical, case series, theoretical):** Sulforaphane in autoimmunity, ketogenic diet in MG (Mya-Keto ongoing), dairy-free diets in MS (molecular mimicry model), calycosin and astragaloside IV, gluten-free diets outside coeliac disease.
- **No evidence (but mechanistic rationale):** Wahls Protocol specifically in MG; most elimination diets specifically in MG.

### Safety Cautions Specific to MG

1. **Magnesium supplements (therapeutic doses):** Contraindicated — inhibits acetylcholine release at the neuromuscular junction.
2. **High-dose curcumin (above 2g/day supplement):** May increase azathioprine toxicity via TPMT inhibition.
3. **Antacids and MMF:** Reduce mycophenolate absorption — take at least 2 hours apart.
4. **Grapefruit:** May affect metabolism of multiple immunosuppressants.
5. **Weight loss diets in MG with dysphagia:** Caloric restriction combined with swallowing difficulties can create significant nutritional risk; dietitian input essential.
6. **NSAIDs with corticosteroids:** Combined GI ulceration risk; avoid ibuprofen-class medications where possible.
