## What Is Myasthenia Gravis?

Myasthenia gravis (MG) is a chronic autoimmune neuromuscular disease. Its name comes from Latin and Greek, meaning roughly "grave muscular weakness," though the outlook for most people living with MG today is far better than the name implies.

The condition causes the immune system to mistakenly attack proteins at the neuromuscular junction — the point where nerve signals meet muscle fibres. The result is a characteristic pattern of muscle weakness that:

- Worsens with activity and improves with rest (fatiguability)
- Is painless
- Fluctuates unpredictably throughout the day and over time
- Does not affect sensation, cognition, or most automatic (autonomic) body functions

MG affects approximately 20 per 100,000 people, and prevalence has been rising as diagnosis and life expectancy improve. It can affect people of any age, sex, or background, though patterns differ between groups (see Types below).

## Types and Classifications

MG is not a single uniform disease. It is categorised by several different systems — by which muscles are affected, which antibodies are present, and the age at which it begins. Understanding your type matters because it influences which treatments are most likely to help.

### By Clinical Presentation

#### Ocular Myasthenia Gravis (oMG)

Weakness is confined strictly to the muscles controlling the eyelids and eye movements. Symptoms are limited to drooping eyelids (ptosis) and double vision (diplopia).

- Affects approximately 15% of people with MG long-term
- Often the first presentation of MG in up to 85% of cases
- Around 50–80% of people with ocular MG will progress to generalised MG within 1–2 years
- If symptoms remain purely ocular for 2–3 years, they are unlikely to spread further
- Diagnosis can be challenging: up to 50% of people with ocular MG do not have detectable antibodies in standard blood tests

#### Generalised Myasthenia Gravis (gMG)

Weakness affects muscles beyond the eyes — including those involved in speaking, swallowing, breathing, and limb movement. This accounts for approximately 85% of all MG cases.

The Myasthenia Gravis Foundation of America (MGFA) classifies generalised MG by severity:

| MGFA Class | Description |
|---|---|
| Class I | Ocular muscles only; all other strength normal |
| Class IIa | Mild weakness; predominantly limbs or axial muscles |
| Class IIb | Mild weakness; predominantly bulbar or respiratory muscles |
| Class IIIa | Moderate weakness; predominantly limbs or axial muscles |
| Class IIIb | Moderate weakness; predominantly bulbar or respiratory muscles |
| Class IVa | Severe weakness; predominantly limbs or axial muscles |
| Class IVb | Severe weakness; predominantly bulbar or respiratory muscles |
| Class V | Requires intubation with or without mechanical ventilation |

### By Antibody Profile (Serological Classification)

This is increasingly the most important classification for guiding treatment decisions.

#### AChR Antibody-Positive MG

The most common form. The immune system produces antibodies against the acetylcholine receptor (AChR) at the neuromuscular junction.

- Present in 80–85% of people with generalised MG and approximately 50% of those with ocular MG
- Strongly associated with thymus gland abnormalities (see Causes section)
- Generally responds well to acetylcholinesterase inhibitors such as pyridostigmine
- Thymectomy is particularly beneficial in this group

#### MuSK Antibody-Positive MG

Antibodies target Muscle-Specific Kinase (MuSK), a protein essential for maintaining the structure of the neuromuscular junction.

- Present in 5–10% of people with generalised MG (rising to approximately 40% of those who test negative for AChR antibodies)
- More common in women, often with earlier onset
- Characteristically causes severe bulbar symptoms: facial weakness, difficulty speaking and swallowing, and respiratory distress — with relatively preserved limb strength
- Does not typically involve thymus abnormalities
- Responds poorly to pyridostigmine; often requires more aggressive immunotherapy such as rituximab

#### LRP4 Antibody-Positive MG

Antibodies target Low-Density Lipoprotein Receptor-Related Protein 4 (LRP4), which is part of the same pathway as MuSK.

- Found in approximately 1–3% of MG cases, particularly those who test negative for both AChR and MuSK antibodies
- Often a milder clinical presentation
- Typically lacks the thymic abnormalities seen in AChR-positive cases
- Still an area of active research

#### Seronegative MG (SNMG)

Patients with clear clinical symptoms and electrophysiological evidence of MG but no detectable antibodies using standard blood tests.

- Accounts for approximately 5–15% of people with generalised MG
- Clinical presentation is virtually identical to seropositive MG
- Some patients in this group may have antibodies detectable only with more specialised laboratory techniques (e.g., cell-based assays for clustered AChRs)
- Diagnosis relies on clinical assessment combined with electrophysiological tests (repetitive nerve stimulation or single-fibre EMG)

### By Age of Onset and Other Factors

#### Early-Onset MG

- Onset before age 50 (some definitions use 45)
- More common in women
- Strongly associated with thymic hyperplasia
- Often AChR antibody positive
- Linked to the HLA-B8-DR3-DQ2 genetic pattern

#### Late-Onset MG

- Onset after age 50
- More common in men
- Often AChR antibody positive, associated with HLA-DRB1*15:01
- Thymic hyperplasia less common; more likely to have a normal thymus or thymoma
- Now the most rapidly growing group, partly due to an ageing population

#### Thymoma-Associated MG

- Approximately 10–15% of people with MG have a thymoma (a tumour of the thymus gland)
- Almost all are AChR antibody positive
- Thymoma MG can be more severe and harder to control
- Surgical removal of the thymoma is necessary (for the tumour itself), though this does not always resolve the MG

#### Juvenile MG

- Autoimmune MG with onset before age 18
- Roughly 10–15% of autoimmune MG cases in Western populations
- Children who develop MG before puberty (before approximately age 12) are more likely to have purely ocular involvement and higher rates of spontaneous remission
- Management must consider the long-term effects of immunosuppressant medications on a growing child

#### Neonatal (Transient) MG

- Affects approximately 10–15% of babies born to mothers with autoimmune MG
- Caused by maternal IgG antibodies crossing the placenta during pregnancy
- Symptoms appear within hours to days of birth: weak sucking, floppy muscle tone (hypotonia), breathing difficulties, a weak cry
- It is temporary — symptoms resolve as the maternal antibodies naturally clear from the baby's system, usually within 2–4 weeks
- Not a lifelong condition and does not mean the child will develop MG

#### Congenital Myasthenic Syndromes (CMS)

Importantly, these are not autoimmune. CMS is a group of rare genetic disorders caused by inherited mutations in proteins at the neuromuscular junction.

- Estimated prevalence of 1–2 per million people
- Onset at birth or early childhood
- Autoantibodies are absent; the immune system is not involved
- Standard MG immunosuppressants and thymectomy are ineffective and should not be used
- Diagnosis requires genetic testing
- Treatment varies significantly by the specific gene mutation involved; some respond to ephedrine, fluoxetine, or 3,4-diaminopyridine

## Symptoms

### The Core Characteristic: Fatiguable Weakness

The defining feature of MG is weakness that becomes more pronounced with sustained or repeated use of a muscle, and improves with rest. This is called fatiguability. A person with MG might feel relatively well in the morning but significantly weaker by the afternoon. After a period of rest — even a short nap — strength often partially or fully returns.

This pattern distinguishes MG from most other causes of muscle weakness, where weakness tends to be constant.

### Eye and Eyelid Symptoms

These are the most common initial symptoms, occurring in approximately 85% of people when MG first presents.

- **Ptosis:** Drooping of one or both eyelids. It may affect one eye more than the other, and can switch sides. It typically worsens as the day progresses or with prolonged upward gaze.
- **Diplopia:** Double vision caused by weakness of the muscles that move the eyes (extraocular muscles). The eyes fall out of alignment, producing two overlapping images.

### Bulbar Symptoms (Speech, Swallowing, Chewing)

The term "bulbar" refers to muscles in and around the throat, mouth, and face that are controlled by cranial nerves originating in the brainstem.

- **Dysarthria:** Slurred or nasal-sounding speech. Speech may start clearly but become increasingly difficult to understand as a conversation continues, due to muscle fatigue.
- **Dysphagia:** Difficulty swallowing, which can cause choking, food or liquid going the wrong way (aspiration), or the need to eat very slowly. Severe dysphagia can lead to weight loss and aspiration pneumonia.
- **Jaw fatigue:** Difficulty chewing, particularly with tough foods. The jaw may feel too tired to stay closed toward the end of a meal; some people need to support their chin with their hand.
- **Facial weakness:** The facial muscles may weaken, producing a flat or expressionless appearance. Attempting to smile may produce a horizontal "snarl" (sometimes called a "myasthenic snarl").
- **Nasal regurgitation:** Liquids may come back up through the nose when swallowing.

### Neck and Trunk Weakness

- **Neck extensor weakness:** Difficulty holding the head upright, leading to "dropped head syndrome" — the head falls forward involuntarily. People may need to support their head with a hand or chin rest.
- **Truncal weakness:** Weakness of the muscles supporting the spine and torso, which can contribute to fatigue when sitting or standing for long periods.

### Limb Weakness

- Typically affects proximal muscles (those closest to the body's centre) more than distal muscles (hands and feet)
- Arms are often more affected than legs
- Typical complaints: difficulty raising the arms above the head (e.g., hair washing, reaching shelves), climbing stairs, rising from a chair
- Weakness can cause difficulty with fine motor tasks when the small muscles of the hands are involved

### Respiratory Symptoms

Weakness of the muscles that control breathing — the diaphragm and the muscles between the ribs (intercostal muscles) — can cause:

- Shortness of breath, especially during activity
- Difficulty breathing when lying flat (orthopnoea), because the diaphragm has to work harder against gravity
- A feeling of not being able to take a satisfying breath

Respiratory involvement is the most dangerous aspect of MG.

### Myasthenic Crisis

A myasthenic crisis is a life-threatening emergency in which respiratory muscle weakness becomes severe enough to cause respiratory failure.

- Affects approximately 15–20% of people with MG at some point in their illness
- Requires immediate hospitalisation, often in intensive care
- May require mechanical ventilation to support breathing

Common triggers include:
- Infections (particularly respiratory infections)
- Surgery or other physical stressors
- Certain medications (see Causes section)
- Emotional stress
- Heat
- Tapering of immunosuppressant medications too quickly
- Pregnancy or the postpartum period

### What MG Does Not Affect

- Sensation: MG does not cause numbness, tingling, or pain (though musculoskeletal pain from abnormal posture or compensatory strain is possible)
- Consciousness and cognition: MG does not affect the brain
- Pupil responses and most autonomic functions (heart rate, blood pressure, digestion are not typically affected by MG)
- Smooth muscle (involuntary muscle, such as in blood vessels)

### How Symptoms Progress Over Time

MG does not follow a single predictable path. General patterns include:

- Most people have ocular symptoms first; of these, roughly half to four-fifths will develop generalised symptoms within 1–2 years
- If MG remains purely ocular for 2–3 years, it is likely to stay that way
- Severity typically peaks within the first 2–3 years of diagnosis
- Many people experience fluctuating periods of relative remission and worsening (exacerbations) throughout their lives
- Spontaneous complete remission does occur but is not common; it is more likely in children with ocular-onset MG
- With treatment, the majority of people with MG can achieve a good quality of life

## Causes and Pathophysiology

Understanding why MG happens requires understanding how muscles are normally told to contract — and how the immune system disrupts this process.

### The Normal Neuromuscular Junction

When you decide to move a muscle, an electrical signal travels along a motor nerve. When it reaches the end of the nerve, it triggers the release of a chemical messenger called acetylcholine (ACh) into a tiny gap between the nerve ending and the muscle surface — the neuromuscular junction (NMJ).

Acetylcholine crosses this gap and binds to specialised proteins on the muscle surface called acetylcholine receptors (AChRs). This binding opens ion channels in the receptor, allowing a rapid flow of charged particles (ions) that triggers the muscle to contract.

After the signal is delivered, an enzyme called acetylcholinesterase breaks down the acetylcholine quickly, resetting the junction and allowing the muscle to respond to the next signal.

The muscle side of this junction has a deeply folded structure — called the postsynaptic membrane — that is packed with AChRs. These folds greatly increase the surface area available for signalling.

### The Autoimmune Attack in MG

In MG, the immune system produces antibodies that target this junction. Different antibodies target different proteins and cause damage through distinct mechanisms.

#### AChR Antibodies: Three Mechanisms of Damage

In the most common form of MG (AChR antibody-positive), IgG1 and IgG3 class antibodies attack the acetylcholine receptor through three overlapping mechanisms:

**1. Complement-mediated destruction**
This is the most damaging mechanism. The antibodies activate the "classical pathway" of the complement system — part of the immune system's rapid-response toolkit. This triggers a cascade that ends in the formation of the Membrane Attack Complex (MAC), which punches holes in the postsynaptic membrane. Over time, this destroys the deep folds of the junction, reducing the surface area and depleting the AChRs. This process is called "simplification" of the NMJ.

**2. Antigenic modulation**
The antibodies cross-link adjacent AChRs on the muscle surface. This cross-linking signals the muscle cell to pull the receptors inside itself and break them down — a normal cellular process that is now happening too quickly and too extensively. The AChRs are degraded faster than they can be replaced.

**3. Direct blockade**
Some antibodies physically block the site on the receptor where acetylcholine would normally bind, preventing the receptor from functioning even when it is still intact.

The combined effect of all three mechanisms is a dramatic reduction in the number and function of acetylcholine receptors. With fewer functional receptors, many nerve signals fail to produce muscle contractions — and those that succeed become progressively weaker with repetition as the remaining receptors are pushed to their limits.

#### MuSK Antibodies: A Different Mechanism

In MuSK-positive MG, antibodies target Muscle-Specific Kinase (MuSK), a protein that is not a receptor itself but plays a critical role in organising and maintaining the cluster of AChRs at the junction.

MuSK is part of a signalling pathway — triggered by a protein called Agrin, working through LRP4 — that instructs AChRs to cluster together densely at the correct location on the muscle surface.

MuSK antibodies are predominantly of the IgG4 subclass, which is important because IgG4 antibodies do not activate the complement system. There is no destructive lysis of the membrane. Instead, the antibodies interfere with the Agrin-LRP4-MuSK signalling pathway, causing the AChR cluster to disperse and gradually disappear. The architecture of the junction deteriorates without the violent destruction seen in AChR-positive MG — but the functional result is similarly severe.

This mechanistic difference explains why the clinical presentation of MuSK MG differs (more bulbar involvement, less limb involvement) and why treatments that work primarily by reducing complement activity are less effective.

#### LRP4 Antibodies

LRP4 (Low-Density Lipoprotein Receptor-Related Protein 4) is the receptor that Agrin binds to in order to activate MuSK. Antibodies to LRP4 disrupt the same Agrin-LRP4-MuSK pathway as MuSK antibodies, but at the upstream step.

### The Role of the Thymus Gland

The thymus is a small gland in the chest, behind the breastbone, that plays a central role in training the immune system during childhood. It is here that T-cells — a key type of immune cell — learn to distinguish between the body's own proteins ("self") and foreign invaders ("non-self"). T-cells that attack the body's own tissues are normally eliminated in the thymus.

In AChR-positive MG, the thymus appears to be the site where this process breaks down:

- The thymus contains "myoid cells" — cells that unusually express acetylcholine receptors on their surface
- In some people (possibly triggered by a viral infection or other environmental factor), the thymus develops an inflammatory environment in which these AChR-bearing cells are presented to T-cells in a way that breaks self-tolerance
- The result is that T-cells that recognise AChR as a target survive and are activated, rather than being eliminated
- These T-cells then help B-cells (another type of immune cell) mature into plasma cells that produce large quantities of anti-AChR antibodies

Approximately 75% of people with AChR-positive MG have thymic abnormalities:
- Thymic hyperplasia (an overactive thymus with germinal centres — areas of active antibody production) in approximately 65% of cases
- Thymoma (a tumour of the thymus) in approximately 10–15% of cases

In MuSK-positive MG, the thymus is typically normal, which explains why thymectomy is less clearly beneficial in this group.

### Genetic Factors

MG is not directly inherited — it does not follow simple Mendelian genetics — but genetic susceptibility plays a role. Specific variants in the genes encoding Human Leukocyte Antigens (HLA) — proteins that help the immune system recognise self from non-self — are associated with different forms of MG:

| MG Subtype | HLA Association |
|---|---|
| Early-onset AChR+ MG | HLA-B8-DR3-DQ2 haplotype |
| Late-onset MG | HLA-DRB1*15:01 |
| MuSK MG | HLA-DQB1*05:02 |

Having a close family member with MG or another autoimmune condition slightly increases risk, but the absolute lifetime risk remains low.

### Triggers and Exacerbating Factors

Even with an underlying predisposition, specific factors often trigger the onset of MG or cause existing symptoms to worsen.

**Infections**
Viral infections — particularly Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) — are among the most studied triggers. The proposed mechanism is "molecular mimicry": a part of the virus closely resembles a part of the AChR protein, so an immune response mounted against the virus inadvertently begins attacking the body's own receptors.

**Medications that can trigger or worsen MG**
A range of commonly used medications can unmask latent MG or cause significant deterioration in someone already diagnosed. These include:
- Certain antibiotics: aminoglycosides (gentamicin, tobramycin), fluoroquinolones, macrolides, and telithromycin
- Beta-blockers (e.g., propranolol, atenolol)
- Calcium channel blockers
- Antiarrhythmics: quinidine, procainamide
- Magnesium (including antacids and laxatives containing magnesium)
- Checkpoint inhibitor cancer immunotherapies (which can trigger new-onset MG)
- Some anaesthetic agents and muscle relaxants

Anyone with MG should ensure all healthcare providers are aware of the diagnosis before any new medication is started.

**Hormonal factors**
Hormonal fluctuations — particularly those involving oestrogen and progesterone — can influence MG activity. Pregnancy is particularly unpredictable:
- Symptoms may improve, remain stable, or significantly worsen
- The first trimester and the postpartum period carry the highest risk of deterioration
- Careful specialist monitoring throughout pregnancy is essential

**Physical and emotional stress**
Surgery, trauma, extreme heat, and significant emotional stress can all precipitate myasthenic crisis in susceptible individuals.

## Treatments

The treatment of MG has transformed dramatically in the last decade, moving from broad immunosuppression toward precise, targeted therapies. Treatment is usually a combination of approaches — addressing symptoms immediately while working toward longer-term immune control.

A useful way to think about treatment is in three overlapping layers:
1. Symptomatic treatment — making the neuromuscular junction work better
2. Short-term immune modulation — rapidly reducing the antibody burden
3. Long-term immunosuppression — suppressing the underlying immune response

---

### Symptomatic Treatment

#### Pyridostigmine (Mestinon)

Pyridostigmine is almost always the first treatment tried. It does not treat the underlying autoimmune cause but improves symptoms by making the most of the acetylcholine that is available.

**How it works:** Pyridostigmine inhibits acetylcholinesterase — the enzyme that breaks down acetylcholine at the neuromuscular junction. By slowing this breakdown, acetylcholine remains in the junction longer, giving it more chances to find and activate the reduced number of functioning receptors.

**Dosing:** Typically 30–60 mg taken every 4–6 hours during waking hours. A slow-release version (Mestinon Timespan, 180 mg) can be taken at night to help with morning weakness. Doses are adjusted based on individual response.

**Side effects:** Because pyridostigmine increases acetylcholine activity throughout the body — not just in skeletal muscle — it can cause:
- Gastrointestinal effects: nausea, abdominal cramps, increased bowel movements, diarrhoea
- Increased salivation and sweating
- Muscle twitching (fasciculations)
- Bradycardia (slow heart rate) at higher doses

**Important note on MuSK MG:** Pyridostigmine is less effective — and in some cases can worsen symptoms — in people with MuSK antibody-positive MG. It is generally used with caution or not at all in this group.

---

### Long-Term Immunosuppression

These treatments target the underlying autoimmune disease. They take weeks to months to show full effect, so symptomatic treatment is usually continued alongside them.

#### Corticosteroids (Prednisone / Prednisolone)

Corticosteroids are potent anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressant drugs and remain the most widely used immunosuppressant in MG.

**How they work:** They broadly suppress immune system activity, reducing production of the autoantibodies that attack the NMJ.

**Clinical course:** Most people begin to improve within 2–4 weeks, with maximum benefit over 3–6 months. A temporary worsening of symptoms can occur in the first 1–2 weeks after starting treatment — a phenomenon known as "steroid dip" — particularly in people with significant bulbar or respiratory involvement; some neurologists initiate steroids during a brief hospital admission for this reason.

**Long-term use and side effects:** Corticosteroids are effective but carry significant side effects with long-term use:
- Weight gain and redistribution of body fat (moon face, central obesity)
- Osteoporosis (bone thinning) — calcium and vitamin D supplements and sometimes bone-protective medications (bisphosphonates) are usually prescribed alongside
- Raised blood glucose (steroid-induced diabetes)
- High blood pressure
- Cataracts and glaucoma
- Increased susceptibility to infection
- Skin thinning and bruising
- Mood changes, insomnia

The goal is always to find the lowest dose that maintains disease control — or, ideally, to use a steroid-sparing agent to reduce or eliminate the steroid dose altogether.

#### Steroid-Sparing Immunosuppressants

These agents allow steroid doses to be reduced while maintaining immune control. They are slower to work than steroids (often 6–18 months for full effect) and are usually added alongside steroids rather than replacing them immediately.

**Azathioprine (Imuran)**
The most commonly used steroid-sparing agent in MG worldwide. Typical dose: 2–3 mg/kg per day. Takes 12–18 months to show full benefit. Side effects include nausea, liver toxicity, bone marrow suppression (low white blood cell count), and a small increased risk of lymphoma with very long-term use. A genetic test (TPMT enzyme activity) is usually done before starting, as people with certain variants are at higher risk of serious side effects.

**Mycophenolate Mofetil (CellCept)**
Widely used as an alternative or addition to azathioprine. Typical dose: 1,000–1,500 mg twice daily. Better tolerated than azathioprine by many people. Side effects include gastrointestinal upset, increased infection risk, and teratogenicity (must not be used in pregnancy or by women who may become pregnant without reliable contraception).

**Cyclosporine and Tacrolimus**
Calcineurin inhibitors that more selectively suppress T-cell activity. Both have a faster onset than azathioprine (weeks to months). Used when other agents are ineffective or not tolerated. Side effects include kidney toxicity, high blood pressure, tremor, and increased infection risk. Regular monitoring of blood levels and kidney function is required.

**Methotrexate**
An alternative steroid-sparing agent used when others are not suitable. Requires folic acid supplementation. Not suitable during pregnancy.

### Thymectomy

Surgical removal of the thymus gland.

**Who benefits:** Thymectomy is strongly recommended for:
- People with thymoma (removal is necessary regardless of MG)
- People with AChR antibody-positive generalised MG who are aged under approximately 65 and are fit for surgery

The landmark MGTX trial (published in the New England Journal of Medicine) provided robust evidence that thymectomy in non-thymomatous, AChR-positive generalised MG significantly improves clinical outcomes over a 3-year period — and these benefits continue for at least 5 years. Participants who had thymectomy needed lower doses of prednisone and had better quality of life scores compared to those who had prednisone alone.

**When to do it:** Current guidance suggests thymectomy is most beneficial when performed within the first 2–3 years of diagnosis, before the disease becomes more established.

**Surgical approach:** Minimally invasive techniques (video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery, VATS, or robotic surgery) are now standard in most centres, with shorter recovery than the traditional open approach (median sternotomy).

**Realistic expectations:** Thymectomy is not a guaranteed cure. Benefits often take months to years to become apparent. Some people achieve complete stable remission; others experience significant improvement in symptoms and reduction in medication requirements; a minority see little benefit. Response is harder to predict in late-onset MG and is generally less clear in MuSK-positive MG.

### Acute and Rapid Treatment (Crisis Management and Pre-Surgical Preparation)

When someone experiences a myasthenic crisis, or needs rapid stabilisation before surgery or during a severe flare, two treatments can quickly reduce circulating antibody levels.

#### Intravenous Immunoglobulin (IVIG)

IVIG involves infusing large quantities of pooled human IgG antibodies (collected from thousands of blood donors) directly into the bloodstream.

**How it works:** Several mechanisms are proposed, including modulating immune cell activity, competing with pathogenic antibodies for FcRn receptors (which normally recycle IgG antibodies), and providing anti-idiotypic antibodies that neutralise the disease-causing autoantibodies.

**Dosing:** Typically 2 g/kg given over 2–5 days.

**Onset of effect:** Improvement usually begins within days and lasts 3–6 weeks.

**Side effects:** Generally well tolerated. Can cause headache (sometimes severe — aseptic meningitis), flushing, fever, and rarely kidney problems or thrombosis. Contraindicated in people with IgA deficiency who have anti-IgA antibodies.

#### Plasmapheresis (Plasma Exchange / PLEX)

Plasmapheresis is a mechanical process in which blood is removed from the body, spun to separate the plasma (the liquid component that contains the antibodies) from the blood cells, and then the blood cells are returned with a replacement fluid.

**How it works:** Directly removes pathogenic autoantibodies from the bloodstream.

**Schedule:** Typically 5–7 sessions over 10–14 days.

**Onset of effect:** Often faster than IVIG — improvement can begin within days of the first exchange.

**Side effects:** Requires venous access (often a central line). Can cause drops in blood pressure, bleeding risk (removes clotting factors along with antibodies), and electrolyte imbalances. Not suitable for everyone.

**IVIG vs Plasmapheresis:** Both are similarly effective for myasthenic crisis and short-term stabilisation. The choice depends on availability, individual patient factors, and local expertise.

### Targeted Biological Therapies

The last decade has seen an explosion of new biological treatments that target specific pathways in the autoimmune process. These represent a major advance, particularly for people with refractory MG (disease that has not responded to conventional treatments) and those unable to tolerate standard immunosuppressants.

#### Complement Inhibitors

The complement system's role in destroying the neuromuscular junction in AChR-positive MG makes it a compelling target.

**Eculizumab (Soliris)**
The first complement inhibitor approved for MG (2017 in the US, 2018 in EU). Eculizumab is a monoclonal antibody that binds to complement protein C5, preventing it from being cleaved and thereby blocking the formation of the Membrane Attack Complex.

- Given as an intravenous infusion — weekly for 4 weeks (induction), then every 2 weeks (maintenance)
- Approved for refractory, AChR antibody-positive generalised MG in adults
- Significant clinical benefit demonstrated in the REGAIN trial
- Because blocking complement C5 removes an important defence against certain bacteria, all patients must be vaccinated against Neisseria meningitidis (meningococcal bacteria) at least 2 weeks before starting treatment. Some centres also use prophylactic antibiotics.

**Ravulizumab (Ultomiris)**
A longer-acting version of eculizumab with the same mechanism but an extended half-life. Maintenance infusions are required only every 8 weeks (compared to every 2 weeks for eculizumab), which is a significant practical advantage.

**Zilucoplan (Zilbrysq)**
A newer C5 inhibitor with a critical practical difference: it is a small peptide administered as a once-daily subcutaneous (under the skin) self-injection — making it the first complement inhibitor for MG that patients can administer themselves at home without attending an infusion centre.

- Approved by the FDA in 2023 for AChR antibody-positive generalised MG
- Has a dual mechanism: inhibits C5 cleavage AND blocks C5b from binding to C6, potentially providing more complete complement blockade
- Long-term data from the RAISE-XT extension trial (2025) confirmed sustained safety and efficacy over 2+ years

---

#### FcRn Inhibitors (Neonatal Fc Receptor Blockers)

A newer class of treatments that work by blocking a receptor (FcRn — neonatal Fc receptor) that is responsible for recycling IgG antibodies. By blocking FcRn, these drugs accelerate the natural breakdown of all IgG antibodies — including the pathogenic autoantibodies causing MG.

This approach is more broadly acting than complement inhibitors (it reduces overall IgG, not just complement activity) but is also more non-specific.

**Efgartigimod (Vyvgart)**
The first approved FcRn inhibitor for MG (2021 in the US, 2022 in EU).

- Available as an intravenous infusion (Vyvgart) or as a subcutaneous injection formulation (Vyvgart Hytrulo, combining efgartigimod with hyaluronidase to allow faster subcutaneous delivery)
- Approved for AChR antibody-positive generalised MG
- Treatment is typically given in cycles — a course of weekly doses, followed by a break, with subsequent cycles based on the patient's response
- The ADAPT trial demonstrated significant improvements in daily living activities and muscle strength scores

**Rozanolixizumab (Rystiggo)**
Approved in 2023 for both AChR antibody-positive and MuSK antibody-positive generalised MG — an important distinction as it is one of the few newer agents approved for both subtypes.

- Administered as a subcutaneous infusion (usually via a small pump worn on the body)
- More recently approved for home self-administration, reducing the burden of clinic visits
- Treatment given once weekly for 6 weeks per cycle

**Nipocalimab (Imaavy)**
FDA-approved in May 2025 for generalised MG in adults and adolescents aged 12 and over.

- Intravenous infusion given every 4 weeks
- Works through the same FcRn blocking mechanism

**Batoclimab**
Still in clinical development. Being investigated with a focus on different dosing schedules and potentially longer treatment cycles. Promising early data in refractory MG.

---

#### Anti-CD20 Therapy (Rituximab)

CD20 is a protein found on the surface of B-cells — the immune cells responsible for producing autoantibodies. Rituximab is a monoclonal antibody that targets and depletes CD20-positive B-cells.

**Rituximab (MabThera / Rituxan)**
Rituximab has become a standard therapy for MuSK antibody-positive MG, where it has demonstrated particularly impressive results — including deep reductions in MuSK antibody levels and durable remission in many patients.

- Given as an intravenous infusion, usually 2 doses 2 weeks apart, with repeated cycles based on response or antibody levels
- International consensus guidelines (2025) now recommend considering rituximab within the first year of diagnosis for MuSK-positive MG — a significant shift from the previous approach of reserving it for refractory disease
- Also used in refractory AChR-positive MG, though the response is generally less dramatic
- Key side effects include infusion reactions, increased infection risk (particularly respiratory infections), and the need to avoid live vaccines. Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML) — a rare but serious brain infection — is an extremely rare but recognised risk.

### Emerging and Experimental Treatments

The treatment pipeline for MG is exceptionally active, reflecting the condition's clear autoimmune mechanism and the availability of well-validated outcome measures for clinical trials.

#### CAR-T Cell Therapy

Chimeric Antigen Receptor T-cell (CAR-T) therapy involves engineering a patient's own T-cells (or donor T-cells) to target and destroy specific immune cells.

**Descartes-08 (BCMA-targeted mRNA CAR-T)**
This is one of the most exciting developments in MG therapy. Unlike traditional CAR-T therapies used in cancer treatment, Descartes-08 uses mRNA to transiently (temporarily) engineer T-cells to target BCMA (B-Cell Maturation Antigen) — a protein found on plasma cells that produce antibodies.

- Does not require lymphodepleting chemotherapy (which makes traditional CAR-T so toxic)
- Phase 2 trial results showed meaningful and sustained reductions in symptom burden in refractory MG
- Phase 3 AURORA trial results in 2025 showed sustained remission in patients with refractory disease

**KYV-101 (CD19-targeted CAR-T)**
Targets CD19, another B-cell marker, with the aim of depleting disease-causing B-cells more broadly. Received FDA Regenerative Medicine Advanced Therapy (RMAT) designation in late 2024, accelerating its development pathway.

#### Gene Therapy and Other Approaches

Early-stage research is exploring several directions:

- **AAV-based decoy receptors:** Gene therapy using adeno-associated viruses (AAV) to deliver "decoy" AChR proteins, which could absorb circulating autoantibodies before they reach the real receptors
- **Gene editing:** Approaches to modify B-cells to prevent them from producing pathogenic antibodies
- **Neonatal tolerisation:** Research into whether the immune system could be re-taught to tolerate AChR as a "self" protein

These remain in early research phases and are not yet available outside of clinical trials.

#### Other Investigational Agents

- **Ublituximab:** Another anti-CD20 antibody being evaluated in MG
- **Inebilizumab (Uplizna):** Targets CD19 (broader B-cell depletion than CD20-targeting agents). FDA approved December 2025 for generalised MG, with a convenient twice-yearly dosing schedule
- **Stem cell transplantation (HSCT):** Being explored in very severe, refractory cases as a means of "resetting" the immune system; limited data but promising early results in small case series

### Treatment Decision-Making: Matching Treatment to Type

Because MG is heterogeneous, treatment choices are increasingly guided by antibody status and individual patient factors:

| MG Subtype | First-Line Symptomatic | Immunosuppression | Specific Considerations |
|---|---|---|---|
| AChR+ generalised | Pyridostigmine | Steroids + steroid-sparer | Thymectomy; consider complement inhibitor or FcRn inhibitor for refractory cases |
| MuSK+ generalised | Pyridostigmine with caution | Steroids + mycophenolate | Early rituximab; FcRn inhibitor (rozanolixizumab); thymectomy less clearly beneficial |
| Ocular only | Pyridostigmine | Low-dose steroids if needed | Avoid over-treating; monitor for generalisation |
| Thymoma-associated | Pyridostigmine | Steroids + steroid-sparer | Thymoma resection essential; often more refractory |
| Seronegative | Pyridostigmine | Steroids + steroid-sparer | Same general approach; targeted biologics may be less applicable |
| Congenital (CMS) | Condition-specific (NOT pyridostigmine for all) | Not applicable | No immunosuppression; genetic diagnosis essential |

### Living Well with Treatment

A few important points for people managing MG day-to-day:

- **Medication timing matters:** Pyridostigmine should be taken at consistent times, roughly 30–45 minutes before meals if swallowing is affected
- **Drugs to avoid or use with caution:** Always carry a list of medications that can worsen MG and share it with any new healthcare provider (especially before any procedure or anaesthetic)
- **Energy management:** Planning activities around peak energy times (often mid-morning after pyridostigmine has taken effect), building in rest periods, and avoiding known triggers can significantly improve daily function
- **Heat:** Many people with MG find heat makes symptoms worse; practical strategies include staying cool, using cooling vests, and avoiding very hot environments
- **Infections:** Treat infections promptly — even a mild respiratory infection can trigger a significant MG exacerbation
- **Mental health:** The unpredictability and impact of MG on daily life can contribute to anxiety and depression, which are more common in people with MG than in the general population. These deserve attention and support in their own right.

## Glossary of Key Terms

**AChR (Acetylcholine Receptor):** The protein on muscle cells that binds acetylcholine to trigger contraction. The main target of autoantibodies in the most common form of MG.

**Acetylcholine (ACh):** The chemical messenger that carries the signal from nerve to muscle at the neuromuscular junction.

**Acetylcholinesterase:** The enzyme that breaks down acetylcholine after it has acted.

**Autoimmune:** A condition in which the immune system mistakenly attacks the body's own tissues.

**Bulbar:** Relating to the brainstem (the "bulb") and the muscles it controls — principally those involved in speech, swallowing, and chewing.

**Complement system:** A cascade of proteins in the blood that forms part of the innate immune system. In AChR-positive MG, complement activation directly damages the neuromuscular junction.

**Diplopia:** Double vision.

**Dysphagia:** Difficulty swallowing.

**Dysarthria:** Difficulty with speech — typically slurred or nasal-sounding.

**Fatiguability:** The characteristic MG pattern in which weakness worsens with sustained use of a muscle and improves with rest.

**FcRn (Neonatal Fc Receptor):** A receptor that recycles IgG antibodies, extending their lifespan in the circulation. FcRn inhibitors reduce the levels of all IgG antibodies, including pathogenic autoantibodies.

**HLA (Human Leukocyte Antigen):** Proteins that help the immune system distinguish self from non-self. Certain HLA variants are associated with susceptibility to specific forms of MG.

**IgG:** The main class of antibody in the blood. The autoantibodies in MG are IgG antibodies.

**LRP4 (Low-Density Lipoprotein Receptor-Related Protein 4):** A protein that works with MuSK to organise AChRs at the neuromuscular junction. Targeted by antibodies in a minority of MG cases.

**Membrane Attack Complex (MAC):** The end product of complement activation — a structure that punches holes in cell membranes, causing cell death.

**MGFA:** Myasthenia Gravis Foundation of America. Their classification system is used worldwide to grade MG severity.

**MuSK (Muscle-Specific Kinase):** A protein essential for maintaining the structure of the neuromuscular junction. Targeted by antibodies in approximately 5–10% of generalised MG cases.

**Myasthenic Crisis:** A life-threatening emergency in which respiratory muscles fail, requiring mechanical ventilation.

**Neuromuscular Junction (NMJ):** The tiny gap between the end of a motor nerve and the muscle it controls, where the nerve signal is chemically converted into a muscle contraction.

**Ptosis:** Drooping of one or both eyelids.

**Pyridostigmine (Mestinon):** An acetylcholinesterase inhibitor — the main symptomatic treatment for MG.

**Seronegative:** Testing negative for known autoantibodies (AChR, MuSK, LRP4) despite having clinical MG.

**Thymectomy:** Surgical removal of the thymus gland.

**Thymoma:** A tumour of the thymus gland. Associated with MG in 10–15% of cases.

**Thymus:** A gland in the chest that trains immune cells. In AChR-positive MG, it is thought to be where immune tolerance to AChR breaks down.
