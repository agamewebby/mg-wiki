# Deep Dive: T-Cell Immunology and Myasthenia Gravis Research 2024-2026

*A comprehensive synthesis of the latest science on regulatory T-cells, immune resetting, and emerging therapies*

---

## Introduction

Myasthenia Gravis (MG) has long been understood as a disease of misguided antibodies — the immune system produces proteins that attack the neuromuscular junction, the connection point between nerves and muscles. But research from 2024 to 2026 is telling a far more nuanced story. The field has shifted its gaze upstream, to the T-cells that instruct and regulate the entire immune response, and in particular to a group of specialised "peacekeeper" cells called **Regulatory T-cells (Tregs)**.

What has emerged is a picture of compounding failures: Tregs that are poorly trained in the thymus, chemically destabilised once in the bloodstream, and in some cases actively converted into the very inflammatory cells they were meant to suppress. Understanding these failures has opened doors to genuinely new treatment strategies — some of which, for the first time, are producing results that could be described as approaching a functional cure.

This document synthesises findings from eight focused research areas, drawing on clinical trial results, basic science publications, and mechanistic discoveries from 2023 to early 2026. It is written for people living with MG and their carers, with technical terms explained as they arise.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Tregs are not just absent in MG — they are being actively destabilised.** A newly identified molecular pathway (PRDM1-S/SGK-1) causes MG Tregs to "flip" from protective cells into pro-inflammatory ones. This is one of the most important mechanistic discoveries of the past two years.

- **The thymus is the root cause for most AChR-positive MG patients.** Overproduction of a signalling molecule called TSLP by thymic cells produces defective Tregs before they even leave the gland. This explains why thymectomy works — and points to a new drug target.

- **CAR-T cell therapy has produced the first real evidence of drug-free remission in MG.** The KYSA-6 trial (KYV-101) achieved 100% drug-free status in early patients at 24 weeks. The AURORA trial for Descartes-08 is now in Phase 3.

- **Low-dose IL-2 therapy is proving effective at selectively expanding Tregs** across multiple autoimmune diseases. Trials specifically targeting refractory MG are now underway.

- **Antigen-specific tolerance using nanoparticles (CNP-106) has entered clinical trials** and received FDA Orphan Drug Designation, representing the most targeted "teaching" approach to date.

- **TSLP blockade (via tezepelumab, already approved for asthma) is being studied in MG** as an "upstream" fix targeting the thymic source of defective Tregs.

- **EPO (erythropoietin) has a previously unknown role in immune tolerance** via tolerogenic dendritic cells, and STING protein overactivity is emerging as a shared driver of inflammation across autoimmune conditions.

- **Biomarkers for refractory disease are improving.** The Th17/Treg ratio, memory B-cell counts, and specific microRNAs (miR-150-5p, miR-30e-5p) can now help predict who will respond to standard treatment and who needs an escalated approach.

## Part 1: Regulatory T-Cells in Myasthenia Gravis — Why the Peacekeepers Fail

### 1.1 What Tregs Are and Why They Matter

The immune system needs both an accelerator and a brakes system. Effector T-cells (Teffs) and B-cells provide the attack force. Regulatory T-cells (Tregs) provide the brakes — they suppress inappropriate immune activity and maintain tolerance to the body's own tissues.

In healthy individuals, around 5-10% of circulating T-cells are Tregs. In MG patients, not only are Treg numbers often lower, but the cells that remain are frequently dysfunctional. For years, researchers assumed this was simply a numerical problem — not enough peacekeepers. The 2024-2025 research period has fundamentally revised this view.

### 1.2 FOXP3: The Master Switch (and the 2025 Nobel Prize)

The identity of a Treg depends entirely on a single master protein called **FOXP3**. Think of FOXP3 as the "on switch" that tells a T-cell to become a peacekeeper rather than a fighter. Without stable FOXP3, a Treg loses its identity.

The discovery of FOXP3 and its central role in immune tolerance was awarded the **2025 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine**, reflecting how foundational this insight has become to the entire field of autoimmunity research.

In 2025, researchers identified a key molecular mechanism called the **Egr-1 switch** that keeps FOXP3 stable. In healthy conditions, a protein called Egr-1 actively maintains FOXP3 expression. When this switch is disrupted — by inflammation, physiological stress, or in MG, by specific molecular pathways in the cells themselves — FOXP3 expression becomes unstable and the Treg begins to lose its suppressive identity.

This has clinical relevance: it provides a molecular explanation for why MG patients often experience **flares during periods of physiological stress** (surgery, infection, emotional trauma). Stress hormones including cortisol and adrenaline suppress Egr-1 and disrupt the chemical "tagging" (methylation) of FOXP3, causing a transient collapse of Treg function precisely when the immune brakes are most needed. Natural compounds that activate Egr-1, such as Calycosin (a plant flavonoid), are now being explored as potential Treg-stabilising agents.

### 1.3 The PRDM1-S / SGK-1 Pathway: When Tregs Become the Enemy

The most alarming discovery of the 2024 research cycle was published in the *Journal of Clinical Investigation* and concerns a specific molecular pathway that does not merely weaken Tregs — it actively converts them into pro-inflammatory cells.

The sequence works as follows:

1. In MG patients, Tregs often overexpress a short-form protein called **PRDM1-S** (a truncated variant of the BLIMP-1 transcription factor).
2. PRDM1-S activates an enzyme called **SGK-1 (Serum and Glucocorticoid-regulated Kinase 1)**.
3. SGK-1 directly phosphorylates and destabilises FOXP3 at a biochemical level.
4. The Treg "flips" — it stops producing suppressive molecules and begins producing pro-inflammatory cytokines including IL-17 and IFN-gamma.
5. These converted cells are now actively contributing to the autoimmune attack rather than restraining it.

This is not a theoretical concern. The study demonstrated this conversion in cells taken from MG patients, not just in laboratory models. The clinical implication is significant: a patient might appear to have "normal" Treg numbers on a blood test while those cells are, in reality, functioning as inflammatory agents.

### 1.4 Thymic Dysfunction: Broken Before They Leave

The thymus is the gland behind the breastbone where T-cells are educated. In AChR-positive MG — which accounts for around 85% of cases — the thymus is abnormal in the majority of patients. Research in 2024-2025 has clarified the precise mechanism by which the MG thymus produces defective Tregs.

**Medullary thymic epithelial cells (mTECs)** are the cells responsible for educating T-cells in the thymus, including imprinting Treg identity. In the MG thymus, these cells significantly overproduce a signalling molecule called **TSLP (Thymic Stromal Lymphopoietin)**. In small quantities, TSLP is important for normal Treg development. In excess — as occurs in the MG thymus — it interferes with the maturation process, producing Tregs that are functionally defective and inherently unstable before they ever enter the bloodstream.

This means MG patients face a compounding problem: poor quality Tregs produced at source, which are then further destabilised by the PRDM1-S/SGK-1 pathway once in circulation. It also provides the clearest molecular explanation to date for why **thymectomy** (surgical removal of the thymus) produces lasting benefit in many AChR-positive patients: it removes the primary source of defective Treg production.

## Part 2: CAR-T Cell Therapy — The Closest Thing to a Reset

### 2.1 What CAR-T Therapy Is

CAR-T (Chimeric Antigen Receptor T-cell) therapy is a process in which a patient's own T-cells are extracted, genetically modified in a laboratory to target a specific cell type, expanded into large numbers, and reinfused. In cancer treatment, they have been used to hunt and destroy cancer cells. In autoimmune disease, the target is different: the B-cells and plasma cells that produce the damaging antibodies.

The concept is appealing: if you can eliminate the entire population of autoreactive B-cells that have learned to attack the neuromuscular junction, the immune system may repopulate with "naive" new B-cells that have no memory of that attack — an immune reset.

### 2.2 KYV-101 and the KYSA-6 Trial

**KYV-101** (mivocabtagene autoleucel, Kyverna Therapeutics) targets CD19, a protein found on all B-cells. Results presented at the 2025 AANEM (American Association of Neuromuscular and Electrodiagnostic Medicine) Annual Meeting were striking:

- **100% response rate**: All 6 patients in the early cohort achieved clinically meaningful improvement.
- **MG-ADL reduction of -8.0 points** on average at 24 weeks (the MG-ADL scale measures how MG affects daily activities; a reduction of 2 points is considered clinically meaningful).
- **100% of patients were able to stop all background immunosuppressive medications** — including steroids, nonsteroidal immunosuppressants, and newer biologics — by week 24.
- **No serious side effects**: No high-grade Cytokine Release Syndrome (CRS, the dangerous inflammatory reaction sometimes seen with CAR-T) and no neurotoxicity.

KYV-101 received **FDA Regenerative Medicine Advanced Therapy (RMAT) designation** in August 2024, a status that accelerates development for serious conditions with unmet need. Following the KYSA-6 results, the trial expanded into a registrational Phase 2/3 study.

One important note: KYV-101 uses permanent genetic modification of T-cells and requires pre-treatment with low-dose chemotherapy (lymphodepletion) to create space for the CAR-T cells to work. This currently requires inpatient treatment at a specialised centre.

### 2.3 Descartes-08 and the AURORA Trial

**Descartes-08** (Cartesian Therapeutics) takes a different technical approach. Rather than permanently modifying T-cells, it uses **mRNA technology** — the same approach as COVID vaccines — to give T-cells temporary instructions. These CAR-T cells have a self-limiting design and do not permanently alter the patient's DNA.

Descartes-08 targets **BCMA**, a protein found on long-lived plasma cells (the deep-sitting antibody factories in bone marrow that standard treatments often struggle to reach).

Phase 2b data published in *Nature Medicine* in 2025 showed:
- **57% of biologic-naïve patients** achieved "Minimal Symptom Expression" (essentially clinical remission) 12 months after a single 6-week course of treatment.
- The therapy does **not require lymphodepletion** (no chemotherapy pre-treatment), allowing it to be administered in an outpatient setting.

The **AURORA trial**, launched in May 2025, is the first randomised, placebo-controlled Phase 3 trial of a CAR-T therapy in MG. It represents a genuine milestone: evidence of sufficient confidence in the approach to run the definitive test.

A third candidate, **CABA-201** (Cabaletta Bio), is in the **RESET-MG Phase 1/2 trial**, targeting CD19 with the goal of eliminating memory B-cells entirely and allowing a healthy, non-autoreactive B-cell population to regrow.

### 2.4 The Immune Reset Mechanism

The reason these therapies may produce lasting remission rather than just temporary suppression comes down to depth of depletion and the nature of immune memory.

Standard antibody therapies (like rituximab, which also targets CD19) deplete B-cells in the blood and lymph nodes but often fail to reach plasma cells hiding in deep bone marrow niches. CAR-T cells, being living cells that actively hunt their targets, can penetrate these sanctuaries. Once the autoreactive B-cell and plasma cell populations are deeply depleted, the immune system rebuilds from stem cells in the bone marrow. These new B-cells lack the "memory" of the attack on the neuromuscular junction.

The 2025 Treg/FOXP3 Nobel work also highlighted that after the inflammatory burden is removed, the body's own natural Tregs are better able to recover and re-establish self-tolerance — the clearing of the storm allows the peacekeepers to function.

## Part 3: Low-Dose IL-2 — Selectively Boosting the Peacekeepers

### 3.1 The IL-2 Paradox

Interleukin-2 (IL-2) is a signalling molecule with a paradoxical history. At high doses it was used as a cancer treatment — a blunt instrument that activated the entire immune system. At very low doses, a completely different biology emerges: IL-2 selectively stimulates Tregs.

The key is receptor affinity. Tregs carry a high-affinity version of the IL-2 receptor (specifically, the CD25 component makes the receptor bind IL-2 very tightly). Other immune cells have lower-affinity receptors. At low doses, the signal is strong enough to activate Treg receptors but falls below the threshold needed to activate inflammatory cells. This selectivity is the therapeutic opportunity.

### 3.2 Iltolizumab (ILT-101) and the TRANSREG Trial

**Iltolizumab** is a clinical-grade formulation of native low-dose IL-2. A landmark study from 2024, the **TRANSREG "basket" trial**, administered iltolizumab across patients with 13 different autoimmune diseases simultaneously. The key finding: **Treg expansion was universally achieved** across all 13 conditions tested, including Systemic Lupus Erythematosus, Rheumatoid Arthritis, Ankylosing Spondylitis, and inflammatory conditions of the nervous system.

This is important because it validates the mechanism — the selective Treg expansion works regardless of the specific autoimmune condition, suggesting it could be applicable to MG. Phase 2 trials specifically in refractory MG using iltolizumab are now underway following the TRANSREG findings.

An important terminology note for anyone reading the literature: **iltolizumab** (the low-dose IL-2 for Treg expansion) is a different drug from **itolizumab** (an anti-CD6 monoclonal antibody used for graft-versus-host disease). The names are easily confused.

### 3.3 Rezpegaldesleukin (NKTR-358): Engineering Selectivity

**NKTR-358** (rezpegaldesleukin, Nektar Therapeutics) takes a more engineered approach to the same problem. Rather than relying on low dose alone, the IL-2 molecule is chemically modified with **PEG (polyethylene glycol) chains** that physically block the region of the molecule that binds to low-affinity receptors on inflammatory cells. The result is a molecule that is structurally incapable of activating effector T-cells and NK cells, regardless of dose.

NKTR-358 also has a much longer half-life (7-13 days) compared to native IL-2 (hours), allowing for once-weekly or fortnightly dosing.

2025 clinical trial results:
- **REZOLVE-AD** (atopic dermatitis, Phase 2b, February 2025): Met its primary endpoint with high statistical significance. The FDA granted Fast Track designation.
- **REZOLVE-AA** (alopecia areata, Phase 2b, December 2025): Showed significant hair regrowth in the treated group. Core biological data confirmed massive Treg expansion.

The success across multiple autoimmune indications has accelerated interest in applying this approach to MG specifically.

## Part 4: Antigen-Specific Tolerance — Teaching Forgiveness, Not Just Suppression

### 4.1 The Fundamental Difference

Most existing MG treatments suppress the immune system broadly — they turn down the volume on the entire response. This works, but at the cost of reduced ability to fight infections and increased risk of certain cancers over time. The holy grail of autoimmune therapy is **antigen-specific tolerance**: teaching the immune system to accept one specific target (in MG's case, the acetylcholine receptor, or AChR) as "self" while leaving the rest of immune function intact.

### 4.2 CNP-106: Nanoparticle Tolerance Induction

**CNP-106** (COUR Pharmaceuticals) represents the most advanced clinical attempt at antigen-specific tolerance in MG. It uses biodegradable nanoparticles — tiny manufactured spheres — loaded with **seven specific fragments of the AChR protein**.

When injected intravenously, these particles are naturally cleared by the liver and spleen, the same organs that routinely process cellular debris. In these organs, immune sentinel cells (dendritic cells and macrophages) encounter the AChR fragments in the context of normal cellular cleanup rather than inflammation. This context — "this protein is being cleaned up, not fighting an infection" — triggers tolerance rather than activation. Specifically, it promotes the development of Tregs that specifically recognise and suppress immune responses directed at AChR.

Key milestones:
- A multicenter **Phase 1b/2a clinical trial (NCT06106672)** began dosing the first patients in late 2024, evaluating safety, tolerability, and impact on clinical MG scores (MG-ADL and Quantitative MG score).
- **FDA Orphan Drug Designation** was granted in October 2025.
- Research published in *Science Advances* in early 2026 confirmed that the mechanism depends on the **STING/type I interferon pathway** to induce CD4+ T-cell tolerance — providing a molecular explanation for why liver and spleen processing creates tolerance rather than activation.

### 4.3 CAAR-T Therapy: Precision Beyond CAR-T

For patients with **MuSK-positive MG** (where antibodies attack a different receptor called MuSK rather than AChR), an even more targeted approach called **CAAR-T therapy** is in development.

**MuSK-CAART** (Cabaletta Bio) uses Chimeric Autoantibody Receptor T-cells — modified T-cells engineered to carry the MuSK protein on their surface as bait. Only B-cells bearing antibodies that specifically target MuSK will bind to these cells and be destroyed. B-cells carrying antibodies for every other target in the body are left completely untouched.

This therapy is currently in **Phase 1 trials (MusCAARTes)** and has received **FDA Fast Track designation**. It represents perhaps the most surgically precise immune intervention ever attempted in autoimmune disease.

## Part 5: TSLP and the Thymus — Fixing the Problem at Source

### 5.1 The Upstream Opportunity

All the therapies described so far work at various stages of the immune response: resetting B-cells (CAR-T), expanding Tregs (IL-2), re-teaching tolerance (nanoparticles). TSLP blockade represents a different and arguably more fundamental opportunity: intervening at the point where the immune problem originates, inside the thymus itself.

### 5.2 How TSLP Derails Treg Development

As described in Part 1, the **medullary thymic epithelial cells (mTECs)** in the MG thymus significantly overproduce TSLP compared to healthy individuals. In normal circumstances, TSLP is one of several signals that guides the development of Tregs during their "education" in the thymus. In excess, it becomes disruptive — producing Tregs that are inherently unstable before they ever leave the gland.

2024 research additionally identified that **miR-19b-5p** — a small non-coding RNA — is elevated in MG thymic tissue and acts as a regulator of the TSLP overproduction axis. This is particularly notable in thymoma-associated MG (where a tumour develops in the thymus), suggesting TSLP dysregulation may be even more pronounced in this subgroup.

In vitro studies have shown that **neutralising TSLP** can partially restore the suppressive function of MG-derived Tregs, even after they have been extracted from a patient — suggesting the damage is partly reversible.

### 5.3 Tezepelumab: A Drug Already on the Shelf

**Tezepelumab (Tezspire)** is a fully human monoclonal antibody that blocks TSLP. It is already FDA-approved for severe asthma, and in 2024 the **COURSE trial** demonstrated its effectiveness in COPD, reducing exacerbations by 37%. An application for chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyps was on track for late 2025 approval.

This matters enormously for MG research: a drug with a well-established safety profile, an understood mechanism directly relevant to MG thymic dysfunction, and existing manufacturing infrastructure could potentially be studied in MG without the years of safety groundwork needed for a brand-new molecule.

While large-scale Phase 3 trials in MG were still early stage as of 2025, the mechanistic case is strong and has attracted significant research attention. Additional anti-TSLP agents in early development include **HBM9378** and **CM326**, both in Phase 1/2 trials as of 2025.

## Part 6: T-Cell Metabolism — A New Class of Target

### 6.1 Why Metabolism Matters for Immunity

Activated T-cells — whether they are fighting an infection or, inappropriately, attacking the body's own tissues — have dramatically different energy requirements from resting cells. They switch their metabolism from efficient, oxygen-dependent energy production (oxidative phosphorylation) to faster but less efficient glucose burning (glycolysis). Disrupting this metabolic shift in autoreactive T-cells, without disrupting normal immune function, is an emerging therapeutic frontier.

### 6.2 ABHD11 and the "Sterol Shunt"

Research published in *Nature Communications* in 2025 identified **ABHD11** as a key metabolic enzyme in T-cells. ABHD11 is a deglutarylating enzyme that regulates a step in the TCA cycle (the cellular energy cycle occurring in mitochondria) via a compound called lipoate.

When ABHD11 is inhibited — using a compound called **ML-226** in laboratory studies — a cascade of metabolic changes occurs, culminating in the production of a sterol molecule called **24,25-epoxycholesterol**. This molecule activates **LXR (Liver X Receptor)**, which in turn suppresses the inflammatory effector functions of overactive T-cells.

This pathway has been termed the **"Sterol Shunt"** — a metabolic bypass that effectively dials down T-cell inflammatory activity without requiring a direct immunosuppressive drug. Because ABHD11 is expressed at higher levels in activated T-cells than resting cells, this approach has an inherent degree of selectivity.

### 6.3 QRICH1: The Intracellular Brake

A separate 2025 study in *Science Immunology* identified **QRICH1** as what researchers are calling an "intracellular checkpoint" — an internal braking mechanism within CD8+ T-cells (a subset of T-cells that kill target cells directly).

QRICH1 works by binding to the **CARD11 signalosome**, a molecular complex that activates three major inflammatory signalling pathways: NF-kB, JNK, and mTOR. By binding to this complex, QRICH1 physically blocks its activity, preventing CD8+ T-cells from becoming hyper-responsive.

In autoimmune disease, CD8+ T-cells that have lost effective QRICH1 braking become persistently over-activated, contributing to tissue damage. The discovery of QRICH1 provides a new target: drugs that restore or mimic QRICH1 function could selectively cool down autoreactive CD8+ T-cells without broadly suppressing the immune system.

Both ABHD11 inhibition and QRICH1 enhancement represent what researchers are calling **metabolic targeting immunotherapy** — a field that is likely to produce drug candidates for clinical trials within the next 3-5 years.

## Part 7: EPO, STING, and Unexpected Routes to Immune Tolerance

### 7.1 Erythropoietin Beyond Red Blood Cells

**Erythropoietin (EPO)** is best known as the hormone produced by the kidneys to stimulate red blood cell production — and infamously as a doping agent in endurance sports. A separate line of research, highlighted by work from Stanford in 2024-2025, has revealed that EPO has significant **non-hematopoietic immune functions** that could be directly relevant to autoimmunity.

EPO receptors are expressed on dendritic cells — the immune system's "intelligence gatherers" that decide whether to trigger an attack response or a tolerance response to what they encounter. Research has shown that EPO signalling in dendritic cells promotes a **tolerogenic phenotype**: it pushes dendritic cells toward instructing T-cells to become tolerant rather than reactive.

This discovery links to the broader STING research area, as EPO-stimulated dendritic cells appear to modulate the STING pathway's activation threshold. Clinical EPO derivatives that maintain the immune-modulating function without the red-blood-cell-stimulating function (to avoid the cardiovascular risks of polycythaemia) are now being explored as a potential approach to restoring immune tolerance in autoimmune conditions.

### 7.2 STING Overactivity: A Common Thread in Autoimmunity

The **STING (Stimulator of Interferon Genes) pathway** is the immune system's alarm system for detecting DNA in the wrong place — normally a signal of a virus. In autoimmune disease, STING can become chronically overactive, mistaking the body's own cellular debris for viral DNA and maintaining a persistent state of low-grade inflammation that erodes tolerance mechanisms including Treg stability.

2024-2025 research has established STING overactivity as a shared driver across multiple autoimmune conditions, including lupus, inflammatory arthritis, and conditions affecting the nervous system. In the context of MG, chronic STING activation in thymic cells is hypothesised to contribute to the inflammatory microenvironment that produces dysfunctional Tregs.

**STING PROTACs** (molecules that degrade the STING protein rather than merely inhibiting it) are in early development as a more complete approach to breaking this inflammatory cycle. The CNP-106 nanoparticle tolerance work (described in Part 4) is notable because it works *with* the STING pathway — using it to instruct tolerance rather than trying to suppress the entire pathway.

## Part 8: Biomarkers — Knowing Who Needs What

### 8.1 The Th17/Treg Ratio

One of the most clinically useful measures to emerge from 2024-2025 research is the **Th17/Treg ratio**. Th17 cells are pro-inflammatory T-cells; Tregs suppress inflammation. The ratio between them in the blood reflects the balance (or imbalance) of the immune system at a given moment.

Research has confirmed that the ratio is driven substantially by **IL-6 and IL-21**, which simultaneously promote Th17 development and suppress Treg stability. An elevated Th17/Treg ratio correlates with active disease and elevated inflammation markers.

In practical terms, this ratio is emerging as a useful tool for:
- Assessing whether a patient is in a period of active disease versus relative remission
- Predicting who may need more aggressive treatment
- Monitoring the effectiveness of Treg-boosting therapies (such as low-dose IL-2)

### 8.2 Memory B-Cells and Predicting Refractory Disease

A distinct immune signature has been identified that predicts **treatment-resistant (refractory) MG** before it becomes apparent through clinical failure:

- **Low circulating Treg numbers** combined with
- **High numbers of memory B-cells**

This combination identifies patients who are unlikely to respond adequately to conventional immunosuppression with steroids and steroid-sparing agents, and who may benefit from early escalation to targeted therapies. The identification of this signature in early disease could eventually allow clinicians to match patients to appropriate treatment intensity from the outset, rather than waiting for clinical failure.

The KYSA-6 CAR-T trial data demonstrated that successful B-cell depletion was associated with a restoration of the Treg/memory B-cell balance in responders, validating the biomarker's biological relevance.

### 8.3 MicroRNA Biomarkers

MicroRNAs (miRNAs) are tiny non-coding RNA molecules that regulate gene expression and can be detected in blood samples. Several are showing clinical promise as MG-specific biomarkers:

**miR-150-5p**: Elevated in the serum of MG patients generally. Importantly, levels specifically decrease following successful thymectomy, making it a potential objective measure of surgical outcome — useful in a condition where clinical response to thymectomy can take months or years to fully emerge.

**miR-21-5p**: Also elevated in active MG and decreases with successful immunotherapy. Together with miR-150-5p, it could form a panel for monitoring treatment response.

**miR-30e-5p**: A predictor of **ocular-to-generalised MG progression**. Elevated levels in a patient presenting with only eye and eyelid symptoms may indicate a higher risk that symptoms will spread to affect limbs, breathing, or swallowing. This would allow earlier preventive treatment in high-risk patients.

**miR-19b-5p**: Found elevated specifically in thymic tissue in MG and acts as a regulator of TSLP overproduction. This connects the miRNA biomarker field to the thymic dysfunction research described in Part 5.

### 8.4 Stress, Flares, and Predicting Crisis

At a molecular level, the link between physiological stress and MG flares is now understood with greater precision. Cortisol and adrenaline suppress Egr-1 (the FOXP3 stability switch described in Part 1) and disrupt FOXP3 methylation in Tregs. This creates a predictable window of reduced immune regulation that corresponds to the clinical observation that MG patients are at higher risk of flares and crisis during and after major infections, surgeries, or periods of intense physical or emotional stress.

This molecular understanding may eventually lead to **preventive protocols** — brief courses of Treg-stabilising agents around high-stress periods — to buffer against stress-induced flares. It also reinforces the practical importance of stress management, adequate sleep, and careful infection management in day-to-day MG care.

## Synthesis: How These Research Areas Connect

What becomes clear when viewing these eight research areas together is that they are not parallel independent threads — they are converging on the same underlying problems from different angles.

**The central problem is Treg failure.** Tregs are produced defectively in the MG thymus (TSLP dysregulation), chemically destabilised in the bloodstream (PRDM1-S/SGK-1 pathway), and made unstable by the inflammatory environment their own absence creates (Th17/Treg imbalance). Stress compounds all of this acutely.

**The solutions cluster around two broad strategies:**

The first is **resetting the immune system** — using CAR-T therapy to wipe the autoreactive memory clean and allow rebuilding from scratch. This is the most radical approach and is producing the most dramatic clinical results, with drug-free remission now documented in small but real patient populations.

The second is **restoring regulation** — using low-dose IL-2, antigen-specific nanoparticles, TSLP blockade, or metabolic targeting to rebuild the Treg population or fix its function without a full immune reset. These approaches are less dramatic but may prove more accessible, repeatable, and applicable earlier in the disease course.

**Biomarkers are the bridge** between these strategies. The Th17/Treg ratio, memory B-cell counts, and microRNA profiles are increasingly able to tell us which patients need which approach — and when.

The emergence of EPO's immune-tolerogenic function and STING's role as an inflammatory driver adds a further layer: there are systemic physiological processes beyond the immune system's internal machinery that modulate autoimmune activity, and these may eventually be targetable as adjuncts to the specific immunological therapies.

---

## What This Means for People Living with MG

The current standard of care for MG — pyridostigmine for symptoms, immunosuppressants for the underlying immune problem, and newer biologics (complement inhibitors, FcRn blockers) for refractory cases — remains effective and is not being replaced by these discoveries. But the direction of travel has changed substantially.

**In the near term (2025-2028):** CAR-T trials will complete their registrational phases, and the first therapies likely to achieve drug-free remission in larger patient populations will become available in specialist centres. Low-dose IL-2 trials in MG-specific populations will report. CNP-106 will complete Phase 2 data. TSLP blockade trials will launch.

**In the medium term (2028-2032):** Biomarker-guided treatment selection will become standard — meaning treatment will increasingly be matched to a patient's specific immune profile rather than a one-size-fits-all escalation ladder. Metabolic immunotherapy agents targeting ABHD11 and QRICH1 may enter clinical trials. The precision of approaches like CAAR-T may expand.

**The long-term picture** is one in which MG transitions from a condition requiring lifelong immunosuppression — with all the cumulative risks that entails — to one where an initial period of intensive therapy (immune reset or tolerance induction) is followed by stable, sustained remission with minimal or no ongoing treatment. For many patients, this remains aspirational rather than current reality. But for the first time, it is a medically credible aspiration, backed by human clinical data.
