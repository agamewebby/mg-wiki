### 1.1 Pyridostigmine (Mestinon)

**Drug Class:** Acetylcholinesterase inhibitor (symptomatic treatment)
**FDA/MHRA Status:** Long-established approval for MG

#### Mechanism of Action
Pyridostigmine reversibly inhibits the enzyme acetylcholinesterase at the neuromuscular junction (NMJ), preventing the breakdown of acetylcholine (ACh). This increases the concentration and duration of ACh in the synaptic cleft, compensating for the reduced number of functional ACh receptors caused by autoantibody-mediated damage. It does not treat the underlying autoimmune disease — it is purely symptomatic.

It is the standard first-line symptomatic treatment for all forms of generalised MG and many ocular MG cases. It works within 30–60 minutes of an oral dose and lasts approximately 3–4 hours.

#### Side Effects

**Common (cholinergic effects — predictable and dose-related):**
- Gastrointestinal: nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, abdominal cramps
- Increased salivation and lacrimation
- Muscle twitching and fasciculations
- Sweating (diaphoresis)
- Bradycardia

**Serious:**
- **Cholinergic crisis**: An overdose of pyridostigmine causes excessive ACh accumulation, paradoxically worsening weakness. Clinically distinguishable from myasthenic crisis by the presence of SLUDGE symptoms (Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defaecation, GI distress, Emesis) plus miosis and bradycardia. Requires immediate dose reduction and may require atropine.
- Bronchospasm and increased bronchial secretions (hazardous in patients with asthma or COPD)

**Rare:**
- Seizures at very high doses
- Severe bradyarrhythmias

#### Contraindications

**Absolute:**
- Mechanical obstruction of the gastrointestinal or urinary tract
- Known hypersensitivity to pyridostigmine or bromide compounds

**Relative:**
- Asthma or COPD (increased bronchial secretions)
- Cardiac arrhythmias (vagotonic effects)
- Peptic ulcer disease
- Epilepsy

#### Drug Interactions

| Interacting Drug | Effect | Clinical Action |
|---|---|---|
| Beta-blockers | Additive bradycardia; may antagonise the positive inotropic effects needed at NMJ | Use with caution; monitor HR |
| Aminoglycoside antibiotics (e.g. gentamicin) | These antibiotics impair NMJ transmission and may worsen MG, partially offsetting pyridostigmine benefit | Avoid if possible; see Drugs to Avoid |
| Quinolone antibiotics (e.g. ciprofloxacin) | May exacerbate MG weakness | Caution — consider alternative antibiotics |
| Magnesium (IV) | Magnesium directly impairs NMJ and antagonises pyridostigmine | Strictly contraindicated in MG (see pregnancy section) |
| Neuromuscular blocking agents | Pyridostigmine may prolong the effects of depolarising agents (suxamethonium) and can be used to reverse non-depolarising agents | Inform anaesthetist; careful perioperative planning required |
| Atropine | Antagonises cholinergic side effects — used therapeutically if needed | May be given to manage cholinergic side effects |

#### Special Populations

**Pregnancy:** Oral pyridostigmine is considered safe throughout pregnancy. IV forms should be avoided in the third trimester as they can trigger uterine contractions.

**Breastfeeding:** Compatible — transfer into breast milk is minimal.

**Elderly:** Use with caution due to increased sensitivity to cholinergic side effects (particularly diarrhoea, bradycardia, increased secretions). Start at the lower end of the dose range.

**Renal impairment:** Pyridostigmine is renally excreted. Dose reduction required in moderate-to-severe renal impairment to avoid accumulation and cholinergic toxicity.

**Hepatic impairment:** Minimal hepatic metabolism; generally safe.

#### Dosing Notes
- Standard oral dose: 30–60 mg every 4–6 hours, titrated to response
- Maximum dose: typically 480 mg/day (higher doses increase cholinergic side effects without added benefit)
- Slow-release formulations (Mestinon Timespan 180 mg) available for overnight dosing
- Note: MuSK-antibody positive MG patients often respond poorly to pyridostigmine and may require higher doses of immunosuppression earlier

### 1.2 Prednisolone / Prednisone

**Drug Class:** Corticosteroid (systemic immunosuppressant)
**Status:** Backbone of MG immunosuppression; widely used for decades

#### Mechanism of Action
Corticosteroids exert broad immunosuppressive and anti-inflammatory effects through genomic mechanisms (binding to intracellular glucocorticoid receptors, altering gene transcription) and non-genomic mechanisms. In MG, key effects include:
- Reduction of B-cell activation and autoantibody production (including anti-AChR and anti-MuSK antibodies)
- Suppression of T-helper cell responses
- Inhibition of cytokine production (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-alpha)
- Reduction in complement-mediated NMJ damage

Onset of benefit in MG is typically 2–4 weeks, with maximum response at 6–12 months.

**Important warning:** In approximately 30–50% of patients, steroids cause an initial transient worsening of weakness in the first 1–3 weeks of treatment (the "steroid dip"). Starting at low doses and titrating up slowly reduces this risk. For patients with significant bulbar or respiratory compromise, steroids may need to be started in hospital.

#### Side Effects

**Common (especially with long-term use):**
- Weight gain and redistribution of body fat (Cushingoid appearance: moon face, buffalo hump)
- Fluid retention and oedema
- Hyperglycaemia / steroid-induced diabetes
- Hypertension
- Insomnia, mood changes, irritability (steroid-induced psychiatric effects)
- Increased appetite

**Serious (long-term):**
- Osteoporosis and fragility fractures (highest risk: vertebral and hip fractures)
- Avascular necrosis of bone (especially femoral head) — can occur even with short courses
- Cataracts (posterior subcapsular)
- Glaucoma
- Adrenal suppression and insufficiency on abrupt withdrawal
- Immunosuppression: susceptibility to opportunistic infections (including reactivation of latent tuberculosis, Pneumocystis pneumonia)
- Gastrointestinal ulceration and bleeding
- Muscle weakness (steroid myopathy — can confound MG assessment)

**Rare:**
- Steroid psychosis
- Pancreatitis
- Steroid-induced glaucoma progressing to blindness
- Severe opportunistic infections (fungal, viral, bacterial)

#### Contraindications

**Absolute:**
- Active, untreated systemic infection (especially fungal or TB)
- Known hypersensitivity to the formulation

**Relative:**
- Uncontrolled diabetes mellitus
- Active peptic ulcer disease
- Severe osteoporosis
- Active psychiatric illness
- Heart failure (fluid retention risk)
- Glaucoma

#### Drug Interactions

| Interacting Drug | Effect | Clinical Action |
|---|---|---|
| NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) | Additive gastric ulcer and GI bleeding risk | Use with PPI prophylaxis; prefer paracetamol |
| Antidiabetic drugs (metformin, insulin) | Corticosteroids raise blood glucose; may require dose adjustment of antidiabetics | Monitor glucose frequently |
| Antihypertensives | Steroids cause fluid retention and raise BP | Monitor BP; may need antihypertensive dose increase |
| Live vaccines | Contraindicated during corticosteroid therapy (risk of vaccine-induced disease) | Avoid live vaccines; plan before starting immunosuppression |
| Warfarin / anticoagulants | Variable effects on INR | Monitor INR closely |
| Ciclosporin / Tacrolimus | Mutually interacting metabolism; steroids may affect levels | Monitor drug levels |
| Rifampicin, phenytoin, carbamazepine | Enzyme inducers reduce steroid efficacy | May need higher steroid doses |

#### Special Populations

**Pregnancy:** Prednisolone is the preferred immunosuppressant in pregnancy. It is extensively metabolised by the placenta (unlike dexamethasone/betamethasone which cross freely). Risk of neonatal adrenal suppression if high doses used near term. Slight increased risk of cleft palate in first trimester. Overall: acceptable risk-benefit ratio. See section 4.

**Breastfeeding:** Compatible at doses up to 40 mg/day. Transfer into milk is low; prednisolone is preferred over prednisone.

**Elderly:** High risk of corticosteroid adverse effects: osteoporosis prophylaxis (calcium, vitamin D, bisphosphonate) is mandatory. Increased risk of hyperglycaemia, infections, and psychiatric effects. Careful BP and blood glucose monitoring essential.

**Renal impairment:** Generally safe; minor dose adjustments may be needed in severe impairment.

**Hepatic impairment:** Prednisone requires hepatic conversion to active prednisolone — in severe liver disease, use prednisolone directly. Avoid high doses in significant hepatic impairment.

#### Bone Protection Protocol
All patients on prednisolone >7.5 mg/day for more than 3 months should receive:
- Calcium supplementation (1000–1200 mg/day)
- Vitamin D3 (800–1000 IU/day)
- Bisphosphonate (e.g. alendronate 70 mg weekly or risedronate) unless contraindicated
- DEXA bone density scan at baseline and every 1–2 years

#### 2025 Dosing Guidance
The **2025 ABN Update** recommends **daily dosing** over the previously popular alternate-day regimen. Daily dosing provides more stable immune suppression and avoids the symptom "yo-yo" effect seen with alternate-day therapy. Typical starting dose: 10–20 mg/day increasing to 60–80 mg/day, then tapering once stable.

### 1.3 Azathioprine (Imuran)

**Drug Class:** Purine synthesis inhibitor (antimetabolite immunosuppressant)
**Status:** Most widely used steroid-sparing agent in MG; standard of care

#### Mechanism of Action
Azathioprine is a pro-drug that is converted to 6-mercaptopurine (6-MP) and subsequently to thioguanine nucleotides (6-TGN), which are incorporated into DNA, inhibiting purine synthesis. This selectively suppresses proliferating lymphocytes (both T and B cells), reducing autoantibody production. The enzyme TPMT (thiopurine methyltransferase) regulates metabolism; genetic variants affect toxicity risk.

Onset of benefit in MG: **12–18 months** (slow-acting). Used primarily as a steroid-sparing agent to allow steroid dose reduction once therapeutic effect is established.

#### Side Effects

**Common:**
- Nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain (especially in first weeks — can be managed by splitting doses or taking with food)
- "Azathioprine flu" (idiosyncratic febrile reaction in first 2–4 weeks in ~10%: fever, malaise, nausea, rash — requires drug discontinuation)
- Fatigue
- Hepatotoxicity (elevated transaminases)

**Serious:**
- **Myelosuppression**: Leukopenia, thrombocytopenia, and anaemia — potentially severe and life-threatening, especially with TPMT deficiency or allopurinol interaction
- **Increased infection risk**: Including viral reactivation (VZV, CMV, EBV)
- Hepatotoxicity (cholestatic or hepatocellular)
- Pancreatitis (rare idiosyncratic reaction)

**Long-term / Rare:**
- Increased risk of malignancy, particularly non-melanoma skin cancers (squamous cell carcinoma) and lymphoma. Risk increases with duration of use and cumulative dose.
- Macrocytosis (often used as a marker of drug activity; not itself harmful)

#### Contraindications

**Absolute:**
- Pregnancy (teratogenic risk — category D) — though some guidelines consider it acceptable when benefit outweighs risk; see section 4
- Hypersensitivity to azathioprine or 6-MP
- Concurrent use with allopurinol or febuxostat without dose reduction (see drug interactions — life-threatening)

**Relative:**
- Active severe infection
- Known TPMT deficiency (homozygous) — dramatically increases toxicity risk
- Significant hepatic impairment
- Pre-existing bone marrow suppression

#### Drug Interactions

**CRITICAL INTERACTION — Azathioprine + Allopurinol/Febuxostat:**

This is a **Grade A life-threatening interaction** that must be understood by all MG patients and prescribers.

- **Mechanism:** Azathioprine is metabolised to 6-MP, which is inactivated by the enzyme xanthine oxidase (XO). Allopurinol (used for gout) and febuxostat are both XO inhibitors. When XO is blocked, 6-MP accumulates to toxic levels.
- **Outcome:** Severe, potentially fatal **bone marrow suppression** (profound leukopenia, anaemia, thrombocytopenia)
- **Management:** Avoid this combination whenever possible. If allopurinol is absolutely necessary, the azathioprine dose must be **reduced by 75%** (to 25% of the original dose) with weekly full blood count monitoring.
- **Practical note:** Patients with MG on azathioprine should be warned that if a doctor prescribes allopurinol or febuxostat (for gout), they MUST immediately inform their neurologist.

| Interacting Drug | Effect | Clinical Action |
|---|---|---|
| Allopurinol / Febuxostat | 4-fold increase in 6-MP toxicity; fatal bone marrow suppression | CONTRAINDICATED unless dose reduced by 75% |
| Warfarin | Azathioprine may reduce warfarin effect | Monitor INR closely |
| ACE inhibitors | Additive risk of leukopenia | Monitor FBC |
| Live vaccines | Contraindicated during immunosuppression | Avoid; plan vaccinations before starting |
| Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (co-trimoxazole) | Additive bone marrow suppression | Monitor FBC |
| Ribavirin (hepatitis C treatment) | May inhibit TPMT, altering azathioprine metabolism | Caution; monitor |

#### Pre-Treatment Assessment
- **TPMT genotyping or enzyme activity testing is strongly recommended** before starting azathioprine
  - Normal TPMT: standard dosing (1.5–2.5 mg/kg/day)
  - Heterozygous (intermediate metaboliser): start at 50% of normal dose, monitor closely
  - Homozygous deficient: azathioprine is contraindicated (extreme myelosuppression risk)
- Check FBC, LFTs, renal function, and TPMT before starting
- Hepatitis B surface antigen and core antibody — reactivation risk
- Varicella immunity status

#### Monitoring Requirements
- FBC and LFTs: weekly for 8 weeks, then fortnightly for 3 months, then every 3 months
- Stop drug if WBC <3.0 x 10⁹/L or neutrophils <1.5 x 10⁹/L
- MCV often rises (macrocytosis) — useful marker of therapeutic activity
- Target: lymphocyte count 0.6–1.0 x 10⁹/L

#### Special Populations

**Pregnancy:** Classified as category D. Crosses the placenta. Some evidence of neonatal immunosuppression and bone marrow suppression; theoretical teratogenicity risk. Many specialist centres consider it acceptable to continue if MG is severe and unstable, weighing risk-benefit carefully. The EUROMYASTHENIA-PREG registry data suggests outcomes are generally acceptable when necessary.

**Breastfeeding:** Low levels in breast milk; considered compatible by some guidelines. Monitor infant FBC if used.

**Elderly:** Increased sensitivity to myelosuppression; use lower doses and monitor more frequently. Increased malignancy risk with long-term use.

**Renal impairment:** Use with caution and dose reduction in severe impairment; accumulation of metabolites.

**Hepatic impairment:** Risk of hepatotoxicity; monitor LFTs closely. Avoid in severe hepatic disease.

### 1.4 Mycophenolate Mofetil (CellCept)

**Drug Class:** Purine synthesis inhibitor (selective lymphocyte immunosuppressant)
**Status:** Second-line steroid-sparing agent; increasingly used as first-line alternative to azathioprine

#### Mechanism of Action
Mycophenolate mofetil (MMF) is hydrolysed to mycophenolic acid (MPA), which selectively inhibits inosine monophosphate dehydrogenase (IMPDH), a key enzyme in the de novo pathway of purine synthesis. Lymphocytes (unlike most other cells) are uniquely dependent on this pathway, making MMF highly selective for immune cells. It suppresses both T and B lymphocyte proliferation, reducing autoantibody production.

Onset of benefit in MG: **6–12 months** (faster than azathioprine for some patients). Commonly used when azathioprine is not tolerated or has failed. Particularly used in combination with rituximab for MuSK-positive MG.

#### Side Effects

**Common:**
- Gastrointestinal: diarrhoea, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain (often dose-related; enteric-coated formulation [Myfortic] may reduce GI effects)
- Fatigue
- Headache
- Peripheral oedema

**Serious:**
- Myelosuppression: leukopenia, anaemia, thrombocytopenia
- Increased susceptibility to infections (bacterial, viral, fungal, opportunistic)
- Viral reactivation: CMV, BK virus, EBV, HSV
- Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML) due to JC virus reactivation (rare but serious)

**Long-term / Rare:**
- Increased risk of malignancy (lymphoma, non-melanoma skin cancer) with long-term use
- Pure red cell aplasia (rare)

#### Contraindications

**Absolute:**
- **Pregnancy — ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATION.** MMF is highly teratogenic. Associated with miscarriage (25–45% spontaneous abortion risk) and a specific pattern of birth defects (MMF embryopathy: cleft lip/palate, ear abnormalities, cardiac defects). Must be discontinued at least **6 weeks before planned conception**. Two forms of contraception required for women of childbearing age.
- Active serious infection
- Hypersensitivity to MMF or MPA

**Relative:**
- Renal impairment (dose adjustment needed)
- Planned surgery (increased infection risk)

#### Drug Interactions

| Interacting Drug | Effect | Clinical Action |
|---|---|---|
| Antacids (aluminium/magnesium-containing) | Reduce MMF absorption | Separate administration by 2 hours |
| Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) | May reduce MMF levels (mechanism: altered pH) | Monitor clinical response |
| Rifampicin | Significantly reduces MMF levels | Avoid combination |
| Colestyramine / cholestyramine | Reduces MMF levels by interrupting enterohepatic circulation | Avoid |
| Aciclovir / Ganciclovir | Competitive renal excretion; may increase both drugs' concentrations | Monitor renal function |
| Live vaccines | Contraindicated during immunosuppression | Avoid |
| Combined oral contraceptive pill | MMF may reduce contraceptive efficacy | Use additional contraception (condoms) |
| Ciclosporin | Ciclosporin reduces enterohepatic recirculation of MPA, lowering MMF levels | Monitor efficacy when combining |

#### Monitoring Requirements
- FBC and LFTs: baseline, then fortnightly for 3 months, then monthly for 9 months, then every 3 months
- Pregnancy testing: mandatory in women of childbearing potential before starting and regularly during treatment (FDA mandates REMS programme)
- Renal function monitoring

#### Special Populations

**Pregnancy:** ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATION. Teratogenic; causes miscarriage and birth defects. Must stop 6 weeks before conception. Men planning fatherhood: limited evidence but some guidance suggests stopping 90 days before conception.

**Breastfeeding:** CONTRAINDICATED — excreted in breast milk; theoretical risk to infant.

**Elderly:** Use with caution; increased susceptibility to infections.

**Renal impairment:** Dose reduction required. Avoid doses >1 g twice daily in severe impairment.

**Hepatic impairment:** Generally safe; primarily renal excretion. Monitor LFTs.

### 1.5 Ciclosporin / Cyclosporine (Neoral)

**Drug Class:** Calcineurin inhibitor (selective T-cell immunosuppressant)
**Status:** Third-line agent; used in refractory MG or when other agents not tolerated

#### Mechanism of Action
Ciclosporin binds to the intracellular protein cyclophilin, and this complex inhibits calcineurin, a phosphatase that is essential for activating transcription factors (particularly NFAT) that drive T-cell activation and interleukin-2 (IL-2) production. IL-2 is critical for T-cell proliferation and T-helper cell support of B-cell autoantibody production. By selectively suppressing T-cell activation, ciclosporin indirectly reduces AChR autoantibody production.

Onset of benefit in MG: approximately **3–6 months** (faster than azathioprine). More rapid but more toxic profile.

**Important:** Brand-name prescribing (Neoral) is required as different formulations have different bioavailability; generic substitution can cause unpredictable drug level changes.

#### Side Effects

**Common:**
- **Nephrotoxicity**: dose-related renal impairment (creatinine elevation) — the most important long-term toxicity
- Hypertension (affects up to 50% of patients)
- Hirsutism (excess hair growth)
- Gingival hyperplasia (gum overgrowth)
- Tremor and headaches
- Nausea and GI disturbances
- Hyperlipidaemia

**Serious:**
- Nephrotoxicity progressing to chronic kidney disease with long-term use
- Hypertensive crisis
- Neurotoxicity (tremor, seizures, encephalopathy at high levels)
- Hepatotoxicity
- Increased infection risk (including viral reactivation)

**Long-term / Rare:**
- Increased malignancy risk: lymphoma, non-melanoma skin cancer (squamous cell carcinoma particularly)
- Thrombotic microangiopathy (TMA) — rare

#### Contraindications

**Absolute:**
- Renal impairment (significant pre-existing CKD)
- Uncontrolled hypertension
- Hypersensitivity to ciclosporin

**Relative:**
- Diabetes mellitus (worsens glycaemic control)
- Active infection
- Malignancy (current or recent)
- Hyperlipidaemia

#### Drug Interactions — EXTENSIVE (CYP3A4 pathway)

Ciclosporin is a substrate and inhibitor of CYP3A4 (hepatic) and P-glycoprotein. This creates numerous clinically significant interactions:

| Drug Class | Examples | Effect | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| CYP3A4 inhibitors (increase ciclosporin levels — TOXICITY RISK) | Azole antifungals (fluconazole, itraconazole, ketoconazole), macrolide antibiotics (erythromycin, clarithromycin), diltiazem, verapamil, amiodarone, **grapefruit juice** | Elevated ciclosporin levels → nephrotoxicity, neurotoxicity | Monitor levels closely; avoid grapefruit juice entirely |
| CYP3A4 inducers (decrease ciclosporin levels — EFFICACY LOSS) | Rifampicin, phenytoin, carbamazepine, phenobarbitone, **St John's Wort** | Reduced ciclosporin levels → MG relapse | Avoid; if unavoidable, monitor levels |
| Nephrotoxic drugs | Aminoglycosides, NSAIDs, amphotericin B, ACE inhibitors | Additive nephrotoxicity | Avoid combination; monitor renal function |
| Statins (HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors) | Simvastatin, lovastatin | Ciclosporin inhibits statin metabolism → rhabdomyolysis risk | Use low-dose pravastatin or rosuvastatin instead |
| Potassium-sparing diuretics / ACE inhibitors | Spironolactone, amiloride | Additive hyperkalaemia | Monitor electrolytes |

#### Monitoring Requirements
- **Therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM):** Target trough levels 100–200 ng/mL (MG; lower than transplant protocols)
- Renal function (creatinine, eGFR): fortnightly until stable, then every 1–3 months
- Blood pressure: regular monitoring
- LFTs, FBC, electrolytes (particularly potassium), uric acid
- Lipid profile

#### Special Populations

**Pregnancy:** Category C. Associated with intrauterine growth restriction and premature birth. Not teratogenic but potential neonatal toxicity. Used when benefit outweighs risk; some specialist centres allow continuation. Monitor fetal growth. Not contraindicated but requires careful risk-benefit discussion.

**Breastfeeding:** Present in breast milk; generally advised against.

**Elderly:** Increased sensitivity to nephrotoxicity and hypertension; use lower doses with careful monitoring.

**Renal impairment:** Contraindicated in significant pre-existing renal disease; worsens kidney function.

**Hepatic impairment:** Extensive hepatic metabolism; monitor levels and LFTs; dose reduction may be needed.

### 1.6 Tacrolimus (Prograf)

**Drug Class:** Calcineurin inhibitor (T-cell immunosuppressant)
**Status:** Alternative to ciclosporin; particularly useful in refractory MG; some evidence for MuSK-positive MG; more commonly used in Japan where there is an established evidence base

#### Mechanism of Action
Tacrolimus binds to FK-binding protein 12 (FKBP12), and this complex inhibits calcineurin, blocking NFAT transcription and IL-2 production in the same way as ciclosporin but approximately 10–100 times more potent. In MG, it suppresses autoreactive T-helper cells, reducing AChR and MuSK autoantibody production.

Onset of benefit: **3–6 months**, similar to ciclosporin. Unlike ciclosporin, tacrolimus causes less hirsutism and gingival hyperplasia but more neurotoxicity and new-onset diabetes.

#### Side Effects

**Common:**
- Tremor (fine hand tremor — hallmark)
- Headache and insomnia
- Hyperglycaemia and new-onset diabetes (NODAT — new-onset diabetes after transplant/treatment)
- Nephrotoxicity (less than ciclosporin in some studies but still significant)
- Hypertension (less frequent than with ciclosporin)
- GI disturbances (diarrhoea, nausea)
- Hyperkalaemia
- Hypomagnesaemia

**Serious:**
- Nephrotoxicity → chronic kidney disease
- Neurotoxicity: tremor, seizures, posterior reversible encephalopathy syndrome (PRES) at toxic levels
- New-onset diabetes mellitus
- Increased infection susceptibility
- Prolonged QT interval (cardiac arrhythmia risk)

**Long-term / Rare:**
- Malignancy (lymphoma, skin cancer)
- Pure red cell aplasia
- Thrombotic microangiopathy

#### Contraindications

**Absolute:**
- Hypersensitivity to tacrolimus or macrolide antibiotics (structural similarity)
- Concomitant use of ciclosporin (additive nephrotoxicity and calcineurin inhibitor toxicity)

**Relative:**
- Pre-existing renal impairment
- Diabetes mellitus
- Pre-existing QT prolongation or cardiac disease
- Active severe infection

#### Drug Interactions
Very similar to ciclosporin (shared CYP3A4 pathway):

| Drug Class | Examples | Effect | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| CYP3A4/P-gp inhibitors | Azole antifungals, macrolides (clarithromycin, erythromycin), diltiazem, amiodarone, **grapefruit juice** | Elevated tacrolimus → toxicity | Monitor levels; avoid grapefruit juice |
| CYP3A4 inducers | Rifampicin, phenytoin, carbamazepine, **St John's Wort** | Reduced tacrolimus levels → loss of efficacy | Avoid; monitor levels |
| Nephrotoxic drugs | Aminoglycosides, NSAIDs, amphotericin B | Additive nephrotoxicity | Monitor renal function |
| QT-prolonging drugs | Amiodarone, certain antibiotics (clarithromycin, azithromycin), antipsychotics | Additive QT prolongation | Check ECG; avoid combinations |
| Ciclosporin | Additive nephrotoxicity and calcineurin inhibition | Do not use simultaneously |
| Potassium supplements / ACE inhibitors | Additive hyperkalaemia | Monitor electrolytes |

#### Monitoring Requirements
- **Therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM):** Trough levels 5–10 ng/mL (MG; lower than transplant targets)
- Renal function (creatinine, eGFR): regular monitoring
- Blood pressure, blood glucose (HbA1c)
- Electrolytes (potassium, magnesium)
- ECG at baseline and if doses change
- LFTs

#### Special Populations

**Pregnancy:** Category C. Associated with neonatal hyperkalaemia, renal dysfunction, and premature birth. Not absolutely contraindicated but use with caution; some centres use when benefit outweighs risk. Monitoring of maternal tacrolimus levels required (pharmacokinetics change significantly in pregnancy).

**Breastfeeding:** Present in breast milk; generally avoided.

**Elderly:** Use lower doses; increased risk of nephrotoxicity and diabetes.

**Renal impairment:** Significant caution required; nephrotoxic. Monitor levels closely.

**Hepatic impairment:** Dose reduction needed in severe hepatic disease; extensively hepatically metabolised.

### 1.7 Methotrexate

**Drug Class:** Antimetabolite (folate antagonist immunosuppressant)
**Status:** Third-line steroid-sparing agent; less commonly used in MG than azathioprine or MMF; some evidence base exists

#### Mechanism of Action
Methotrexate inhibits dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR), blocking the conversion of dihydrofolate to tetrahydrofolate, which is essential for purine and thymidine synthesis and therefore DNA replication. At low (immunosuppressive) doses, it also has anti-inflammatory effects through adenosine pathway modulation. In MG, it suppresses both T and B cell proliferation.

Onset of benefit: **3–6 months**. Typically used when azathioprine or MMF are not tolerated.

#### Side Effects

**Common:**
- Nausea and vomiting (reduced by once-weekly oral/subcutaneous dosing and folic acid supplementation)
- Mucositis / mouth ulcers
- Fatigue
- Headache
- Hair thinning (alopecia)
- Elevated liver enzymes

**Serious:**
- **Hepatotoxicity**: dose-dependent; can progress to hepatic fibrosis or cirrhosis with cumulative high doses. Liver biopsy may be required after high cumulative doses.
- **Myelosuppression**: pancytopenia (especially with folic acid deficiency, renal impairment, or drug interactions)
- **Pulmonary toxicity** (methotrexate pneumonitis): rare but serious; can occur at any dose; presents with cough, dyspnoea, fever
- Severe mucocutaneous reactions (Stevens-Johnson syndrome — rare)

**Long-term / Rare:**
- Lymphoma
- Accelerated nodulosis

#### Contraindications

**Absolute:**
- **Pregnancy — ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATION.** Highly teratogenic (category X). Causes miscarriage and severe fetal malformations (methotrexate embryopathy: limb abnormalities, craniofacial defects, neural tube defects). Must be stopped **at least 3 months before conception** in women and **at least 3 months** in men (some guidelines recommend 6 months for men).
- Breastfeeding
- Significant hepatic disease / cirrhosis
- Significant renal impairment (creatinine clearance <10 mL/min; methotrexate is renally excreted)
- Active severe infection

**Relative:**
- Alcohol use (additive hepatotoxicity)
- Pleural effusions or ascites (methotrexate accumulates in "third spaces")
- Obesity or pre-existing liver disease

#### Drug Interactions

| Interacting Drug | Effect | Clinical Action |
|---|---|---|
| NSAIDs (ibuprofen, diclofenac, naproxen) | Reduce renal excretion of methotrexate → toxicity | **Avoid NSAIDs** with methotrexate (major interaction) |
| Trimethoprim (including co-trimoxazole) | Additive antifolate effect → severe bone marrow suppression | **Avoid combination** |
| Proton pump inhibitors | May reduce renal tubular secretion of methotrexate → elevated levels | Use with caution; monitor |
| Penicillins | Reduce renal excretion | Monitor |
| Live vaccines | Contraindicated | Avoid |
| Alcohol | Additive hepatotoxicity | Patient should be counselled to avoid alcohol |
| Folic acid supplements | **Protective** — reduces side effects without reducing efficacy | Folic acid 5 mg once weekly (not on the same day as methotrexate) is standard |

#### Monitoring Requirements
- Pre-treatment: FBC, LFTs, renal function, CXR, hepatitis B/C serology
- FBC and LFTs: weekly until dose stable (4–6 weeks), then every 1–3 months
- **Folic acid 5 mg once weekly is mandatory** (taken on a different day from methotrexate to reduce side effects)
- Cumulative dose monitoring: consider liver biopsy after cumulative dose of 1.5–2 g (or earlier if LFTs persistently elevated)
- Pulmonary function / CXR if respiratory symptoms develop

#### Special Populations

**Pregnancy:** ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATION. Both women AND men must stop well before conception. See note above.

**Breastfeeding:** CONTRAINDICATED.

**Elderly:** High-risk population; renal clearance declines with age, increasing toxicity risk. Start at low dose; monitor renal function before each dose.

**Renal impairment:** Dangerous — methotrexate is renally excreted. Contraindicated in severe impairment. Dose reduce in moderate impairment; monitor closely.

**Hepatic impairment:** Contraindicated in significant hepatic disease.

### 1.8 Rituximab (MabThera / Rituxan)

**Drug Class:** Anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody (B-cell depleting agent)
**Status:** Now established as first- or second-line for MuSK-positive MG by 2024–2025 international consensus; used for refractory AChR-positive MG

#### Mechanism of Action
Rituximab is a chimeric monoclonal antibody targeting CD20, a surface antigen expressed on B lymphocytes from the pre-B cell stage through mature B cells, but not on plasma cells or B-cell precursors. Binding of rituximab to CD20 leads to B-cell depletion through:
1. Complement-dependent cytotoxicity (CDC)
2. Antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC)
3. Direct induction of apoptosis

In MG, B-cell depletion reduces the production of pathogenic autoantibodies (anti-AChR and anti-MuSK). MuSK-positive MG responds particularly well, likely because MuSK IgG4 antibodies are produced by short-lived plasmablasts that are CD20+ and thus directly depleted. B-cell repopulation occurs at 6–12 months, and repeat infusions are given accordingly.

Onset of benefit: **3–6 months**, with some patients responding earlier.

#### Side Effects

**Infusion-related reactions (IRR) — most common overall:**
- Fever, chills, rigors during or shortly after infusion
- Flushing and urticaria
- Hypotension or hypertension
- Nausea
- Usually manageable with premedication (IV antihistamine, paracetamol, and corticosteroid) and slowing the infusion rate

**Serious:**
- **Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML)**: caused by JC virus reactivation; rare but potentially fatal or causing permanent neurological disability. Risk is very low with short-term MG dosing but increases with cumulative use and concurrent immunosuppression.
- Severe infusion reactions (bronchospasm, anaphylaxis)
- Severe infections (bacterial, viral, fungal) due to B-cell depletion and hypogammaglobulinaemia
- Hepatitis B reactivation (potentially fatal — always screen before starting)
- Cardiac arrhythmias during infusion (rare)
- Tumour lysis syndrome (rare in MG context but seen in haematological indications)

**Long-term:**
- Prolonged hypogammaglobulinaemia (reduced IgG levels) with multiple courses — may require IVIG supplementation
- Persistent B-cell depletion
- Increased risk of infection with prolonged B-cell depletion
- Late-onset neutropenia (rare)

#### Contraindications

**Absolute:**
- Active severe infection
- Hepatitis B surface antigen positivity without specialist management (risk of fatal reactivation)
- Hypersensitivity to rituximab or murine proteins

**Relative:**
- History of severe cardiac arrhythmias
- Pre-existing hypogammaglobulinaemia or immunodeficiency
- Active HIV infection

#### Drug Interactions
- **Live vaccines:** Contraindicated. Inactivated vaccines should be given at least 4 weeks before rituximab, as B-cell depletion impairs vaccine response for up to 6–12 months post-infusion.
- **Other immunosuppressants:** Additive immunosuppression; increased infection risk.
- **Natalizumab / other CNS-active agents:** Theoretical PML risk additive (particularly relevant if patients have other neurological conditions treated with biologics).
- **Cisplatin / nephrotoxic agents:** Additive renal toxicity in high-dose protocols.

#### Pre-Treatment Assessment
- Hepatitis B surface antigen, core antibody, and surface antibody (treat/prophylax HBsAg positive patients)
- HIV, hepatitis C serology
- Baseline immunoglobulin levels (IgG, IgA, IgM)
- FBC, renal function, LFTs
- Varicella zoster immunity
- All appropriate vaccinations should be administered at least 4 weeks before first infusion

#### Monitoring
- Infusion monitoring (vital signs, anaphylaxis readiness)
- Regular FBC
- Immunoglobulin levels every 3–6 months (risk of hypogammaglobulinaemia)
- B-cell count (CD19+/CD20+) to guide re-dosing

#### Clinical Evidence in MG

**BEAT-MG Trial:** Randomised controlled trial of rituximab vs. azathioprine in generalised MG (AChR+ predominantly). Results were mixed — did not meet primary endpoint (steroid reduction) but showed significant benefit in MuSK+ subgroup and in those with more severe disease. This supported rituximab's emerging role particularly in MuSK+ MG.

**2025 Meta-analysis and Consensus:** Low-dose protocols (375 mg/m² single dose or 500 mg fixed dose every 6 months) have shown equivalent efficacy to higher doses in many patients, reducing cumulative immunosuppression burden. The 2025 international consensus now supports **early rituximab use in MuSK+ MG** and in Late-Onset MG (LOMG), rather than reserving it solely for refractory disease.

#### Special Populations

**Pregnancy:** Category C. Not recommended during pregnancy. Limited data. If essential, some centres have used it in severe refractory MG with close monitoring; neonatal B-cell depletion reported.

**Breastfeeding:** Not recommended (data limited).

**Elderly:** Generally well-tolerated but increased infection risk due to age-related immune senescence combined with B-cell depletion. Monitor immunoglobulin levels closely.

### 1.9 Eculizumab (Soliris)

**Drug Class:** Terminal complement C5 inhibitor (monoclonal antibody)
**Status:** FDA-approved September 2017 for anti-AChR positive generalised MG; EMA-approved 2017. First complement inhibitor approved for MG.

#### Mechanism of Action
Eculizumab is a humanised monoclonal antibody that binds with high affinity to the complement protein C5, preventing its cleavage into C5a (anaphylatoxin) and C5b. C5b is the initiating component of the membrane attack complex (MAC, C5b-9). In AChR-positive MG, complement-mediated destruction of the NMJ is a major pathological mechanism — IgG1 and IgG3 anti-AChR antibodies activate complement on the postsynaptic membrane, leading to MAC formation and NMJ damage.

By blocking C5 cleavage, eculizumab prevents MAC formation at the NMJ, protecting postsynaptic membranes from complement-mediated destruction. It does not directly reduce autoantibody levels.

Only effective in **AChR-antibody positive** MG (complement pathway is not the primary mechanism in MuSK+ MG). Must be given as an IV infusion every 2 weeks (maintenance).

#### Side Effects

**Common:**
- Headache (very common, ~44%)
- Nasopharyngitis (upper respiratory infections)
- Nausea
- Fatigue
- Infusion-related reactions (generally mild)

**Serious — MENINGOCOCCAL INFECTION:**
- **The most critical safety concern.** Eculizumab blocks terminal complement, which is essential for killing encapsulated bacteria, particularly *Neisseria meningitidis*. Patients on eculizumab have a **1,000–2,000-fold increased risk of meningococcal disease**, which can be rapidly fatal.
- **All patients MUST receive meningococcal vaccination at least 2 weeks before starting** (both serogroups A, C, W135, Y and serogroup B vaccines, e.g. MenACWY and Bexsero/Trumenba)
- If treatment must start before vaccination can take effect: **prophylactic antibiotics** (penicillin V or ciprofloxacin) for at least 2 weeks after vaccination
- All patients carry a patient safety card and must present to emergency services immediately if they develop fever, headache, or neck stiffness

**Other serious:**
- Severe infections (encapsulated bacteria: *Haemophilus influenzae*, *Streptococcus pneumoniae*)
- Serious infusion reactions/anaphylaxis
- Haemolysis if drug is discontinued abruptly (in paroxysmal nocturnal haemoglobinuria patients)

#### Contraindications

**Absolute:**
- Unresolved meningococcal infection
- Active meningococcal disease
- Patients not vaccinated against meningococcus (unless started on antibiotic prophylaxis)
- Hypersensitivity to eculizumab

**Relative:**
- Systemic active infection
- History of complement deficiency (rare — these patients are already susceptible to encapsulated bacteria)

#### Drug Interactions
- **Live vaccines:** Avoid.
- No major CYP450 interactions (monoclonal antibody).
- Drug interactions are primarily pharmacodynamic (additive immunosuppression with other agents).

#### Monitoring Requirements
- Meningococcal vaccination status — MUST BE CONFIRMED before every course
- Prophylactic antibiotic prescription (patient education)
- Regular FBC, LFTs, renal function
- Complement levels (CH50, C3, C4) — to confirm pharmacological effect
- Infusion reactions monitoring

#### Clinical Evidence

**REGAIN Trial (Phase 3):** Eculizumab significantly improved MG-ADL scores (primary endpoint) versus placebo in AChR+ generalised MG. Mean improvement in MG-ADL: 4.2 points at 26 weeks. REGAIN Open-Label Extension showed sustained benefits over 3+ years. FDA and EMA approved on basis of REGAIN data.

#### Special Populations

**Pregnancy:** Category C. Limited data. Some case reports of use in pregnancy (primarily for PNH) with generally good outcomes. Complement system plays a role in placental development; blocking terminal complement has theoretical risks. Discuss risk-benefit carefully.

**Elderly:** Generally well-tolerated; meningococcal infection risk is equally elevated.

**Renal/hepatic impairment:** No dose adjustment required (monoclonal antibody; not renally or hepatically cleared in the conventional sense).

### 1.10 Ravulizumab (Ultomiris)

**Drug Class:** Terminal complement C5 inhibitor (next-generation monoclonal antibody)
**Status:** FDA-approved April 2022 for AChR+ generalised MG; EMA-approved 2022

#### Mechanism of Action
Ravulizumab has the same mechanism as eculizumab (binds C5, blocks cleavage, prevents MAC formation at the NMJ) but has been engineered for extended half-life through modifications to the antibody's FcRn binding domain, allowing for:
- **IV infusion every 8 weeks** (vs. every 2 weeks for eculizumab)
- More stable, sustained C5 inhibition between doses
- Potentially more consistent pharmacological effect

Specifically approved for **AChR+ generalised MG** only.

#### Side Effects
Similar profile to eculizumab:
- Headache, nasopharyngitis, upper respiratory infections
- Nausea, fatigue
- Infusion-related reactions
- **MENINGOCOCCAL INFECTION RISK: identical to eculizumab** — same mandatory vaccination and prophylaxis requirements apply (see eculizumab section)

The reduced dosing frequency (every 8 weeks vs. 2 weeks) significantly reduces treatment burden without compromising the safety profile.

#### Contraindications
Identical to eculizumab — vaccination requirement, active meningococcal infection.

#### Drug Interactions
Same as eculizumab — no major CYP450 interactions; pharmacodynamic additive immunosuppression.

#### Monitoring Requirements
Same as eculizumab, with meningococcal vaccination the paramount safety requirement.

#### Clinical Evidence

**CHAMPION-MG Trial (Phase 3):** Randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial of ravulizumab in AChR+ generalised MG. Primary endpoint (MG-ADL improvement at 26 weeks) was met: mean improvement of 3.1 points vs. 0.4 for placebo. QMG, MG-QOL-15r, and MGFA post-intervention status all improved significantly. Open-label extension and subsequent **real-world data (2024)** confirmed sustained benefit, standardising the shift to 8-week dosing intervals in clinical practice.

#### Comparison: Eculizumab vs. Ravulizumab

| Feature | Eculizumab (Soliris) | Ravulizumab (Ultomiris) |
|---|---|---|
| Mechanism | C5 inhibitor | C5 inhibitor (same) |
| Dosing frequency | Every 2 weeks | **Every 8 weeks** |
| Indication | AChR+ gMG | AChR+ gMG |
| Evidence base | REGAIN (2017) | CHAMPION-MG (2022) |
| Patient burden | Higher | **Lower** |
| Meningococcal risk | Yes — mandatory vaccination | Yes — identical requirement |

For most newly-diagnosed patients requiring C5 inhibition, ravulizumab is now preferred over eculizumab due to the reduced infusion burden.

### 1.11 Zilucoplan (Zilbrysq)

**Drug Class:** Subcutaneous C5 complement inhibitor (macrocyclic peptide)
**Status:** FDA-approved October 2023; EMA-approved 2024 for AChR+ generalised MG

#### Mechanism of Action
Zilucoplan is a macrocyclic peptide (not a monoclonal antibody) that binds to complement component C5, inhibiting its cleavage and preventing MAC formation — the same downstream mechanism as eculizumab and ravulizumab. Importantly, zilucoplan also binds to C5b, providing a **dual mechanism** that may offer additional inhibition.

Key distinguishing feature: **once-daily self-administered subcutaneous injection**, making it the first C5 inhibitor available as a self-injection for home use. This represents a significant advance in patient convenience and accessibility.

Approved specifically for **AChR+ generalised MG**.

#### Side Effects
- **Injection site reactions**: bruising, erythema, pain at injection site (most common overall complaint)
- Headache
- Upper respiratory tract infections
- Fatigue
- **MENINGOCOCCAL INFECTION RISK: identical to IV C5 inhibitors** — all patients must be vaccinated and educated about the risk. Same warnings apply.
- Infusion reactions do not apply (subcutaneous route)

#### Contraindications
- Unvaccinated against meningococcus (unless antibiotic prophylaxis started)
- Active meningococcal infection
- Hypersensitivity to zilucoplan

#### Drug Interactions
- No major CYP450 interactions (peptide; not hepatically metabolised via cytochrome system)
- Live vaccines: avoid
- Additive immunosuppression with other immunosuppressants

#### Monitoring
- Meningococcal vaccination (MANDATORY — MenACWY + serogroup B)
- Regular safety assessments

#### Clinical Evidence

**RAISE Trial (Phase 3):** Significant improvement in MG-ADL (-4.39 points vs. -2.30 for placebo at 12 weeks). Fast onset of effect. FDA approved on basis of RAISE data.

**RAISE-XT (Long-term extension, 2025):** Confirmed sustained safety and efficacy over 2+ years of continuous daily self-injection. No new safety signals emerged; injection site tolerability remained acceptable long-term.

#### Patient Considerations
The subcutaneous self-administration route is particularly valuable for:
- Patients in geographically remote areas
- Those with needle phobia (requires training but manageable for most)
- Patients who prefer home-based management
- Those for whom regular hospital visits for infusions are burdensome

### 1.12 Efgartigimod (Vyvgart / Vyvgart Hytrulo)

**Drug Class:** FcRn (neonatal Fc receptor) antagonist — IgG recycling blocker
**Status:** FDA-approved December 2021 (IV); FDA-approved June 2023 (subcutaneous formulation Vyvgart Hytrulo); EMA-approved 2022. Approved for **all generalised MG** (AChR+ and MuSK+).

#### Mechanism of Action
Efgartigimod is a human IgG1-derived Fc fragment that acts as a competitive antagonist of the neonatal Fc receptor (FcRn). Normally, FcRn is expressed on endothelial cells and monocytes, where it "rescues" IgG antibodies from lysosomal degradation, recycling them back into the circulation and maintaining long half-lives of IgG. By saturating and blocking FcRn, efgartigimod prevents this recycling, leading to accelerated lysosomal catabolism of all IgG subclasses, including the pathogenic anti-AChR and anti-MuSK autoantibodies.

This mechanism is antibody-class-specific (affects all IgG), making efgartigimod effective for **both AChR+ and MuSK+ MG**, and does not require the autoantibody subtype to be defined for clinical use.

**Dosing pattern:** Administered as a treatment cycle — typically 4 weekly infusions (IV) or injections (SC), then reassessed. Repeat cycles given as needed based on clinical response. This intermittent cycle approach is distinct from continuous maintenance dosing of complement inhibitors.

#### Side Effects

**Common:**
- Headache (most common)
- Nasopharyngitis and upper respiratory infections (due to reduced IgG levels)
- Nausea
- Fatigue
- Urinary tract infections
- Injection site reactions (SC formulation)

**Serious:**
- **Hypogammaglobulinaemia**: Total IgG levels fall substantially (by ~70% of baseline in some studies). Risk of serious bacterial infections. Monitor for serious or opportunistic infections.
- Severe infections requiring hospitalisation
- Theoretical increased risk of infections with pre-existing hypogammaglobulinaemia

**Not associated with:**
- Meningococcal risk (unlike complement inhibitors)
- Infusion reactions as severe as those seen with biologics
- The myelosuppression seen with conventional immunosuppressants

#### Contraindications

**Absolute:**
- Hypersensitivity to efgartigimod
- Active severe infection

**Relative:**
- Pre-existing severe hypogammaglobulinaemia (IgG deficiency)
- History of recurrent serious infections

#### Drug Interactions
- **Other IgG-lowering therapies** (e.g., IVIG or other FcRn inhibitors): additive reduction in IgG levels
- **Vaccines:** Antibody responses to vaccination may be impaired while IgG is reduced; plan vaccinations carefully
- No significant CYP450 interactions

#### Monitoring
- Baseline immunoglobulin levels (IgG, IgA, IgM)
- IgG levels during treatment cycles (target: not below threshold for clinical infection risk)
- MG-ADL and clinical response assessment between cycles to determine re-treatment timing
- Screen for infections before each cycle

#### Clinical Evidence

**ADAPT Trial (Phase 3, published 2021):** Primary endpoint met — significantly more patients achieved clinically meaningful improvement in MG-ADL at cycle 1, week 4 (67.7% vs. 29.7% placebo). Improvements were rapid (within days to 1–2 weeks). Both AChR+ and MuSK+ patients responded.

**2024–2025 Real-World Data:** Confirmed that efgartigimod reduces hospitalisations and emergency presentations in generalised MG. CIDP (chronic inflammatory demyelinating polyneuropathy) indication approved 2023, broadening the FcRn inhibitor evidence base.

#### Formulations

| Formulation | Name | Administration | Dosing |
|---|---|---|---|
| IV infusion | Vyvgart | 10 mg/kg IV weekly × 4 | Cycles every 3–4 months or PRN |
| Subcutaneous injection | Vyvgart Hytrulo | Fixed dose SC weekly × 4 | More convenient; co-formulated with hyaluronidase |

### 1.13 Rozanolixizumab (Rystiggo)

**Drug Class:** FcRn antagonist (anti-FcRn monoclonal antibody)
**Status:** FDA-approved June 2023; EMA-approved 2023; 2025 EU approval for home self-administration. Approved for **both AChR+ and MuSK+ generalised MG** (first targeted therapy approved across both subtypes).

#### Mechanism of Action
Rozanolixizumab is a humanised IgG4 monoclonal antibody that targets the FcRn receptor itself (rather than binding to IgG as efgartigimod does). By blocking the FcRn receptor on endothelial cells, it prevents FcRn from rescuing circulating IgG from degradation, leading to accelerated catabolism of all IgG subclasses, including pathogenic anti-AChR and anti-MuSK autoantibodies.

Administered as a **subcutaneous infusion once weekly** for 6 weeks per treatment cycle, with retreatment cycles given as needed.

#### Side Effects

**Common:**
- **Headache** (the most prominent side effect; often moderate-severe; may be a dose-limiting issue for some patients; usually manageable with analgesia)
- Pyrexia (fever)
- Nausea and vomiting
- Diarrhoea
- Fatigue
- Injection site reactions
- Nasopharyngitis / upper respiratory infections

**Serious:**
- Hypogammaglobulinaemia and increased infection risk (IgG levels fall by ~50–70% during treatment cycle)
- Serious infections
- Severe headaches (aseptic meningitis-like syndrome reported in some cases — distinct from typical drug headache)

**Notable:** Rozanolixizumab is particularly effective for **ocular and bulbar symptoms** (diplopia, ptosis, dysphagia, dysarthria), which are often the most functionally limiting symptoms. This has been a specific clinical observation from post-marketing data and the MycarinG trial.

#### Contraindications

**Absolute:**
- Hypersensitivity to rozanolixizumab or any component
- Active severe infection

**Relative:**
- Pre-existing hypogammaglobulinaemia

#### Drug Interactions
- Same class as efgartigimod; similar interaction profile
- No CYP450 interactions of note
- Vaccines: reduced immunogenicity when given during treatment cycle

#### Clinical Evidence

**MycarinG Trial (Phase 3):** Primary endpoint (MG-ADL change at week 24) met — significant improvement versus placebo in both AChR+ and MuSK+ patients. The inclusion of MuSK+ patients (who have limited other approved targeted options) was particularly significant. Rapid onset within the first 1–2 weeks of the cycle.

**2025 EU Self-Administration Approval:** The European Medicines Agency approved home self-administration of rozanolixizumab in early 2025, allowing patients to receive treatment without regular hospital visits — a significant quality-of-life benefit.

### 1.14 Nipocalimab (Imaavy)

**Drug Class:** FcRn antagonist (anti-FcRn monoclonal antibody)
**Status:** FDA-approved April 2025 for generalised MG (adults and adolescents aged 12+). First FcRn inhibitor approved for adolescents with MG.

#### Mechanism of Action
Nipocalimab is a fully human, aglycosylated IgG1 monoclonal antibody targeting the FcRn receptor. By blocking FcRn, it accelerates the degradation of all circulating IgG, including pathogenic autoantibodies. It is engineered to be aglycosylated (lacking sugar residues on the Fc domain), which eliminates the capacity for complement activation and Fc receptor binding, theoretically reducing off-target immune effects.

Administered as an **IV infusion**, with a loading dose followed by maintenance infusions.

#### Side Effects
- Headache
- Nasopharyngitis / respiratory infections
- Nausea
- Infusion-related reactions (generally mild)
- Hypogammaglobulinaemia and associated infection risk (all FcRn inhibitors carry this risk)
- Fatigue

#### Clinical Evidence

**Vivacity-MG3 Trial (Phase 3):** Demonstrated sustained disease control over 24 weeks. Clinically meaningful improvement in MG-ADL scores (4.7-point improvement vs. placebo). Both AChR+ and MuSK+ patients showed benefit. The adolescent inclusion (aged 12+) was a landmark — no other FcRn inhibitor had been approved for paediatric/adolescent MG.

#### Special Populations
- **Adolescents (12+):** Approved use — first targeted MG therapy with paediatric approval.
- **Pregnancy:** Data limited; not recommended during pregnancy unless benefit clearly outweighs risk.

### 1.15 Inebilizumab (Uplizna)

**Drug Class:** Anti-CD19 monoclonal antibody (broad B-cell depleting agent)
**Status:** FDA-approved December 2025 for generalised MG. First B-cell depleting agent with formal FDA approval specifically for MG.

#### Mechanism of Action
Inebilizumab is a humanised, afucosylated IgG1 monoclonal antibody targeting CD19, a B-cell surface antigen expressed across a broader range of B-cell lineage cells than CD20. Key differences from rituximab (anti-CD20):
- **Broader B-cell depletion**: Targets not only mature B cells (CD20+) but also **plasmablasts** and some **plasma cells** that express CD19 but have downregulated or lost CD20 expression
- **Afucosylated** Fc region enhances ADCC (antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity), improving B-cell killing
- Given as an **IV infusion twice yearly** (every 6 months after loading doses) — significantly less frequent than rituximab

In MG, the broader depletion of plasmablasts (short-lived antibody-producing cells that are important in MuSK+ and AChR+ MG) may offer advantages over CD20-targeted therapies.

#### Side Effects
- **Infusion reactions** (most common during loading doses)
- Increased infection susceptibility (B-cell depletion)
- Urinary tract infections, nasopharyngitis
- Hypogammaglobulinaemia with prolonged use
- Arthralgia

**Serious:**
- Serious infections (including opportunistic)
- Hepatitis B reactivation (screen before starting)
- PML (theoretical risk with prolonged B-cell depletion, as with all B-cell depleting agents)

#### Contraindications
- Active severe infection
- Hepatitis B surface antigen positivity without specialist management
- Hypersensitivity to inebilizumab

#### Clinical Evidence

**MINT Trial (Phase 3, 2024/2025):** Practice-changing results. Inebilizumab demonstrated effective generalised MG control with twice-yearly dosing. Critically, the MINT trial showed that patients could significantly **taper and reduce corticosteroid doses** — demonstrating steroid-sparing effects that are particularly valuable for long-term management. Approved by FDA in December 2025.

#### Key Advantage
The **biannual dosing schedule** (compared to monthly/quarterly options for many other biologics) represents a significant reduction in treatment burden for patients and healthcare systems.

### 1.16 IVIG (Intravenous Immunoglobulin)

**Drug Class:** Biological product (pooled human IgG)
**Role:** Acute rescue treatment; bridge therapy; pre-surgical preparation

#### Mechanism of Action
IVIG is a preparation of pooled, purified IgG antibodies derived from thousands of human plasma donors. In MG, it acts through multiple mechanisms:
1. **FcRn saturation:** The large dose of exogenous IgG saturates FcRn receptors, reducing the recycling of the patient's own pathogenic autoantibodies (accelerating their degradation) — the same mechanism exploited by FcRn inhibitors
2. **Anti-idiotypic antibodies:** The pooled IgG contains antibodies that bind to and neutralise the patient's anti-AChR/MuSK autoantibodies
3. **Complement neutralisation and Fc receptor blockade:** Modulates complement-mediated damage
4. **Cytokine modulation:** Broad anti-inflammatory effects

Onset: **3–5 days**. Duration: 4–8 weeks. Does not provide long-term disease modification — effects are temporary.

#### Side Effects

**Common:**
- Headache (often prominent; can be severe)
- Flushing and fever during infusion
- Flu-like symptoms (myalgia, fatigue)
- Nausea
- Hypertension during infusion

**Serious:**
- **Thromboembolic events**: deep vein thrombosis (DVT), pulmonary embolism, stroke, myocardial infarction. Risk is highest with:
  - High infusion rate (must be given slowly)
  - Pre-existing cardiovascular disease or prior thrombosis
  - Hypercoagulable states
  - Older patients
  - High-viscosity products
- **Acute renal failure**: particularly with sucrose-containing IVIG products in patients with pre-existing renal impairment
- **Aseptic meningitis**: severe headache, fever, neck stiffness (typically 12–24 hours post-infusion; self-limiting; treatable with analgesia)
- **Haemolytic anaemia**: due to blood group antibodies in the pooled IgG
- **Transfusion-related acute lung injury (TRALI)**: rare
- **Anaphylaxis in IgA-deficient patients**: IgA-deficient patients who have anti-IgA antibodies can develop anaphylaxis. Screening for IgA deficiency before IVIG is important.

**Rare:**
- Thrombotic microangiopathy
- Pompholyx (blistering eczema-like reaction on hands and feet)

#### Contraindications

**Absolute:**
- **IgA deficiency with anti-IgA antibodies** — risk of anaphylaxis (use IgA-depleted IVIG preparation if absolutely needed)
- Hypersensitivity to human immunoglobulin products

**Relative:**
- Pre-existing renal failure (acute or chronic)
- High cardiovascular risk (prior stroke, MI, DVT/PE, known hypercoagulable state)
- Severe heart failure (volume overload risk)
- Thrombocytopenia
- Severe hyperviscosity syndromes

#### Drug Interactions
- **Plasmapheresis after IVIG:** PLEX removes the just-infused exogenous IgG, wasting the treatment. Do NOT perform PLEX within 2–4 weeks of IVIG.
- **Live vaccines:** Reduced vaccine immunogenicity for up to 3–11 months after IVIG (depending on dose and indication). Check specific vaccine guidance.

#### Clinical Use in MG
- **Acute myasthenic crisis:** First-line alongside PLEX (PLEX preferred if faster response needed or MuSK+ MG)
- **Bridge therapy:** Started 2–3 weeks before initiating immunosuppression (while waiting for slow-onset agents), or before thymectomy, or during pregnancy exacerbations
- **Pre-surgical preparation:** Used to stabilise MG before major procedures
- **Maintenance top-up:** Some refractory patients receive regular IVIG cycles as maintenance

**Dose:** Standard MG dose is 2 g/kg total, given over 2–5 days (e.g., 0.4 g/kg/day × 5 days, or 1 g/kg/day × 2 days). Infuse slowly, especially in elderly or cardiovascular risk patients.

**MuSK+ MG and IVIG:** IVIG is generally less effective in MuSK-antibody positive MG compared to AChR+ MG. Plasmapheresis is preferred for MuSK+ patients.

#### Special Populations

**Pregnancy:** Safe and preferred for acute MG exacerbations during pregnancy.

**Elderly:** Use with extra caution regarding infusion rate (thromboembolism risk), renal function, and fluid balance.

**Renal impairment:** Avoid sucrose-containing products; infuse slowly; monitor renal function.

### 1.17 Plasmapheresis (Plasma Exchange / PLEX)

**Role:** Acute rescue treatment; fastest-acting intervention for MG crisis
**Classification:** Therapeutic procedure, not a drug

#### Mechanism
Plasmapheresis physically removes the patient's plasma (which contains pathogenic autoantibodies, complement components, and inflammatory mediators) and replaces it with albumin solution or fresh frozen plasma. Direct antibody removal is immediate and effective, making it the fastest-acting treatment available for acute MG.

Each session removes approximately 1–1.5 plasma volumes. Standard course: **5 exchanges over 10–14 days** (typically every other day). Effects last 4–8 weeks (until antibody levels rebound).

#### Advantages over IVIG in MG:
- Faster onset: **1–3 days** (vs. 3–5 days for IVIG)
- Highly effective in **MuSK-antibody positive** MG (IVIG is less effective in MuSK+ disease)
- Direct antibody removal (quantifiable IgG reduction)
- Preferred for **myasthenic crisis with respiratory failure**

#### Complications and Side Effects

**During the procedure:**
- **Hypocalcaemia**: The citrate anticoagulant used in the circuit chelates calcium. Can cause perioral tingling, muscle cramps, and tetany. Treated with IV calcium gluconate replacement.
- Hypotension (volume shifts)
- Hypothermia (cold replacement fluids)
- Bradycardia
- Allergic reactions to replacement fluids (FFP > albumin)

**Access-related (central venous catheter):**
- **Infection and sepsis** (catheter-related bloodstream infection)
- Pneumothorax (subclavian/internal jugular access)
- Haemothorax
- Deep vein thrombosis

**Other:**
- **Coagulopathy:** PLEX removes clotting factors along with antibodies. Avoid invasive procedures immediately after PLEX until coagulation normalises.
- Anaemia (removal of red cells during machine processing)
- Hypogammaglobulinaemia

#### Contraindications

**Absolute:**
- Severe haemodynamic instability
- Active systemic sepsis (relative contraindication — may worsen sepsis if circuit is infected)
- Allergy to replacement solution

**Relative:**
- Poor venous access (peripheral access insufficient — central line required)
- Severe coagulopathy or thrombocytopenia
- Active bleeding
- Pregnancy (relative — risk-benefit must be assessed; PLEX has been used in severe MG during pregnancy when necessary)

#### Drug Interactions with PLEX
- PLEX removes plasma drug levels: oral immunosuppressants (particularly those with enterohepatic circulation) should be checked after PLEX courses
- Importantly: **oral pyridostigmine levels may drop** after PLEX; dose may need adjustment
- **Do not give IVIG immediately after PLEX** — the infused IVIG will simply be removed in the next session

#### Comparison: IVIG vs. Plasmapheresis

| Feature | IVIG | Plasmapheresis |
|---|---|---|
| Mechanism | Immunomodulation / FcRn saturation | Direct antibody removal |
| Speed of onset | 3–5 days | **1–3 days (faster)** |
| AChR+ MG efficacy | High | High |
| MuSK+ MG efficacy | Lower | **Higher (preferred)** |
| Route | Peripheral IV | Central venous catheter |
| Course | 2–5 days | 5 sessions over 10–14 days |
| Main serious risks | Thrombosis, renal failure, anaphylaxis (IgA deficient) | Sepsis, hypocalcaemia, coagulopathy |
| Preferred for: | Moderate exacerbations, bridge therapy | **Crisis + respiratory failure, MuSK+ MG** |
| Outpatient possible? | Yes | Generally requires hospital |

## 2. Combination Therapy Considerations

### 2.1 Standard Treatment Framework

MG treatment is layered and combinatorial. Most patients require multiple agents simultaneously:

**Layer 1 — Symptomatic control:**
Pyridostigmine for NMJ compensation. Does not modify disease.

**Layer 2 — Rapid immunosuppression:**
Corticosteroids (prednisolone) for rapid onset immune suppression. Used short-to-medium term, with a goal to taper once a steroid-sparing agent takes effect.

**Layer 3 — Steroid-sparing maintenance:**
Azathioprine, mycophenolate, tacrolimus, ciclosporin, or methotrexate. All are slow-onset (3–18 months). Allows steroid tapering and long-term disease modification.

**Layer 4 — Biologics (when needed):**
Rituximab, complement inhibitors, FcRn inhibitors, B-cell depleting agents — for refractory disease, specific subtypes (MuSK+), or where conventional immunosuppressants are inadequate.

**Bridge / rescue:**
IVIG or plasmapheresis used temporarily during flares, crises, pre-surgery, or while waiting for layer 3/4 agents to take effect.

### 2.2 Commonly Used Combinations

**The classic triad:**
- Pyridostigmine + Prednisolone + Azathioprine (most common combination worldwide)

**MuSK+ MG preferred approach (2025 consensus):**
- Low-dose pyridostigmine (often less effective but partially helpful) + Prednisolone + Rituximab + Mycophenolate

**Refractory AChR+ MG:**
- Steroid + Azathioprine/MMF + C5 inhibitor (eculizumab/ravulizumab/zilucoplan) OR FcRn inhibitor (efgartigimod/rozanolixizumab)

**Bridge therapy (crisis / pre-op / flare):**
- IVIG or PLEX + continuing background immunosuppression

**Late-Onset MG (LOMG) approach:**
- Lower thresholds to start biologics; rituximab increasingly used early. Steroid-sparing strategy even more important due to comorbidities in elderly patients.

### 2.3 Critical Drug Interaction: Azathioprine + Allopurinol

This is the most dangerous drug interaction in MG pharmacology and deserves special emphasis.

**What happens:**
- Azathioprine → 6-mercaptopurine (6-MP) → metabolised by xanthine oxidase (XO) → inactive metabolites
- Allopurinol (gout treatment) inhibits xanthine oxidase
- Result: 6-MP accumulates to toxic levels; fatal bone marrow suppression

**Clinical scenario:** An MG patient on azathioprine develops gout. Their GP or rheumatologist prescribes allopurinol. Unless the interaction is known and acted upon immediately, severe leukopenia can develop within weeks.

**Management:**
1. **Avoid the combination** whenever possible. Use febuxostat or other gout management strategies (but note febuxostat ALSO inhibits XO — same interaction)
2. If combination is unavoidable: reduce azathioprine dose by **75%** and monitor FBC weekly
3. All patients on azathioprine should carry a medication card documenting this interaction

### 2.4 Calcineurin Inhibitor Interactions (CYP3A4)

Both ciclosporin and tacrolimus are sensitive to CYP3A4 interactions:

**Drugs that INCREASE levels (toxicity risk):**
- Azole antifungals: fluconazole, itraconazole, ketoconazole, voriconazole
- Macrolide antibiotics: erythromycin, clarithromycin (but NOT azithromycin with ciclosporin — azithromycin is safe)
- Calcium channel blockers: diltiazem, verapamil
- Amiodarone
- Grapefruit and pomelo juice

**Drugs that DECREASE levels (loss of efficacy — MG relapse risk):**
- Rifampicin (rifampin): reduces levels dramatically
- Phenytoin, carbamazepine, phenobarbitone
- **St John's Wort** (hypericum): patients must be warned to avoid herbal supplements
- Nafcillin

**Clinical implication:** Any time a patient on ciclosporin or tacrolimus is prescribed a new medication (or changes a supplement), a drug interaction check is essential.

### 2.5 Monitoring When Combining Drugs

| Drug Combination | Monitoring Priority | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Prednisolone + Azathioprine | FBC, LFTs, BP, blood glucose, bone density | FBC/LFTs: 3-monthly; DEXA: annually |
| Prednisolone + MMF | FBC, LFTs, pregnancy test (if applicable) | FBC/LFTs: 3-monthly |
| Prednisolone + Tacrolimus | TDM (trough 5–10 ng/mL), renal function, BP, HbA1c | TDM: monthly until stable, then 3-monthly |
| Prednisolone + Ciclosporin | TDM (trough 100–200 ng/mL), creatinine, BP, lipids | As above |
| Azathioprine + any new drug | Check interaction (especially allopurinol risk) | With any new prescription |
| Complement inhibitor + any agent | Meningococcal vaccination status; annual booster | Before each treatment cycle |
| FcRn inhibitor + any agent | IgG levels; infection screen | Before each cycle |
| Rituximab + MMF | IgG levels, infection screen, hepatitis B status | 3–6 monthly |

### 2.6 Sequencing: IVIG and Plasmapheresis

- **Never give IVIG immediately before PLEX** — the PLEX will remove the infused IVIG, wasting the therapy
- **PLEX before IVIG** is acceptable if rapid antibody removal is needed first
- **Rituximab timing with PLEX:** If PLEX is needed urgently, rituximab should be re-dosed after the PLEX course, as PLEX will remove rituximab from the circulation

### 2.7 Vaccinations and Immunosuppression

A key consideration when managing combinations:
- **Live vaccines are contraindicated** in patients on any immunosuppressive therapy (including low-dose steroids, azathioprine, MMF, calcineurin inhibitors, rituximab, complement inhibitors)
- **Inactivated vaccines** (including annual influenza, COVID-19, pneumococcal, meningococcal, and shingles zoster vaccine Shingrix) are safe and strongly recommended
- **Meningococcal vaccination** is MANDATORY for all complement inhibitor patients (eculizumab, ravulizumab, zilucoplan)
- **Annual influenza vaccination** is recommended for all immunosuppressed MG patients
- **Hepatitis B vaccination** should be offered if not immune before starting rituximab or other B-cell depleting agents

## 3. Drug Research and Clinical Trials

### 3.1 Completed Trials (2020–2025) That Changed Clinical Practice

#### Complement Inhibitors

**RAISE Trial — Zilucoplan (2023)**
- Phase 3, randomised, placebo-controlled
- Result: MG-ADL improvement of -4.39 points vs. -2.30 for placebo at 12 weeks
- Impact: Led to FDA approval (October 2023); introduced self-administered subcutaneous C5 inhibition to clinical practice; gave patients an alternative to IV infusions for complement inhibition

**RAISE-XT (2025)**
- Long-term extension of RAISE
- Result: Confirmed sustained safety and efficacy of daily zilucoplan SC injection over 2+ years; no new safety signals
- Impact: Reinforced long-term use confidence; established a new standard for SC delivery of complement inhibitors

**CHAMPION-MG — Ravulizumab (2022)**
- Phase 3, randomised, placebo-controlled
- Result: MG-ADL improved by 3.1 points vs. 0.4 for placebo at 26 weeks; QMG, QoL measures also met
- Impact: Established 8-weekly IV dosing as practical; reduced treatment burden vs. eculizumab; FDA approved April 2022. Real-world data (2024) standardised the shift to ravulizumab as preferred C5 inhibitor.

#### FcRn Inhibitors

**ADAPT Trial — Efgartigimod (2021)**
- Phase 3, randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled
- Result: 67.7% of AChR+ patients achieved clinically meaningful MG-ADL improvement vs. 29.7% placebo; MuSK+ patients also responded
- Impact: First FcRn inhibitor approved for MG (December 2021); opened FcRn pathway to MG therapeutics; cycle-based treatment model introduced

**MycarinG Trial — Rozanolixizumab (2023)**
- Phase 3 trial in AChR+ and MuSK+ patients
- Result: Significant MG-ADL improvement in both subtypes; rapid onset
- Impact: June 2023 FDA approval — the first targeted therapy approved for both AChR+ AND MuSK+ MG; expanded treatment options for MuSK+ patients specifically

**Vivacity-MG3 — Nipocalimab (2025)**
- Phase 3 trial
- Result: 4.7-point MG-ADL improvement; sustained disease control over 24 weeks; efficacy in both subtypes; approved for adolescents (12+) as well as adults
- Impact: April 2025 FDA approval; first targeted MG therapy approved for adolescents

#### B-Cell Depleting Agents

**MINT Trial — Inebilizumab (2024/2025)**
- Phase 3 trial with twice-yearly dosing
- Result: Effective generalised MG control; demonstrated significant corticosteroid tapering was achievable; steroid-sparing effects were a key finding
- Impact: December 2025 FDA approval; twice-yearly dosing reduces treatment burden; ultra-low steroid protocols shown achievable; first anti-CD19 agent approved for MG

**BEAT-MG — Rituximab (2020)**
- Phase 2b randomised trial vs. azathioprine
- Result: Did not meet primary endpoint (steroid reduction at 12 months) in the overall population; however, significant benefit in MuSK+ subgroup
- Impact: Despite "negative" primary endpoint, established rituximab as preferred agent for MuSK+ MG; influenced updated international consensus for early rituximab use in MuSK+ disease

#### Clinical Practice Summary (2023–2025)

| Year | Trial / Event | Clinical Practice Change |
|---|---|---|
| 2021 | ADAPT (Efgartigimod) | First FcRn inhibitor for MG approved; biologic rescue strategy formalised |
| 2022 | CHAMPION-MG (Ravulizumab) | 8-weekly C5 inhibition becomes clinical standard; reduces infusion burden |
| 2023 | RAISE (Zilucoplan) | Self-administered SC complement inhibition available; home-based C5 treatment possible |
| 2023 | MycarinG (Rozanolixizumab) | First therapy approved for both AChR+ and MuSK+; FcRn inhibition for MuSK MG established |
| 2025 | Vivacity-MG3 (Nipocalimab) | First approved therapy for adolescent MG; extends paediatric treatment toolkit |

| 2025 | ABN Guidelines Update | Daily steroid dosing replaces alternate-day; lower threshold for early biologics |

### 3.2 Active and Ongoing Trials (2025–2026)

#### CAR-T Cell Therapies — The Emerging Frontier

CAR-T (chimeric antigen receptor T-cell) therapies represent the most radical and potentially transformative treatment approach in MG — aiming for long-term, drug-free remission or even a "cure" by resetting the immune system.

**Descartes-08 (BCMA-targeted mRNA CAR-T) — Cabaletta Bio**
- *Mechanism:* Uses mRNA technology (similar to mRNA vaccines) to transiently engineer a patient's T-cells to target BCMA (B-cell maturation antigen), expressed on long-lived plasma cells. Key innovation: mRNA-based engineering means T-cell CAR expression is **transient** (days to weeks), avoiding permanent CAR-T persistence, and **no high-dose chemotherapy (lymphodepletion) is required** before treatment.
- *Phase 2b Results (2024/2025):* Mean MG-ADL reduction of 5.5 points at month 4; 83% of patients maintained clinically meaningful response at 12 months.
- *Phase 3 AURORA Trial:* Launched early 2025. Randomised, placebo-controlled evaluation of mRNA CAR-T in generalised MG. This is the first randomised CAR-T trial in MG.
- *Potential significance:* If successful, could offer periodic (quarterly or even less frequent) infusions instead of daily medications.

**KYV-101 (CD19-targeted conventional CAR-T) — Kyverna Therapeutics**
- *Mechanism:* Conventional lentiviral CAR-T product targeting CD19 (depletes B cells broadly; mechanism similar to inebilizumab but using T-cells instead of antibodies). Requires lymphodepletion conditioning.
- *KYSA-6 Interim Data (2025):* 100% response rate in first 6 evaluable patients; mean MG-ADL reduction of -8.0 points at 24 weeks; no grade 3+ cytokine release syndrome (CRS) or ICANS (neurotoxicity). Highly promising early results.
- *Current status:* Phase 1/2 KYSA-6 trial ongoing; Phase 2 expansion expected.

**CABA-201 (CD19-targeted CAR-T) — Cabaletta Bio**
- *Mechanism:* CD19-targeted CAR-T (different from Descartes-08 which targets BCMA); aims for B-cell immune reset
- *Current status:* Phase 1/2 trials ongoing; includes both AChR+ and MuSK+ patients
- *Goal:* Long-term sustained remission through complete B-cell depletion and immune reconstitution

#### Next-Generation FcRn Inhibitors

**Batoclimab (IMVT-1402 successor)**
- Batoclimab Phase 3 FLEX trial (2025) met primary endpoints, but development focus has shifted to **IMVT-1402**, the next-generation molecule engineered without the cholesterol-raising side effect that limited batoclimab's clinical acceptability.
- IMVT-1402 Phase 2/3 trials ongoing.

**Nipocalimab Adolescent Extension:** Phase 3 data in adolescents (12–17 years) — ongoing follow-up post-approval.

#### Other Active Areas

**Complement Pathway (Beyond C5):**
- Investigations into C3 inhibitors (which block complement upstream of C5, potentially offering broader complement inhibition)

**Tolerance Induction Approaches:**
- Research into antigen-specific tolerance: delivering AChR peptides to re-educate the immune system to stop attacking itself. Early phase research; no major trials in late-stage yet for MG.

**TACI-Ig (Telitacicept):**
- Dual inhibitor of BAFF and APRIL (B-cell survival factors)
- May offer B-cell depletion without the risks of anti-CD20 or anti-CD19 antibodies
- Phase 2 trials in MG ongoing (predominantly in China/Asia; data emerging)

**Low-dose IL-2:**
- Attempting to expand regulatory T-cells (Tregs) to re-establish immune self-tolerance in MG
- Phase 2 research ongoing

### 3.3 Upcoming / Pipeline Therapies

**Gene Therapy:**
- Early-stage research exploring direct genetic correction of the immune dysregulation underlying MG
- CRISPR-based approaches to modify autoreactive T-cell or B-cell clones
- No trials in humans for MG-specific gene therapy yet as of 2026; preclinical data emerging

**Plasma Cell Targeting:**
- Next-generation agents targeting long-lived plasma cells (which produce persistent autoantibodies) more effectively than current B-cell depleting agents
- Belantamab mafodotin (anti-BCMA antibody-drug conjugate) and similar agents being evaluated
- The success of BCMA-targeted CAR-T (Descartes-08) validates this mechanistic approach

**CAR-Treg Therapies:**
- Engineering regulatory T-cells with chimeric antigen receptors to specifically suppress AChR-reactive immune responses
- Very early-stage; preclinical work ongoing

**CGRP Inhibitors and NMJ Neuroprotection:**
- Research into drugs that promote NMJ repair and regeneration (rather than just suppressing the immune attack)
- Neurotrophic factor approaches; still preclinical

## 4. Pregnancy and MG

### 4.1 Overview

Managing MG during pregnancy requires a multidisciplinary approach (neurologist, obstetrician, anaesthetist, neonatologist) and careful medication review before conception if possible. The goal is to maintain "Minimal Manifestation Status" (MMS) or better throughout pregnancy, as maternal disease stability is the best predictor of good fetal outcomes.

MG does not affect the smooth muscle of the uterus, so the **first stage of labour** typically proceeds normally. The **second stage** (pushing, which relies on voluntary skeletal muscle effort) can be affected by MG weakness — instrumental delivery may be needed.

### 4.2 Drug Safety Summary in Pregnancy

| Drug | Safety in Pregnancy | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Pyridostigmine** | SAFE (oral) | Avoid IV in third trimester (may stimulate uterus) |
| **Prednisolone** | GENERALLY SAFE | Preferred immunosuppressant; slight cleft palate risk (first trimester); neonatal adrenal suppression at high doses near term |
| **Azathioprine** | RELATIVELY SAFE (if essential) | Category D; crosses placenta; some risk of neonatal bone marrow suppression; acceptable if MG cannot be controlled otherwise; continue if patient was stable on it pre-conception |
| **Ciclosporin** | USE WITH CAUTION | Category C; associated with intrauterine growth restriction; not teratogenic; specialist monitoring required |
| **Tacrolimus** | USE WITH CAUTION | Category C; neonatal hyperkalaemia, renal issues; monitor levels (pharmacokinetics change); some centres allow continuation |
| **Mycophenolate Mofetil** | **ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED** | Highly teratogenic; miscarriage, MMF embryopathy; stop 6 weeks before conception |
| **Methotrexate** | **ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED** | Highly teratogenic; stop 3–6 months before conception |
| **Rituximab** | NOT RECOMMENDED | Limited data; neonatal B-cell depletion reported; use only if benefit clearly outweighs risk |
| **Eculizumab / Ravulizumab** | CAUTION — LIMITED DATA | Some PNH pregnancy data suggests manageable; discuss risk-benefit; not routinely recommended in MG pregnancy |
| **IVIG** | SAFE | Preferred rescue therapy during pregnancy |
| **Plasmapheresis** | GENERALLY SAFE | Can be used in severe MG during pregnancy; technical challenges but feasible; preferred for MuSK+ crisis |
| **FcRn inhibitors** (efgartigimod, rozanolixizumab, nipocalimab) | NOT RECOMMENDED | FcRn is critical for passive immunity transfer to fetus and maintaining IgG homeostasis; theoretical risk of neonatal hypogammaglobulinaemia; data very limited |

### 4.3 Pre-Conception Planning

**Timing:**
- Achieve stable MG (MMS or better) for at least **6–12 months** before attempting pregnancy
- Complete thymectomy (if indicated) at least **12 months before conception** to maximise disease-modifying benefit and minimise medication requirements during pregnancy
- Perform medication review and switch to pregnancy-safe agents before conception:
  - Switch from MMF to azathioprine
  - Switch from methotrexate: stop 3–6 months before conception
  - Discuss each medication with the treating neurologist

### 4.4 Management During Pregnancy

**Trimester by trimester risk:**
- **First trimester:** Increased risk of MG worsening (immune changes). Major risk period for drug-induced fetal harm.
- **Second trimester:** Many women experience improvement or remission (progesterone immunomodulation)
- **Third trimester:** Variable; some experience worsening. Avoid IV pyridostigmine.
- **Postpartum:** **Highest risk period for MG flare** — in the first 4–8 weeks after delivery. Immunological rebound after pregnancy-related immune tolerance.

**Monitoring:**
- More frequent neurology review (every 4–6 weeks)
- MG severity scores (MG-ADL, QMG) at each visit
- Regular medication level checks (ciclosporin/tacrolimus if used)
- Fetal growth monitoring (especially with ciclosporin/tacrolimus)

### 4.5 Labour and Delivery

**Mode of delivery:**
- **Vaginal delivery preferred** unless standard obstetric indications for Caesarean section
- Second stage of labour may be prolonged due to MG-related skeletal muscle fatigue
- **Forceps or vacuum-assisted delivery** should be available/planned

**Anaesthesia:**
- **Regional anaesthesia (epidural or spinal) is preferred** over general anaesthesia to minimise risks of respiratory complications
- General anaesthesia is significantly more complex in MG patients (variable response to neuromuscular blocking agents; difficult extubation due to muscle weakness)

**CRITICAL — Magnesium sulfate:**
- **Magnesium sulfate is STRICTLY CONTRAINDICATED in MG patients**
- Magnesium is the standard treatment for pre-eclampsia seizure prophylaxis in obstetrics
- In MG, magnesium blocks calcium channels at the NMJ, dramatically impairs neuromuscular transmission, and can precipitate a life-threatening myasthenic crisis
- Alternative anticonvulsant: phenytoin/fosphenytoin or levetiracetam for seizure prophylaxis in MG patients with pre-eclampsia
- All obstetric staff caring for MG patients must be explicitly informed of this contraindication

**Stress dosing:**
- Patients on chronic corticosteroids (prednisolone >5 mg/day for >3 months) are at risk of adrenal insufficiency during the physiological stress of labour
- IV hydrocortisone "stress dosing" should be administered during active labour

**Pyridostigmine during labour:**
- Continue oral pyridostigmine during labour
- If the patient cannot swallow, nasogastric tube administration or IV neostigmine under close supervision may be needed
- Avoid IV pyridostigmine (risk of uterine stimulation)

### 4.6 Neonatal Myasthenia Gravis (Transient Neonatal MG — TNMG)

**Incidence:** Approximately **10–15%** of infants born to mothers with MG (some series report up to 20%)

**Mechanism:** Maternal IgG autoantibodies (anti-AChR, anti-MuSK, or other) cross the placenta (this is normal; maternal IgG is how neonates receive passive immunity). In susceptible infants, these antibodies impair the neonatal NMJ.

**Important note:** Maternal antibody titre does NOT reliably predict whether TNMG will occur or its severity. Even mothers with very low antibody levels or in clinical remission can have affected infants.

**Symptoms** (appear within hours to days of birth):
- Weak, floppy cry
- Poor feeding / weak suck
- Facial weakness
- Generalised hypotonia ("floppy baby")
- Respiratory difficulty (most serious — may require ventilation)
- Ptosis (drooping eyelids)

**Duration:**
- Typically resolves within **2–4 weeks** (rarely up to 3 months)
- Caused by maternal antibody clearance; once maternal antibodies are cleared, the infant's own NMJ function is normal
- No permanent NMJ damage

**Management:**
- All infants of MG mothers should be **monitored in a neonatal unit or NICU-capable facility for at least 3–4 days** after birth
- Treatment if symptomatic: neostigmine (oral or IM) to manage weakness; respiratory support as needed
- Severe cases may require IVIG or exchange transfusion to remove maternal antibodies faster

**Breastfeeding:**
- Compatible drugs: pyridostigmine, prednisolone (at doses <40 mg/day), azathioprine
- **Avoid during breastfeeding:** Mycophenolate mofetil (excreted in milk; potential infant toxicity), methotrexate
- FcRn inhibitors: data lacking; caution advised

### 4.7 Postpartum Management

- **Close monitoring** for the first 8 weeks postpartum (highest flare risk period)
- If the patient was taking pregnancy-safe but non-preferred immunosuppressants (e.g., azathioprine instead of MMF), consider switching back to more effective agents after delivery and once breastfeeding decisions have been made
- Ensure adequate sleep (exhaustion is a potent MG trigger) — practical support in early parenthood is important
- Resume any medications that were paused during pregnancy
- Contraception counselling: importance of effective contraception while on teratogenic drugs (MMF, methotrexate)
